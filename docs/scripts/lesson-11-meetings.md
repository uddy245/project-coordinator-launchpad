# Lesson 11 — Running Meetings That People Don't Hate
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30
**Source chapter:** Handbook Ch 11; reading `docs/lessons/meetings.md`
**Lesson slug:** meetings
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1.

---

### COLD OPEN — The standup that took forty-five minutes (0:00–2:00)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a meeting I sat in early in my career, because it taught me more about meetings than any book did, and because the whole lesson is hiding inside it.

It was a fifteen-minute daily standup that took forty-five minutes every day, for three weeks. I counted. Twelve people attended. Ten of them reported in serial — what I did yesterday, what I'm doing today, what's blocking me — and most of those updates did not need to be heard by the other nine. A side-discussion would erupt about some specific issue, and the facilitator, a well-meaning scrum master, would let it run. Someone would catch up late and ask for a recap. One day a senior stakeholder joined, and the team spent ten minutes explaining context.

Let me do the arithmetic, because the arithmetic is the point. Twelve people, times forty-five minutes, times fifteen working days. That's one hundred and thirty-five person-hours. More than three weeks of one person's work. For updates that could have been a Slack thread of ten minutes a day.

The PM, to her credit, eventually shut it down. She made attendance optional except for the core delivery team — six people. She moved status questions to an async channel. She imposed a hard fifteen-minute cap. The remaining meetings averaged twelve minutes. And nobody — nobody — missed the extra thirty.

---

### THE MOST EXPENSIVE THING (2:00–3:15)

*[Statement slide: "Meetings are the most expensive thing a project does."]*

Here's the framing I want you to carry out of that story. Meetings are the most expensive thing a project does. They spend people's time in large blocks, all at once, and in exchange they produce one of three things: decisions, alignment, or coordination. That's when they're run well.

When they're run badly, they produce something else entirely. Confusion. Resentment. And slightly worse versions of what would have happened anyway, without the meeting at all.

As a coordinator, you are often the one running or supporting the meetings on a project. Which means you are standing right at the lever. You can make those meetings significantly better than most people's defaults — and most people's defaults, as that standup showed, are genuinely bad. This lesson is about how.

---

### KNOW WHAT THE MEETING IS FOR (3:15–5:15)

*[List slide: Decision · Alignment · Working]*

Start here, before anything else. Every useful meeting has exactly one of three purposes, and you should always know which one you're in.

A decision meeting exists to decide something. A choice between options needs to be made. The output is the decision itself — and if no decision gets made, the meeting failed. It's that binary. The people in the room should be the decider, the people whose input the decider needs, and the people who need to hear the reasoning firsthand. Nobody else.

An alignment meeting exists to build shared understanding. A piece of information needs to reach several people in a way that lets them ask questions and clarify. The output is a group that now shares a working model of something. Be honest with yourself here — these are the meetings most often misused, because very often a document would have done the job.

A working meeting exists to do work that genuinely needs several people at once. A requirements workshop. A design session. A retrospective. The output is the work itself — a draft produced, a design agreed, a list of improvements. Working meetings are the most valuable, and frankly the most enjoyable, when they're run well.

Now here's the trap. Status meetings — the most common meeting on any project — are alignment meetings. Their job is shared situational awareness, not decisions. When people try to use a status meeting to decide something, one of two things happens: either the meeting balloons, or the decision gets made badly by a room that was never set up to decide. Remember that. It's the central anti-pattern of this whole lesson.

So before you schedule anything, ask which of the three it is. And if you cannot answer that question — do not schedule it.

---

### AGENDAS THAT ACTUALLY WORK (5:15–7:00)

*[Example slide: bad agenda item vs good agenda item]*

Let's talk about agendas, because most agendas are useless, and the reason they're useless is simple. An agenda is not a list of topics. A list of topics is just a meeting that's going to wander. An agenda is a sequence of outcomes — each one with a time budget and an owner.

Let me show you the difference. Here's a bad agenda item: "Integration vendor discussion." That's it. Four words. It tells the room nothing — not what they're supposed to do, not how long it'll take, not what's at stake.

Now here's a good one. "Decide on integration vendor. Fifteen minutes. Sylvia leads. Options are briefed in the pre-read. Decision needed today so procurement can start by Friday." That single line tells every participant what they're being asked to do, how long it should take, and exactly why it matters. Same topic. Completely different meeting.

And notice that good item referenced a pre-read. Pre-reads are part of the agenda — they're not optional decoration. Any meeting that needs context should have a pre-read sent at least twenty-four hours ahead. Forty-eight is better. If you find yourself burning the first ten minutes of every meeting explaining context, the pre-read is your fix. Pre-reads respect people's time. Meetings that brief in real time do not.

Your specific job, as coordinator, is usually to draft the agenda, circulate it, confirm the pre-reads actually exist, and then — this part gets skipped — adjust the timing afterward based on what really happened. Agendas are living documents. If your standing meeting's agenda hasn't changed in six months, that agenda is ceremonial. It's décor.

---

### FACILITATION, WHICH MATTERS MORE (7:00–9:30)

*[List slide: the core facilitation moves]*

Now the part that matters even more than the agenda. A good agenda run badly is still a bad meeting. But a mediocre agenda run well can still produce real value. Facilitation — the act of steering a conversation toward an outcome — is a genuine skill, and you will be asked to do it whether or not your job description ever mentions it.

Here are the core moves. Memorize them.

Start on time. Waiting for latecomers teaches the whole room that the meeting starts late. Start on time, with whoever is actually there. Latecomers learn.

State the goal explicitly in the first minute. "We need to leave here with a decision on X. We've got thirty minutes." Orient everyone before you begin.

Keep the conversation on topic. When people drift, redirect — politely but firmly. "Helpful point — let's park that for the follow-up and come back to the decision."

Surface dissent. When one person is clearly unconvinced and the room is quietly pretending consensus, name it. "Priya, it looks like you've got a concern — can you say what it is?" Suppressed dissent always comes out later, usually worse. Surfaced dissent gets resolved.

Close with a summary. In the last two minutes, recap what was decided, what actions were agreed, and who owns each one by when. Write them down where people can see them. If the meeting ended without clear actions, it didn't produce value — and you should say so out loud, not hide it.

And respect the time budget. If it was booked for thirty minutes, end at thirty. If the conversation isn't finished, say so and schedule the follow-up. Running over trains the room to treat every meeting time you set as a suggestion.

You won't always be the facilitator. When the PM runs the meeting, you're supporting — capturing actions, watching the clock, occasionally nudging the PM privately when things drift. When you run it yourself, those moves are all yours.

---

### WHEN YOU'RE THE YOUNGEST IN THE ROOM (9:30–11:00)

*[Statement slide: "Frame the moves as service to the room, not control of it."]*

Now I have to be honest about the hard part, because it's the single most common reason coordinators don't do any of this. You're facilitating, and the room is full of people senior to you — sometimes by a decade or more. Every one of those moves I just listed — redirecting drift, surfacing dissent, closing with a summary — is a move that gently constrains what senior people do with their time. The fear that it'll land badly is real. It's not in your head.

A few things that help. First: the moves are far easier when you frame them as service to the room, not control of it. "Helpful point — let's park that for the follow-up" lands completely differently from "can we get back on track." The first treats everyone as a collaborator. The second treats the meeting as your territory. Same intent — different framing, and the framing is everything.

Second: you don't have to do all of them at once. Start with the easiest two — the opening goal-statement and the closing summary. Both feel scripted, both look like preparation rather than assertion. Redirecting drift and surfacing dissent are harder; come to those as you build trust with the room.

Third: you can do the moves on behalf of the PM. If the PM is running the meeting and it drifts, a quiet note — "we've got ten minutes left and three items remaining" — hands the PM the opening to redirect, without you having to do it yourself. You're protecting the meeting's outcomes; the PM is wielding the authority.

And the room learns. The first time you do a real closing summary — decisions, actions, owners, due dates, written where everyone can see them — somebody notices. The second time, people start looking for it. By the fourth or fifth meeting, that summary has become the meeting's natural shape, and you never had to assert anything. You just kept doing the move until the room adopted it.

---

### THE MEETINGS YOU SHOULD PROPOSE CANCELING (11:00–12:30)

*[Statement slide: "Coordinators who remove meetings look thoughtful."]*

Here's a skill almost nobody develops, and it's one of the highest-leverage things you can do. Learn to propose canceling meetings.

When you notice a standing meeting has stopped producing value — attendance is drifting, the discussions are listless, the actions rarely materialize — you can propose to the PM that it be restructured or scrapped. But the way you propose it matters enormously. The wrong way is "this meeting is useless." The right way is: "I've noticed the last four weekly syncs have produced mostly status updates rather than decisions. Could we move status to async and replace the standing slot with a fortnightly working session?"

See the difference? You're not attacking the meeting. You're proposing a design change. That's a completely different conversation, and it's one a PM can actually say yes to.

And the payoff is enormous. Every canceled hour returns to the team as work time. There's a reputational payoff too, and it's worth naming plainly: coordinators who add meetings look busy. Coordinators who remove meetings look thoughtful. You want to be the second kind.

One caution, though — and this is a trap I want you to avoid. When your PM forwards you a meeting setup and asks what you think, your job is to surface what's broken and recommend specific changes. It is not to quietly build a finished v2 invite, write the pre-read template, and offer to send it out yourself. The PM owns the meeting and owns the relationship with the room. Audit. Recommend. Then let the PM run the recommendation through the team. The doers have to own the change for it to actually land.

---

### TOMORROW, SPECIFICALLY (12:30–13:55)

*[List slide: workbook brief + close]*

So here's your work for this lesson, and it's all things you can do this week with the calendar you already have.

Look at next week's calendar. For each meeting, write down which of the three types it is — decision, alignment, or working. Any meeting you can't categorize is a meeting worth questioning. Then review the agenda of the next meeting you'll support, and check each item has an outcome, a time, and an owner — that's usually a ten-minute fix. For the next meeting you facilitate, practice just the closing summary: the last two minutes where you recap decisions and actions and write them visibly. Most meetings end without one. Yours can be the exception. And finally, find one standing meeting that's costing more time than it's producing value, and think through how you'd propose changing it — as a design proposal, in your next one-on-one, not as a critique.

*[Back on camera, slower]*

The reason I started with that forty-five-minute standup is that it's the whole lesson in miniature. Nobody in that room was incompetent. The scrum master meant well. The updates were real. And it still burned one hundred and thirty-five hours that could have been a Slack thread. The meeting wasn't bad because the people were bad. It was bad because nobody was minding the design of it. That's the gap you get to fill. Most of the time, the most valuable thing you can do for a meeting is make it shorter, sharper, or make it disappear.
