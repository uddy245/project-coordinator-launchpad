# Lesson 12 — Minutes, Actions, and Decisions: The Written Record

### COLD OPEN — The minutes that settled a dispute eighteen months later (0:00–2:00)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a phone call I got in late 2015, because it taught me what minutes are actually for — and it's not what most people think.

A project I had coordinated back in 2014 came back to me through a legal colleague at the firm. I was on another project by then; I'd run two more since. A vendor was claiming they'd delivered their full scope and were entitled to their final payment. Our former client was claiming that two specific items had never been delivered, and were part of the original agreement. The dispute was headed for a formal claim.

My colleague asked me whether I remembered what had been agreed at the kickoff — specifically, whether those two items had ever been discussed. I did not remember. It had been a year and a half. But I had written the minutes, and I had written them carefully.

I pulled them from the archive. The kickoff minutes, from March 2014, contained one clear line: "Vendor to deliver A, B, and C. Items D and E confirmed as out of scope per procurement, to be considered under separate engagement if required." Those minutes had gone to every attendee at the time — including the vendor's own lead. Nobody had objected. The minutes, accepted by silence, were the record.

The dispute resolved within a week. The vendor accepted the items had been out of scope. The claim was withdrawn. My colleague told me, in a note I still have somewhere, that the case had been "closed by good paperwork."

That's the lesson hiding in this whole chapter. Minutes are not bureaucratic afterthoughts. They are the durable record of what a project decided, agreed, and committed to — referenced in disputes, audits, closure reviews, and scope arguments months or years later. Done well, they protect the project and the people in it. Done badly, they create confusion and dispute. This lesson is about writing them well.

### MINUTES ARE NOT TRANSCRIPTS (2:00–3:40)

*[Statement slide, then before/after example.]*

Here is the single most important thing I can tell you, and the thing I got wrong on my own first day: minutes are not transcripts. Transcripts are almost never useful.

A transcript is a wall of who-said-what. It records that the meeting happened. It does not tell you what the meeting produced. Six months later, nobody reads a transcript, and nobody can find anything in one. The work of minutes is judgment — deciding what mattered and recording only that.

Let me show you the difference. Same meeting, two records. The transcript version reads: "Sarah said she thought we should look at the licensing again. James pushed back, said it was expensive. There was a long discussion. Eventually people seemed to settle on Option B. Someone mentioned the vendor analysis was still outstanding." That tells a future reader almost nothing they can act on or rely on.

The real minutes from that exact meeting read: "Decision: proceed with Option B (Option A rejected due to licensing cost). Action: Priya to complete vendor analysis and report back by May 15 — owner Priya, due May 15, status open." Shorter. Sharper. Uncontestable. That's the entire craft in two cards. Everything else this lesson covers is in service of getting you from the first version to the second.

---

### THE THREE THINGS MINUTES MUST CAPTURE (3:40–5:40)

*[List slide: Decisions · Actions · Key context.]*

Minutes are structured records with exactly three components. Learn these three and you'll never stare at a blank notes page again.

One, decisions — things the meeting settled. A decision entry says what was decided, by whom if attribution matters, and when. If a decision was unanimous, say so. If there was dissent, note it, with the name of the dissenting party. If a decision was deferred, say that explicitly, with the date of the next conversation. A decision that isn't written down didn't happen, as far as the record is concerned.

Two, actions — things someone committed to do. An action entry says what will be done, who owns it, and by when. And here's the rule that does the most work: every action has exactly one owner. "The team will investigate" is not an action. It's a wish. "Priya will investigate X and report back by May 15" — that's an action. One name. One date. When you write "the team," you've assigned the work to nobody, and nobody is exactly who will do it.

Three, key context — and only when necessary. Sometimes a decision needs a single sentence to stay intelligible later. "Decision: proceed with Option B, noting Option A was rejected due to licensing cost." That context is minimal, and it exists for one reason — to make the record interpretable six months from now, to a reader who wasn't in the room.

Everything else — discussion, side remarks, compliments, complaints, the rehashing of context everybody already knew — does not belong in minutes. And leaving it out is not an omission. It is a professional judgment about what the record is for.

### THE FORMAT THAT SCALES (5:40–7:00)

*[List slide: Header · Decisions · Actions · Notes.]*

A consistent format makes minutes easier to write and easier to read. The one I've used for years is simple, and it never changes from meeting to meeting — which is the point.

At the top of the document: meeting name, date, attendees present and absent, agenda items. Then three sections, each with a clear heading.

Decisions — a list, each item one to three sentences. Clear, attributable, dated. Actions — a table, with columns for the action, the owner, the due date, and the status. Status starts as "open" on every new action. And notes — only when necessary, brief context on specific items for future readers.

No narrative prose. No "the team discussed" entries. If something is worth recording, it is a decision, an action, or a piece of structured context — and it goes in its section. Nothing floats. The format does half the thinking for you, because when you're capturing live and you hear a commitment, your hand already knows which column it goes in.

### THE WRITING DISCIPLINE (7:00–9:30)

*[List slide: Capture live · Send fast · Consistent status · Attribute carefully · Resolve before sending · Read once.]*

Six disciplines separate competent minutes from mediocre ones. None of them are hard. All of them are habits.

Write in real time, not after. Notes you take during the meeting are always better than notes you reconstruct afterward. Reconstruction introduces errors — you misremember who said what, you conflate two discussions, you miss details. If you capture structured notes live, decisions and actions in their own columns from the start, the minutes almost write themselves.

Send within twenty-four hours, ideally same-day. Minutes that arrive three days later are minutes nobody reads, because the meeting has already faded. Same-day is the gold standard. Next-morning is acceptable. More than forty-eight hours is a warning sign about your process.

Use consistent language for status. Actions are open, in progress, completed, or dropped. Decisions are decided, deferred, or reopened. That consistency is what lets minutes be searched and tracked across months. Invent new words each week and you lose that.

Attribute carefully. When you write "the team agreed," you are stating the room was unanimous. When you write "the sponsor approved," you are stating the sponsor exercised decision authority. These words have weight. Do not use them loosely. If three people raised concerns, the room was not unanimous, and the minutes must not say it was — that's exactly the buried disagreement that surfaces unpleasantly later.

Resolve ambiguity by asking before sending. If your notes have an unresolved question — you weren't sure what was decided, or who owned an action — resolve it with a quick message to the PM before the minutes go out. Minutes that go out with "TBD" entries signal incomplete meetings. They land worse than minutes that take another half-day to ship.

And read once before sending. Catch the typos, the wrong dates, the ambiguous sentence. You will catch at least one of each, every document. Ninety seconds. Worth it.

### TONE AND REGISTER (9:30–11:00)

*[Example slide: the contentious meeting, before and after.]*

Minutes are formal documents. Standard register — no contractions, no humor, no editorializing, no emotion. Even when the meeting was contentious, the minutes are spare and neutral. They record what happened without evaluating it.

This matters because minutes are read by people who weren't in the room, who may have different sensitivities than the participants — and by people months later, who've forgotten the context entirely. Neutral phrasing holds up. Evaluative phrasing does not.

Here's the example I always use. A meeting where a senior stakeholder argued forcefully for a position that was eventually rejected. In your private notes, you might scribble: "Bauer pushed back on this heavily." Now — do not put that in the minutes. That phrasing invites her to say, "that's not how I would have characterized my contribution," and now you're litigating the record instead of the work.

What you write instead is this: "X was discussed. After review of the alternatives, the committee decided to proceed with Y. Concerns raised by Bauer regarding the integration risk have been added to the risk register." Neutral. Factual. The decision is captured. The dissent is acknowledged but not editorialized. The follow-up exists. And nobody is embarrassed by the record. That's not softness — that's precision. And precision is its own defense. A line that's plainly factual invites no pushback, because it's recorded in language the participants themselves would have used.

### FOLLOWING UP ON ACTIONS (11:00–12:15)

*[List slide: the action tracker.]*

Minutes are not the end of the job. The actions inside them have to be tracked to closure, and that's a separate discipline.

Maintain an action tracker — a running list of every open action across every meeting, with owners and due dates. This is separate from the minutes, and more useful than them, because it's a living operational view rather than a static record. The minutes are the snapshot. The tracker is the movie.

Before each meeting, review it. Which actions from last time are complete? Which are overdue? Which are coming due? The first agenda item of most standing meetings should be a thirty-second review of last meeting's actions. "Four actions open from last week, three on track, one overdue — Priya, can you update us on the vendor analysis?" That habit does four things at once: it makes actions real rather than ceremonial, it gives slippage a place to be visible, it reinforces that commitments matter, and it saves you from the passive-aggressive dynamic where actions are assigned, forgotten, and rediscovered three meetings later.

One refinement. Chase overdue actions privately when you can. "Priya — just checking on the vendor analysis, due yesterday; any obstacles?" lands better than a public callout. Public reminders are for chronic cases, or when something genuinely needs to be escalated.

### WHEN THE MINUTES ARE WRONG (12:15–13:15)

*[Statement slide.]*

Occasionally someone will challenge your minutes. "I didn't say that." "We didn't agree to that." "That's not what was decided." Your posture in that moment is the whole thing.

Neutral and evidence-based. Not defensive, not apologetic, just factual. "My notes from the meeting record this — can you tell me what you think should be corrected, and I'll compare against my notes and reach out to others if needed?" If your notes are wrong, correct them and reissue with a brief, transparent note. If your notes are right but someone remembers differently, check with another attendee. If the record holds, hold it. If there's genuine disagreement, the PM or the meeting chair rules.

What you never do is quietly revise the minutes to match someone's later preference, with no correction note. Minutes are legal-ish documents. Silent revision is worse than the original error. The same goes when you're asked to review someone else's draft — a junior coordinator's first board minutes, say. Audit, don't rewrite. Identify what needs to change and let the author make the changes. Never invent facts to fill a gap. Wrong content in a durable record is brutally hard to roll back.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:15–14:20)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close.]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Pull up the last three sets of minutes you produced, and check each against the format — are decisions and actions captured separately? Is there an owner and a date on every action? Is there narrative prose you could have cut? Then open your action tracker, or build one if you don't have one, sort by due date, and ping the owners of anything overdue today. And for your next meeting, practice live capture — structured decision and action columns from the start.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

The reason I started with that phone call eighteen months after the project closed is that it's the whole lesson in miniature. The task that day in 2014 was "take the kickoff minutes." The job was to write one line so precise, so factual, and so plainly true that a year and a half later it could close a legal dispute in a week. Everyone in that room could have transcribed the conversation. The work — the thing I was actually paid for — was the judgment about what mattered, written down once, cleanly, in language nobody could contest. That gap, between the transcript and the record, is the entire craft. Live on the right side of it.

See you in the workbook.
