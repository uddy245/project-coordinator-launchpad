# Lesson 4 — The Project Lifecycle: How Projects Actually Happen

### COLD OPEN — The project that died in the gap (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a project that failed in a way that taught me more about lifecycle than any textbook ever did. It was a compliance reporting platform for a financial services firm — about eight months planned, a mix of internal development and vendor integration. I was a senior coordinator, hoping to step up to PM. The official PM was a man named Robert, who'd been running it for six months before I joined.

By the time I arrived, planning was finishing. There was a signed charter. A detailed work breakdown. A Gantt chart with four hundred line items. A risk register with fifty-two risks. A stakeholder analysis with a RACI. Budget approved. Resources named. Kickoff held. By every formal measure, the project was in good shape.

Eight weeks into execution, it was in serious trouble.

Nothing dramatic had happened. No single decision was catastrophically wrong. The developers were working. The vendor was showing up. The steering committee was meeting. But the work wasn't producing what it was supposed to produce, the team didn't share an understanding of what the solution actually was, and the business analyst was rewriting the requirements for the third time — requirements that had been signed off two months earlier.

I spent a week trying to figure out what had gone wrong. The answer, when I understood it, was this: the planning had been done as a deliverable, not as a process. The artifacts existed. The thinking had not happened.

The risk register had fifty-two items, but none had a named owner who'd actually agreed to mitigate them. The work breakdown was detailed, but Robert had built it alone, from his own understanding of the scope — not with the people who'd do the work. The RACI was filled in, but the people it marked as "Accountable" hadn't, in most cases, confirmed they understood what they were accountable for.

The project had gone *through* planning. It hadn't *emerged* from it ready to execute. The two phases never actually connected. We ended up re-planning while executing — and the thing eventually delivered two months late and twenty-five percent over budget. That project taught me what a lifecycle actually is, and what it is not.

### A LIFECYCLE IS A SEQUENCE OF STATES (2:10–3:30)

*[Slide: "states, not deliverables"]*

Here's the lesson hiding inside that story. A lifecycle is not a sequence of deliverables. It is a sequence of *states* the project passes through, each of which prepares it for the next.

The deliverables — the charter, the plan, the closure report — are markers. They are evidence that a state has been reached. But if you produce the deliverable without actually reaching the state, you've done ceremony, not work. And the next phase will expose what you skipped — sometimes gently, sometimes catastrophically.

That's the whole frame for this lesson. I'll walk you through the textbook map, because it's genuinely useful. But the real value is understanding what each phase is *for*, what it feels like when it's working, what it looks like when it's not, and where your moves as a coordinator actually matter.

---

### THE TEXTBOOK MAP, BRIEFLY (3:30–5:05)

*[List slide: initiation · planning · execution · closure]*

Every major framework teaches some version of a four- or five-phase lifecycle. The words vary; the substance is remarkably consistent. The version most people use is: initiation, planning, execution, monitoring and controlling, closure. But execution and monitoring run in parallel, so in practice people think of it as four — initiate, plan, execute, close.

Initiation is where you decide whether the project should happen at all. The business case, the sponsor, a high-level scope, formal authorization. It ends with a signed charter that says: this project exists, this is what it's for, this person is in charge, this is the boundary.

Planning is where you figure out how you're going to do the thing you've been authorized to do. Scope gets detailed, a work breakdown is built, a schedule developed, risks analyzed, the team formed. It ends with a set of plans — and with readiness to begin.

Execution is where you do the work. The team builds the thing. It's the longest phase, and where most of the money is spent. Monitoring and controlling runs right through it — the ongoing discipline of tracking against plan and correcting drift.

And closure is where you finish. Deliverables accepted, the solution transitioned to operations, contracts closed, lessons captured, the project formally ended.

That's the map. As a scaffold, it's useful. As a description of how projects actually happen, it's incomplete in three ways.

---

### WHAT THE TEXTBOOK MISSES (5:05–6:40)

*[List slide: overlap · uneven · alignment]*

First, the phases overlap, and not decoratively — they genuinely bleed into each other. Planning starts before initiation is formally done, because you can't write a charter intelligently with no high-level planning. Execution starts while planning is still finalizing. Closure planning starts months in advance. You never actually *finish* one phase before starting the next. You only shift the center of gravity.

Second, the phases are uneven. Initiation might take two weeks. Planning, two months. Execution, six. Closure, three weeks. Drawing them as four equal boxes hides the truth — that most of the money, most of the risk, and most of your daily work lives in execution, and the other phases are thin bookends that are easy to skimp on.

Third — and this is the most important thing I'll say all lesson — phases are not just about time. They're about *shared understanding*. Initiation isn't complete when the charter is signed. It's complete when the sponsor, the PM, the stakeholders, and the delivery team all share the same understanding of why the project exists and where its boundaries are. A signed charter with a misaligned team is the artifact without the phase. A planning phase that ends with a signed plan the team doesn't believe is worse than no plan at all — because it creates false confidence. A lot of what looks like bad execution is actually bad planning, camouflaged by correct-looking documents.

### INITIATION: SHOULD THIS EXIST? (6:40–8:20)

*[Def slide: the four things good initiation makes clear]*

Let me take the phases one at a time, through a coordinator's eyes.

Initiation is the phase you'll see least — it usually happens before you're assigned. But its quality shapes everything that follows. Good initiation produces a project where four things are clear: why the project exists, who's accountable for its success, what's in scope and what's not, and what success looks like.

Bad initiation muddles one or more of those. The project exists, but nobody can say *why* without vague words like "transformation." The sponsor's named on paper but isn't actually accountable in practice. The scope boundary is soft, full of "to be determined." Success is defined as an output — *we'll deliver the system* — instead of an outcome — *we'll cut reporting time by forty percent* — which means nobody can ever say whether the project succeeded.

You can diagnose this in your first few days. Ask three questions. *Why does this project exist?* — and see if different people give you the same answer. *Who's our sponsor, and what's their stake?* — and see if you get a coherent picture. *What does success look like?* — and see if the answer is something concrete you could actually measure. Consistent and concrete means initiation was done well. Vague or contested means it wasn't — and one of your early contributions is to quietly surface that gap and advocate, through the PM, for closing it before it metastasizes.

---

### PLANNING: THE PHASE DONE BADLY MOST OFTEN (8:20–10:05)

*[Statement slide: "a plan the team believes"]*

If initiation is the phase you see least, planning is the phase you'll see done *badly* most often.

Planning is where the rubber of the authorized project meets the road of actual delivery. And here's the entire game, in one phrase: the output of planning is not a document. It's a plan *that the team believes*. A plan that's written but not believed is a piece of paper. A plan that's believed is a shared map. The difference in outcomes is enormous.

In a good planning phase, the scope emerges from real conversations with the people who'll do the work — the document is downstream of the talking. The work breakdown is *surfaced* by the team in a workshop, not invented by the PM over a weekend. The schedule is negotiated against real resource constraints, not wished into existence. The risk register is built by getting people to genuinely imagine things going wrong — *what worries you? what's gone wrong on projects like this?* — and the risks that surface are specific, named, and owned.

The bad version is the one from my opening story. The PM builds the plan in isolation under pressure to "start real work." The risks come from a generic template — *scope creep, resource availability* — with no owners. The RACI gets glanced at and signed. The plan exists, it's signed, it looks professional. And then execution starts, and within weeks it's clear the planning didn't happen — the planning *artifacts* happened.

Your job in planning is to run the ground game. You schedule the workshops and get the right people in the room. You capture the outputs as living working documents, not minutes. And here's one specific move that matters enormously: you keep the list of questions that *couldn't* be answered during planning — the unclear dependency, the unconfirmed constraint — captured with owners and dates, so they don't get waved away with optimistic language. You're often the keeper of that list. It's a small thing that makes planning honest.

---

### EXECUTION: THE LONG MIDDLE (10:05–11:35)

*[Def slide: execution going well vs going badly]*

Execution is where most of the project actually happens, and where you'll spend most of your time.

It's the longest, most continuous phase — and it's where the quality of initiation and planning becomes visible. If those were good, the team knows why they're doing what they're doing and can execute against a plan they believe. If either was bad, execution becomes an extended recovery exercise — continuously discovering things that should've been discovered earlier.

When execution's going well, the project has a steady heartbeat. Weekly status is *boring*, because nothing new is catching fire. Risks close about as fast as they emerge. The team isn't heroic — they're paced. When it's going badly, the heartbeat is arrhythmic. Decisions pile up. Risks multiply faster than they retire. The team is heroic — pulling long hours to catch up — which is a *bad* sign, because heroics mask problems and aren't sustainable. And one phrase to hold onto: execution is not just "doing the work." It's doing the work *while continuously verifying* it produces what the plan said it would, and adjusting when it doesn't. The adjusting is as important as the doing.

This is the richest phase for you. You're the meeting engine, the information router, the keeper of the record, the early-warning system, the relationship maintenance function. And, if you're paying attention, you're learning to think like the PM — watching how they make the hundred small decisions that constitute running a project, so that one day you can make them yourself.

---

### CLOSURE: THE PHASE MOST PROJECTS DO WORST (11:35–12:45)

*[Statement slide: closure is a cost deferred, not a free saving]*

Closure is the phase most projects do worst — and the one where a good coordinator can make a disproportionate contribution.

It's done badly for three reasons. By the time you reach it, people are tired and want to move on. The organization's already treating the next project as the priority, so resources have been partly reassigned. And closure is rarely rewarded — nobody gets a bonus for a well-documented handover. The incentives are against doing it well.

Doing it well anyway means: deliverables formally accepted by the people who'll own them — not a rubber stamp. The solution genuinely transitioned to operations, with training and support paths agreed. Contracts closed. The team disbanded thoughtfully, with contributions documented for review time. And lessons captured — not as box-checking, but honestly. One specific note: if you do nothing else in closure, run a proper lessons-learned session, and reframe the question. Not "what should we do differently" — ask "what surprised us?" Surprises are concrete, memorable, and they contain the real learning.

The coordinator who can drive a thorough closure — while the PM's attention has already drifted to the next thing — is doing something most projects never get. You won't be thanked for it at the time. You'll be thanked for it, indirectly, for years.

---

### WORK THE MODEL BACKWARD (12:45–13:45)

*[Example slide: a project struggling in execution, then the four questions]*

Let me leave you with a mental model I've found more useful than the phase diagram. Think of the project as passing through four questions. *Should this exist?* — that's initiation. *How will it be done?* — planning. *Are we doing it?* — execution. *Is it finished?* — closure. Each question genuinely answered before the next — not on paper, in shared understanding.

Here's the move that's worth more than anything else in this lesson. When a project feels like it's struggling, ask which of those four questions hasn't actually been answered. A project struggling in execution usually has an unresolved planning question. A project struggling in planning usually has an unresolved initiation question. The visible symptom is in execution, but the source is almost always earlier. And the intervention that works is at the source, not the symptom. Working the model backward is the fastest way I know to find the real problem.

And this holds across methodologies. Waterfall, agile, hybrid — the artifacts and cadence differ, but the four questions are the same. *Has the project answered them?* applies whether you're producing long-form plans or running sprints.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:45–14:30)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. In the workbook, you'll take your current project and answer the four questions honestly — which has it actually answered, and which has it not. Be specific. Does the team really share an understanding of *why*? Is the plan believed, or just signed? Then you'll find the last phase transition your project went through and ask whether it was clean — did the previous phase actually finish, and was there a readiness check? Finally, you'll propose a lightweight readiness check ahead of your next milestone, framed as help, not critique.

*[Back on camera, slower]*

The reason I started with Robert's project is that it's the whole lesson in miniature. They had every artifact a planning phase is supposed to produce. They had not reached the state those artifacts were supposed to mark. The phases never connected — and execution spent eight weeks paying for it. A lifecycle is not the documents. It's the states. Your job is to know which state the project is actually in, not which one the paperwork claims. Learn to tell the difference, and you'll catch the failures that everyone else only sees once they've already happened.

See you in the workbook.
