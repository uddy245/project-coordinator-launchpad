# Lesson 18 — When to Chase, and When to Let Go

**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1.

### COLD OPEN — The action I chased seventeen times (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with the most embarrassing thing I'll tell you in this whole program. I once tracked a single action that I nudged seventeen times over three months before I accepted that it was never going to happen.

It was a deliverable from a senior subject-matter expert on a client team. They'd committed to it, out loud, in a workshop. It was a soft dependency for a later workstream — not on the critical path, but the kind of thing you tell yourself you should stay on top of.

At chase number four, my PM looked over and said, "still at it?" and laughed, kindly. At chase number eight, he suggested I might want to reframe it as a risk rather than an action. I ignored him — because I believed, wrongly, that my job was to close the actions on the tracker, and that closing one meant getting the thing delivered. At chase number twelve, the SME stopped replying to me entirely. At chase number fifteen, I went to his manager. A modest escalation. It produced a polite response and no deliverable.

At chase number seventeen, on a Wednesday afternoon with a hundred other things on my list, I finally sat down and asked the question I should have asked in week two: does this action actually matter — and if it does, what should I do that's different from nudging?

It did not matter very much. The workstream had worked around the missing input months earlier. My chasing was ceremonial at that point — an accumulated habit, not purposeful work. I closed the action with a note that the deliverable hadn't been produced and was no longer required. I felt, in equal measure, embarrassed and relieved.

This lesson is the thing I wish I'd known at chase number two.

---

### WHAT THIS LESSON IS ACTUALLY ABOUT (2:10–3:20)

*[Statement slide.]*

Chasing — reminding someone of a commitment they haven't met, in the hope they'll now meet it — is a real part of the job. Most coordinators do it reflexively, without thinking. The ones who do the job well decide *when* to chase, when to escalate, and when to let go. They do better work and spend less energy doing it.

So this lesson isn't about how to nudge harder. It's about judgment. It's about the moment before you send the nudge, when you decide whether sending it is the right move at all. Get that decision right, habitually, and you'll free up a surprising amount of your week — and you'll stop quietly damaging relationships by chasing people for things nobody needs anymore.

---

### THE SEVENTEEN-NUDGE TRAP (3:20–4:50)

*[Statement slide: "you're closing the tracker, not the loop."]*

Let me name the trap precisely, because you'll feel it before you see it. It is very easy to chase an action far past the point of usefulness. You nudge once, twice, four times. A habit forms. The nudging becomes ceremonial — an accumulated reflex rather than purposeful work.

Here's the tell. You're chasing to close the action on the tracker — not because the deliverable still matters. Those are two completely different motivations, and new coordinators confuse them constantly. They believe their job is to close actions, and that closing one means getting the thing delivered.

It isn't. Your job is to keep the project's record honest and its live work moving. Sometimes that means getting the deliverable. And sometimes — this is the part that takes a while to accept — it means recognising the deliverable is dead, and saying so.

A chase is useful when the commitment is still live and reasonable prompting can produce action. It's wasted when the commitment is dead and prompting won't revive it. The whole skill is distinguishing those two states. And the good news is the signals are knowable.

---

### SIGNALS THE CHASE IS STILL USEFUL (4:50–6:40)

*[List slide: Responsive · Cause resolving · Still needed · Producing movement]*

Four signals tell you a chase is still worth sending.

One: the person is responsive. They reply to your nudges — even if the reply is just "sorry, still on it." Responsiveness means the commitment is still alive in their mind. They haven't written it off; they're just behind.

Two: the slippage has a cause that's resolving. They were on leave, and now they're back. A cross-team blocker has lifted. A temporary constraint is clearing on its own. The thing that stopped them is going away.

Three: the deliverable is still needed. The downstream workstream still cares. The milestone still depends on it. The project still wants the output. This one you have to actually check — don't assume.

Four: gentle prompting produces movement. After a nudge, *something* happens. A partial response. A revised estimate. A request for help. The loop is producing progress, even if it's slow.

When these hold, chase. You're doing exactly what the job needs: closing a feedback loop the person hasn't closed themselves. That's valuable, and it's not ceremonial.

---

### SIGNALS YOU SHOULD STOP (6:40–8:25)

*[List slide: Gone silent · Circumstances changed · No longer needed · No movement]*

And four signals tell you to stop.

One: the person has stopped responding. Not to one nudge — across several, over time. That's a strong signal the commitment is dead in their mind, whatever they originally said. Silence across multiple channels is an answer.

Two: circumstances have changed. They've moved roles. Priorities shifted. The reason they committed in the first place no longer applies. You're chasing a commitment that the world has already retired.

Three: the deliverable is no longer needed. The downstream work has moved on. The output, if it arrived today, would be historical rather than useful. You'd be collecting it for the archive, not for the project.

Four: chasing produces no movement. You've nudged repeatedly and nothing changes. You're spending real energy on a dead loop.

When these hold, chasing is wasted. And "wasted" is the right word — it's not neutral. Every ceremonial nudge costs you a little time and costs the relationship a little goodwill. When the signals say stop, something else is needed.

---

### THREE THINGS TO DO INSTEAD (8:25–10:25)

*[List slide: Escalate · Reclassify · Let it go]*

When a chase has run its course, three paths are open — and choosing the right one for the situation is the heart of the skill.

Escalate. If the deliverable is *still needed* and the person isn't producing it, the next rung of the escalation ladder may fit — their manager, or your PM, for an intervention. Reserve this for items that still matter. Escalation spends senior attention; don't spend it on something redundant.

Reclassify. Close the action as dropped, note why, and convert the underlying need into a risk or an issue. Something like: "Risk — X input not forthcoming from the named owner; mitigation — Y team to proceed with current information and accept lower confidence in the output; owner — Y lead." That's honest, and it preserves the record instead of pretending the action is still in flight.

And let it go. If the deliverable is no longer needed, close the action and move on. Don't leave dead actions on the tracker. They accumulate into clutter that hides what's actually live.

That last one is the hardest. Closing an undelivered action can feel like admitting failure. It isn't. It's accurate record-keeping. The real failure is a tracker full of nominally-open items nobody expects to close — because when everything looks open, nothing tells you what's actually at risk.

---

### PROPORTION CUTS BOTH WAYS (10:25–11:30)

*[Statement slide.]*

One principle sits underneath all three moves: match the move to the item's actual importance.

A soft, off-critical-path dependency whose downstream work has already moved on does not warrant a nuclear escalation to someone's manager and the sponsor. That spends senior attention and goodwill on a non-issue — and worse, it misreports a non-problem as a crisis. People stop trusting your escalations when you escalate things that didn't need it.

But proportion cuts both ways. A genuinely critical, still-needed deliverable warrants more than a polite weekly nudge that you send and forget. If it matters, act like it matters. Under-reacting to a real problem is just as much a failure of judgment as over-reacting to a fake one.

---

### THE CHASE, WRITTEN WELL (11:30–13:05)

*[Example slide: weak chase vs. strong chase, then the five principles.]*

Now — when you *do* decide to chase, the message itself is a small professional artifact. Five principles.

Be specific. "Just checking on the vendor summary for next week's steering-committee pack — do you have a view on timing?" Not "following up." Specific is easy to act on; vague gets ignored.

Be polite but not apologetic. "Just checking" is fine. "So sorry to bother you, I know you're super busy, but…" is not. You are doing your job. Don't apologise for it.

Make it easy to respond. "Should I plan on Friday, or would Monday work better?" A yes-or-no or either-or question gets a faster answer than an open one.

Offer help. "Happy to grab fifteen minutes to walk through it if useful." Sometimes the block is something they'd be embarrassed to name, and offering help makes it easy to ask.

And vary the channel occasionally. Three silent emails? Try Slack, or a short in-person check-in. Varying the channel can break inertia without feeling like pressure.

The tone throughout is patient professionalism. You are not annoyed — you are tracking. And if you *do* feel annoyed, wait before you send. The annoyance leaks through. It hurts the relationship without helping the deliverable.

---

### THE MOVE THAT'S BEING TESTED (13:05–13:55)

*[Statement slide.]*

Let me give you the exact situation this lesson is built around, because it's the one that separates the coordinators who get this from the ones who don't.

You've nudged an action repeatedly. The owner is now silent. It's a soft dependency, and the downstream team has already worked around it.

The skilled move is not a seventh ceremonial nudge. It's not a nuclear escalation of a redundant item. And it's not leaving it open because closing feels like failure. The skilled move is to recognise the chase is dead, let it go — close the action with an honest note — and convert any genuine residue, like an unconfirmed assumption the downstream work now rests on, into a named risk with a mitigation and an owner. Read the signals. Then match the move to them.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:55–14:30)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close.]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Open your action tracker and find the three oldest open actions. For each one, ask three questions: is it still live, is the person still responsive, and is the deliverable still needed? For any that fail, close it — converted to a risk, escalated, or simply dropped, with a note. Then notice whether closing it feels like relief or like failure. That feeling tells you something about your relationship with the tracker.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

I told you about chase number seventeen because the whole lesson lives inside it. Every nudge after about chase number four was me serving the tracker instead of the project. The skill wasn't sending a better nudge. It was the judgment to stop, and to be honest in the record about why. Learn to make that call early — at chase number two, not seventeen — and you'll do better work, with less energy, and people will trust you more for it.

See you in the workbook.
