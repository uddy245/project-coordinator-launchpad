# Lesson 24 — Using AI as a Coordinator Without Losing Your Edge
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30
**Source chapter:** Handbook Ch 24; reading `docs/lessons/using-ai.md`
**Lesson slug:** using-ai
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1.

---

### COLD OPEN — The coordinator who didn't write his own emails (0:00–1:55)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a junior coordinator I worked with a couple of years ago. Bright, technical, early twenties. He showed me his workflow one afternoon, and he was proud of it — genuinely, visibly proud. Every email he sent, every status report, every set of minutes was drafted by an AI tool and then lightly edited. He told me it made him twice as productive as the people around him.

I asked him how it was going. He said it was going well. The outputs were good. His PM hadn't complained. He was getting more done in less time. All of which was true.

Then I asked him a second question. Not "is it working?" — but "what are you learning?"

He paused. And I watched him understand what I was getting at, in real time. The outputs were competent. But he wasn't, in any deep sense, producing them. He was curating the AI's output. The skill of writing a crisp status report, of taking structured minutes, of drafting a clean change request — the skills that actually build a career — those weren't being practiced. He was shipping results while his craft sat exactly where it had been six months earlier.

That conversation has stayed with me, because it names the thing that makes AI dangerous in this role precisely because it's so useful. These tools are a real productivity multiplier. They can also, used without care, substitute for the very practice that builds the professional you're trying to become. This lesson is about using them well — and keeping your edge while you do.

---

### THE DOUBLE EDGE (1:55–3:05)

*[Statement slide: "useful and dangerous for the same reason"]*

Let me be clear about my position, because it isn't the obvious one in either direction.

I'm not here to ban AI. A blanket ban is what someone does when they're frightened of a tool they don't understand, and it throws out something genuinely valuable. But I'm also not here to tell you to let it write your work. That's the rubber-stamp position, and it quietly hollows out your career while making you feel fast.

The honest position is harder than either. AI is useful and dangerous for the same reason: it produces competent text at volume, instantly. That's a gift when the text is mechanics. It's a trap when the text is meaning. The whole craft of using AI well is learning, reliably, which one you're holding. So let me give you both halves — what it's good at, and what it is not.

---

### WHAT AI IS GENUINELY GOOD AT (3:05–4:55)

*[List slide: Drafting · Summarising · Rephrasing · Extraction · Explanation · Ideation]*

Start with the good, because it's real and you should use it.

For a coordinator's work, today's tools are substantively useful for the mechanics — the production of text at volume. Drafting: first cuts of emails, status narratives, minutes from rough notes, change-request descriptions. The drafts are rarely final, but they save you from the blank page, which is where procrastination lives. Summarising: condensing a long document, a transcript, a forty-message thread into something you can brief someone on quickly. Rephrasing: taking a draft you wrote and producing variants in different tones, lengths, or registers — useful when you're writing to an audience you don't know well. Structured extraction: pulling action items from a transcript, risk items from a requirements doc — tedious work it does faster than you can. Explanation: helping you understand a framework or a method you're not familiar with. And ideation: generating options to prime your thinking — possible risks for a workshop, possible questions for an interview.

Used for these things, AI is a multiplier. You get more done, and the time you save can be reinvested into the higher-judgment work that AI doesn't do. That last part is the whole point — so hold onto it.

---

### WHAT AI IS NOT GOOD AT (4:55–6:50)

*[List slide: Context · Judgment · Reading the room · Uncomfortable truth]*

Now the other edge. The same tools are consistently weak at exactly the things that make you a professional.

Context specific to your project. AI doesn't know the politics of your steering committee, the history of your vendor relationship, the unspoken tension between two business units. Output produced without that context is generic, and sometimes wrong in ways that are immediately obvious to anyone who knows the situation.

Judgment under ambiguity. Should you escalate this? Should you push back on the PM? What's the right RAG rating this week? Those are judgment calls that depend on relationships, history, and reading the situation. AI can hand you a generic framework, but it cannot make the call for you.

Reading the room. We spent a whole lesson on political intelligence. AI can't do any of it. It can't see the unspoken no, the pre-alignment that already happened in a corridor, the decision that's being deferred on purpose. It only processes what you hand it.

And the one that matters most: telling the truth when the truth is uncomfortable. This is the dangerous one, so stay with me. AI will cheerfully produce a green status report, a padded risk register, a deferential email — because it optimises for coherence, for sounding plausible and smooth. It does not optimise for the honest signal your situation actually requires. And it will state fabricated facts — numbers, dates, references — with complete, untroubled confidence. The coordinator using AI has to be the one who insists on the honest framing, because the tool never will.

---

### THE RULE — MECHANICS, NOT MEANING (6:50–8:30)

*[Statement slide: "Mechanics can be assisted. Meaning is yours."]*

So here's the rule I give every coordinator starting out with these tools. It's one line, and it carries the whole lesson. Use AI for mechanics, not for meaning.

Mechanics is the production of text at volume — drafting, formatting, rephrasing. The substance of what you're saying is yours; the production of the words can be assisted. Meaning is what you're actually saying — the judgment call, the framing, the specific signal you're sending. That is yours, and it stays yours. The moment you let AI make meaning for you, you've outsourced the exact part of the job that makes you a professional.

And there's a clean test for which is which. Could you defend what you sent? If a senior person turned to you and asked, "why did you frame it that way?" — could you give a reasoned answer, rooted in your own thinking? If the honest answer is "the AI suggested it," then the AI was doing work that should have been yours. A RAG rating is meaning. "The AI said green" is never, under any circumstances, a defence.

---

### SPECIFIC PRACTICES (8:30–10:40)

*[List slide: Draft your own · Accelerate · Never paste · Audit · Your voice]*

Let me make that rule concrete with practices you can use tomorrow.

Write your own first drafts while you're learning. For the first year or two in the role, write your own emails, status reports, minutes, and risk descriptions. Use AI as a tutor — ask it to critique your draft — not as a replacement for drafting it. The skill you build by writing your own first draft compounds. The skill you don't build by curating someone else's never arrives.

Accelerate, don't substitute. Once you can produce a reasonable first draft yourself, then use AI to get to a second draft faster. Give it your draft; ask for tightening, reorganisation, a register adjustment. You remain the author. That's the line — author, not editor of a stranger's words.

Never paste AI output directly into a sensitive communication. A sponsor status, steering-committee minutes, an escalation email — these go through your own edit, every time, even when the draft was AI-assisted. The edit is what makes it yours.

Audit AI output for accuracy, especially the specifics — dates, names, numbers, references. AI produces wrong facts with total confidence. Check everything consequential before it leaves your hands. The fabricated number that ships under your name is your fabricated number now.

And build your own voice, not the AI's. These tools have a recognisable register — slightly generic, slightly padded, reliably competent. If every output you produce lands in that register, people eventually notice your writing reads like everyone else's. Keep the edits that make it yours. Your phrasings, your structure, your specific word choices. That voice is an asset. Don't sand it off.

---

### THE TEST CASE — A GREEN THAT ISN'T (10:40–12:20)

*[Example slide: the AI-drafted steering-committee status, then the move]*

Let me put all of it in one moment, because this is the moment that's coming for you.

Your junior hands you a steering-committee status, AI-drafted. It rates a workstream GREEN — a workstream that failed its gate last week. It cites a metric that, when you check, doesn't exist anywhere; the AI invented it. The risk section is padded with generic entries. And the whole thing reads in that smooth, anonymous AI register. What do you do?

You don't rubber-stamp it. Shipping a fabricated green to a board is how careers end. And you don't respond with a contemptuous blanket ban that throws out a genuinely useful tool. You hold it from the committee, and you fix the substance. The RAG rating is a meaning call, and it's yours — GREEN is wrong, it's amber at best, and you own that judgment. You catch the invented number before it goes anywhere near a sponsor. You strip the dishonest framing. And the sponsor-facing version carries your edit and your accountability — not the tool's.

Then — and this is the part that's easy to skip when you're annoyed — you coach the junior developmentally. AI is genuinely useful for mechanics, so keep using it. But draft your own first, then assist, so the craft actually builds. And keep your own voice. You protect the board and you grow the person. Both.

---

### THE STRATEGIC SHIFT (12:20–13:25)

*[Statement slide: "the value moves up the stack"]*

Step back, because there's a larger question hanging over all of this. If AI can draft status reports, take minutes, extract actions, write change-request narratives — what's the distinctive value of a coordinator who does those things?

The answer, and I believe it holds, is that the value moves up the stack. The production of artifacts becomes less differentiating. The judgment about what to produce, what it should say, and how it should land becomes more. The coordinator of the next decade spends less time typing and more time thinking. The typing still happens — you supervise it — but the judgment is where the career lives.

Which means the judgment skills in this whole program — escalation, push-back, chasing, reading the room — don't get less important as AI gets better. They get more important. They're the part that stays yours when the production is assisted. That's the edge. Guard it.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:25–14:25)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. In the workbook, you'll audit your own use of these tools. For every use, you'll ask one question: am I accelerating my work, or substituting for it? Where you're substituting, you'll flag it to reverse — draft it yourself, then let AI tighten it. Then pick one kind of writing — minutes, maybe, or risk descriptions — that you've been outsourcing and want to own more deeply, and commit to producing the first draft yourself this week before you open any tool.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

The reason I started with that proud junior coordinator is that he's the whole lesson in miniature. He asked the wrong question — "is it working?" — and the answer was yes, which is exactly what made it a trap. The right question was "what am I learning?" and the answer was nothing. AI will make you faster at producing words from your first day. It will not, on its own, make you better at the judgment that those words are supposed to carry. Mechanics it can have. Meaning is yours. Spend this program building the part that stays yours.

See you in the workbook.
