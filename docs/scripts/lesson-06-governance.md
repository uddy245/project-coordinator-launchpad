# Lesson 6 — Governance, Roles, and the People You Answer To

### COLD OPEN — The steering committee that wasn't a steering committee (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I was about four years into coordinator work when I learned something about governance that I should have learned sooner. The project was a procurement transformation for a provincial government agency. Twelve-month budget, around four million dollars. The sponsor was a deputy minister. The steering committee had seven people — three assistant deputies, a director from finance, the chief procurement officer, and, in an arrangement I'd never seen before, a representative from the minister's political office.

I walked in thinking I understood governance. I'd read the standard texts. I'd been through org charts. I could fill out a RACI. I thought of governance as a structure — boxes connected by lines, roles with stated responsibilities.

What I learned, over about six months, was that the structure on paper had almost nothing to do with how decisions actually got made.

The committee met monthly. The deputy spoke last and briefly. The political representative was silent through every meeting — the single word I heard her say in six months was "thanks," at the end of her second meeting. The minutes described decisions as "the Committee agreed." They were accurate in the sense that no one disagreed in the room. They were misleading in every other sense.

Because the real decisions were made before the meetings. The deputy had a thirty-minute briefing from his chief of staff. The political representative had a standing weekly call with that same chief of staff. The chief procurement officer had his own monthly meeting with the deputy. The committee was a ratifying body. The actual decisions happened in pre-meetings and side channels, and the committee's job was to provide visible endorsement.

I didn't understand this for three months. I wrote the minutes as if the meeting was where decisions were made. I sent follow-ups to "the committee." I coordinated through the formal structure and got slow, cautious, generic responses.

---

### THE CONVERSATION THAT CHANGED IT (2:10–3:30)

*[Story slide]*

Month four, one of the assistant deputies took me aside after a meeting and said, gently, "You know how decisions really happen here, right?" I said I did. I was lying. He saw that I was lying. And then he spent twenty minutes walking me through the real structure — the specific names, the specific patterns, who actually had the deputy's trust and who only had the title.

After that conversation, I changed how I worked. I didn't write a different document. I didn't alter the RACI. I just started routing things differently. Before the committee met, I made sure our PM had briefed the deputy's chief of staff, so the deputy arrived already knowing what he thought. When a political concern surfaced, I made sure the minister's representative got a heads-up in advance, so nothing surprised her office. When we needed budget flexibility, I worked through the finance director's office ahead of time instead of hoping the committee would resolve it.

Our pace of decisions doubled the following month. Nothing in the formal structure changed.

That's the single most important thing about governance. The structure on paper is a starting point, not a description. Your job is to learn the real structure — not to enforce the stated one.

### WHAT GOVERNANCE ACTUALLY IS (3:30–5:05)

*[Statement slide, then list]*

So let me teach you both — the stated structure, because you need it, and the real one, because that's where the work is.

Most project governance gets taught as a set of roles and documents. A sponsor. A steering committee. A PM. A PMO. A RACI. A charter. A set of gates. These are real things. But they are not governance.

Governance is the system by which decisions about the project are made. Who decides what. Who gets consulted before decisions. Who is informed afterward. Who can escalate, and to whom. Who can veto, and over what. Who has budget authority, and up to what threshold. And who is accountable if things go wrong — and accountable to whom.

A governance structure tries to answer those questions in a way the organization understands and respects. When it works well, it speeds delivery by removing confusion about decision rights. When it works badly, it creates a theater of approvals that doesn't map to actual authority.

The reason new coordinators misread governance is that they take the documents at face value. The documents are a hypothesis about how decisions work. Your observations are the data. When they conflict, trust the data. That's not cynicism — it's reading. Use the formal structure as a map, and adjust when the map doesn't match the terrain.

### THE SPONSOR (5:05–7:00)

*[Def slide: good sponsor — then list: five failure modes]*

Start with the sponsor, because the sponsor is the most important person on your project. A good sponsor owns the business outcome, has authority to make decisions and unblock escalations, is engaged enough to give direction but not so engaged that they micromanage, and has genuine political capital to spend on the project's behalf. When you have one, your project has a reasonable chance. When you don't, it almost certainly won't.

A bad sponsor takes five forms, and each one has a counter-move. The absent sponsor is named on the charter and attends nothing — you work through their chief of staff or senior advisor, and you don't complain. The weak sponsor attends but defers, because they lack the political capital to assert themselves — so you do more of the pre-decision work, and make their job nothing harder than saying yes to a well-prepared recommendation. The over-engaged sponsor micromanages decisions that should be the PM's — you give them structured engagement, a regular one-to-one, a briefing document, so they feel legitimately in control. The wrong-level sponsor is too junior for the political weight the project needs — and often there's no good move except to flag it, through the PM, as a project risk that senior leadership has to consider. And the misaligned sponsor is personally invested in a different outcome than the one the project was authorized to produce — which calls for a candid early conversation: "what does success look like to you, personally?"

You'll rarely run these conversations yourself. But you can coach your PM into having them if you see the pattern. And here's the part that's directly yours: your relationship with the sponsor is almost always second-hand. You appear as the person who sends materials, captures their words in minutes, occasionally answers on the PM's behalf. By the time you actually meet the sponsor, they will already have a view of you — formed entirely from your written work. You never meet the sponsor for the first time.

### THE STEERING COMMITTEE — FOUR SHAPES (7:00–9:05)

*[List slide: four shapes, then a note on minutes]*

Now the steering committee. In practice it takes one of four shapes, and knowing which one you have is essential.

The decision-making committee is rare and the most effective — three to five members with real authority, who actually debate and decide. Its minutes reflect decisions made in the room. The ratifying committee is more common — five to ten members, where the real decisions are made elsewhere and the committee approves what's already been agreed. That's the shape my provincial project had. The ceremonial committee has eight to fifteen members, meets quarterly, and exists mostly for visibility — it receives reports, it doesn't decide. And the political committee has mixed membership and is as much about managing relationships between stakeholder groups as about the project itself. You'll meet that one in public sector work, in cross-business-unit programs, in anything with external partners.

Your job changes with the shape. For a decision-making committee, the pre-reads are substantive and the minutes are high-stakes. For a ratifying committee, the real work happens before the meeting — identify who needs briefing, anticipate the questions, prep the sponsor. For a ceremonial committee, you're managing attention: a pre-read that tells a coherent story in ten pages or less. For a political committee, it's heavy relationship work — individual briefings, mapping who has side-channels with whom.

One note on steering committee minutes, because they're not routine minutes. They're legal-ish documents that get referenced in contract disputes, audit reviews, post-project reviews — sometimes years later. Be precise about decisions. Specific about actions. Careful about attribution. If a decision was unanimous, say so. If it was made with dissent, say so. If a discussion was inconclusive and deferred, say so — don't paper over it with language that suggests agreement.

### THE PMO AND THE PM (9:05–11:00)

*[Def slide: PMO at its best / at its worst — then def: PM types]*

Two more relationships that will shape your daily life.

The PMO — the project management office — sits in an ambiguous relationship to your project. At its best, it gives you standards, tools, templates, and a portfolio view that helps projects avoid pitfalls others have hit. At its worst, it's a bureaucratic layer that adds reporting overhead with no delivery value. Most are somewhere in between. The moves that work: know exactly what the PMO needs and submit clean work on time. Treat the individual people as individuals — a PMO contact who feels respected is an ally; one who feels dismissed is a bureaucratic obstacle in waiting. Use them for help, because most PMO staff genuinely enjoy helping. Don't go around them — they notice, and the second offense is much worse than the first. And don't become a PMO employee in disguise: your first allegiance is to your project. The PM and the PMO are often mildly adversarial. Be reliable in both directions, and don't take sides.

The PM is your single most important relationship on a daily basis. You work for the PM in the functional sense, even when you report elsewhere on the org chart, and their assessment is the biggest factor in your career progression for the duration of the project. A good PM is a teacher — be genuinely engaged, ask questions, volunteer for stretch work, and stay humble. A mediocre PM is transactional — be self-directed, build your own learning loops, don't expect mentorship that isn't coming. A bad PM is rare but damaging — depending on severity, you cover, you switch, or you document, but you never try to manage them by manipulation.

And whatever the experience: leave every relationship as cleanly as you can. The industry is small. The PM who was mediocre when you were twenty-four will be senior at a different firm when you're thirty-four — and they may be in a position to hire you, or not.

### THE RACI AND READING THE ROOM (11:00–12:50)

*[Def slide: RACI letters — then list: reading the room habits]*

Let me give you the one tool everyone uses and almost everyone misuses: the RACI. Responsible does the work. Accountable owns the outcome and signs off — and there should be exactly one Accountable per decision. Consulted gives input before. Informed is told after. A good RACI is a clarification tool, and the conversation that builds it is usually more valuable than the document, because it surfaces unspoken assumptions. A bad RACI exists because a template demanded one — too many people Accountable, vague entries, never referenced again.

The most common failure: multiple people listed as Accountable for an important decision. That's wrong by definition, but it's everywhere, because organizations are uncomfortable concentrating accountability. Your move is to quietly flag it: "The RACI has three people Accountable for budget sign-off — in practice, who does the PM actually go to?" The answer is usually one person. Update the RACI to reflect reality.

And reality is the theme. Here's how you learn to read the real structure. Pay attention to pre-meetings — who briefs whom before the committee meets. Notice whose questions get substantive answers and whose get deflections — the first group has the sponsor's real attention. Watch who gets consulted outside the formal process — when a decision comes up, whom does the PM actually call? That's the truer list of Consulted. And track whose assessments shape the sponsor's view, because the sponsor forms opinions through a small group of trusted advisors, some on the committee and some not.

None of that is conspiracy thinking. It's just how organizations work. Formal structures are real, but they're overlays on human networks — and the coordinator who learns to read both becomes effective far beyond what the role description suggests.

### WHEN GOVERNANCE IS BROKEN (12:50–13:40)

*[List slide: five failure modes]*

You'll also learn to spot governance that's actually broken. Accountability is diffuse — ask who'll sign off and you get three names and a shrug. The steering committee is a black hole — decisions go in, updates come out, and the minutes say "the Committee noted," never "decided." The sponsor is performative — present, but every intervention is generic. The PMO has no teeth — it asks for reports it can't enforce. And governance bypasses have become the norm — the RACI says one thing, and everyone knows it's wrong.

When you see these patterns, the first move is to notice and form a private assessment. The second is to share it carefully with your PM, so the PM can decide what to do. You don't fix broken governance. You understand it, you work within it, and you make sure your PM sees it clearly enough to advocate for change where it's possible.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:40–14:30)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. In the workbook, you'll take your project's governance document — the charter, the RACI, whatever you have — and compare it to what you've actually observed in the last month, and mark every place the stated structure and reality diverge. Then you'll name the three to five people whose assessments actually shape the sponsor's view, and you'll write the one question you're going to ask your PM in your next one-to-one: how do decisions really get made on this project?

*[Back on camera, slower]*

The reason I opened with that government committee is that it's the whole lesson in miniature. The structure on paper said the committee decided. The reality was that seven people ratified what three people had already settled in a hallway. Nothing in the document was false — and almost nothing in the document was the truth. The job wasn't to fix that gap or to resent it. The job was to read it, and route the work through how things actually work. Learn to see that gap, on every project, and you stop being someone who fills out the RACI — and start being someone the real decision-makers come to.

See you in the workbook.
