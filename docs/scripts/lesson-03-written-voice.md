# Lesson 3 — The Written Voice of a Professional

### COLD OPEN — The email that nearly ended my probation (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with an email I sent in my third month on the job, because it nearly ended my probation, and because everything this lesson is about is hiding inside it.

I'd been hired in January with a six-month probation. The team liked me. I hadn't broken anything visible. And then in late April, a senior director — not my sponsor, but a peer of my sponsor, in another business unit — got cc'd on a status report where we'd flagged an amber on a deliverable his team owed us. He emailed me back. Not my PM. Me. A sharp two-line reply, asking why we were calling his team out in public.

I read it at four o'clock on a Friday. I was tired, and I was annoyed, and I felt the rating was defensible. So I wrote a reply. It was polite. It didn't swear. It wasn't sent in anger. And it was wrong on every level that mattered.

I'll show you the actual email in a minute, because I still have it — it lives in the file where I keep the lessons I don't want to forget. At 5:01 that director forwarded it to my PM with one line: "Your coordinator is telling me my team missed a deliverable. Please handle." My PM called at 5:15. She wasn't angry. She was thoughtful, which was worse. She said, "Come in Monday. I want to walk you through what happened here. It's not what you think."

I spent the weekend sure I'd been right. I was wrong on both counts.

---

### WHY THIS MATTERS MORE THAN YOU THINK (2:10–3:40)

*[Slide: "your writing is the most visible surface of your professional self"]*

Before I take you back to that email, let me tell you why it carried so much weight — because it isn't really about one email.

A coordinator at a mid-sized firm writes, conservatively, between fifty and a hundred separate pieces of prose a week. Email, Slack, minutes, status narratives, risk descriptions, change requests, briefings. Almost all of them are read by someone else. And a meaningful fraction are read by people senior to you, or people you've never met — people whose entire impression of you will be formed by what you put on a page, because you'll never be in the room with them.

So here's the plain fact. Your written voice is the most visible surface of your professional self. And decisions about you are made on its basis. Not big formal ones — small, cumulative ones, made in the three seconds after an email lands. Whether someone reads through or skims. Whether they follow up or disengage. Whether, when your name comes up about staffing the next project, they remember you as "good" or as "fine."

You cannot easily out-work bad writing. But you can, with some effort, learn to write well enough that your prose works *for* you. That's the whole game.

---

### WHAT PROFESSIONAL PROSE ACTUALLY IS (3:40–5:00)

*[List slide: the four commitments]*

Let me name the thing directly, because most guidance dances around it. Professional prose is writing that respects the reader's time, states what it needs to clearly, takes responsibility for its own claims, and doesn't ask the reader to do interpretive work you should have done first.

Four commitments. Respect for the reader's time means you write as short as you can while still being clear. No padding. No throat-clearing. Clear statement means what you're saying, asking, and want done is unambiguous — a reader should never have to read your document twice to figure out what it was for. Responsibility for your claims means you say things you believe, without hedging; and if you're genuinely uncertain, you name the uncertainty with precision instead of hiding behind vague softeners. And not asking the reader to interpret means you've already done the structuring — the reader who stops after sentence one still gets the most important thing.

Everything else in this lesson is just how to deliver on those four in specific situations. Let me show you the situations.

---

### LEAD WITH THE LEDE (5:00–6:30)

*[Example slide: buried lede vs lede-first]*

Most unstudied writers bury the lede. They start with context, then more context, and three paragraphs in, they finally arrive at the point. That's how we're taught to write in school — building toward a conclusion. It's the wrong model for professional writing.

Professional writing is inverted. The lede is first.

Here's a real one. A coordinator writing in school mode opens with "Hi Helen, hope your morning's going well, just wanted to let you know about a couple of things," and then somewhere in the middle the reader learns that the vendor has slipped the integration plan, that it affects testing, and that Raj hasn't been told. Twenty-five seconds to read. The thing Helen actually needs to decide is buried.

Now the same email, lede first. "Helen — vendor has slipped the integration plan from today to next Wednesday. This affects testing, which was planning to start dependent work Tuesday. I haven't spoken to Raj yet — want me to, or will you?" Ten seconds. A clear ask. The decision is visible in the second sentence.

The second version isn't terser because the writer cared less. It's terser because the writer did more work — they decided what mattered *before* they started writing. And your subject line is the first lede of all. "Update," "Quick question," "Following up" — those aren't subject lines. They're labels for the fact that an email exists. Try "Vendor delivery slipped — needs decision on testing start." Let the reader triage from the subject alone.

---

### STRIP THE HEDGES (6:30–8:00)

*[Example slide: nine hedges vs zero]*

Now to the email that nearly ended my probation — because the first thing wrong with it was hedging's close cousin, but let me deal with hedges directly first, because this is one of the highest-return edits you'll ever make.

A hedge is a word or phrase that distances you from what you're saying. "I think. I believe. Perhaps. Maybe. Just. It seems. I might be wrong but. Just a thought." Each one has a use in isolation. The problem is junior writers use them in clusters, and each additional hedge compounds the weakening.

I once got this from a coordinator I was managing. "I was just wondering if maybe we should perhaps think about moving the design review to next week? I might be wrong but it seems like a few of the key people might not be available, and I was just thinking it could maybe be better to have a bit more time. Just a thought though." Four sentences. Nine hedges. She'd actually done the analysis — checked availability, formed a view — and then buried the whole thing so deep I had to write back and ask what she actually thought.

The unhedged version. "Suggest we move the design review to next week — three of the key reviewers are out this week, and the extra time lets us circulate the pre-read properly. OK with you?" Same information. Same politeness. But the writer has taken a position, and the decision loop is short. Here's the test: for each hedge, ask, is this carrying information, or am I using it to soften the fact that I'm saying something? If it's the second, cut it.

---

### STOP APOLOGISING REFLEXIVELY (8:00–9:15)

*[List slide: the three costs of the automatic sorry]*

Closely related, and worth naming separately, is the apology. Junior coordinators apologise reflexively. "Sorry to bother you. Sorry for the delay. Sorry to keep chasing. Sorry if this is a silly question." Each is sometimes appropriate. None should be automatic. Used automatically, they do three bad things.

They inflate. If every ordinary interaction starts with sorry, you've got nothing left for the moments that actually warrant one. They mis-signal — if you apologise every time you chase, you're framing a normal part of your job as an imposition, which makes people less likely to respond well to being chased. And they undermine authority. You cannot apologise your way into being taken seriously. The coordinator who opens every email with a micro-apology is the one whose requests get deprioritised and whose deadlines get treated as flexible.

So apologise when you've actually done something that warrants it. "Sorry, I got the numbers wrong in yesterday's status — here's the corrected version." That one lands, because it's doing work. But don't apologise for doing your job, for asking questions, for following up, for taking space, for existing. If you can't start an email without an apology, replace it with a direct action opener. Not "sorry to bother you, but I wanted to check on the vendor review" — just "Checking in on the vendor review — do you have an ETA?"

---

### MATCH THE REGISTER (9:15–10:35)

*[List slide: conversational · standard · formal]*

Professional prose isn't one thing. Think of register as a dial with three positions.

Conversational is what you use with peers and with your PM in routine interactions. Not sloppy — still professional — but it allows contractions, short sentences, the occasional bit of humour. Standard is your default for most writing that goes beyond your immediate team: polite, neutral, efficient, full sentences, no jokes, clear structure. And formal is for sponsors, external readers, steering-committee minutes — anything that might be read by people you don't know. Spare, precise, free of anything casual. Not cold — dignified.

The error new coordinators make is using the wrong register for the audience. They write to a senior director in the relaxed tone they use with their PM, and come across as not serious enough. Or they write to their PM in the stiff tone they'd use for a sponsor. Or — most common — they write the same neutral-corporate register to everyone, which makes them sound like a generic voice that can't modulate.

And here's the specific rule that would have saved me a Friday. When you reply, match or *slightly elevate* the register of the incoming message. You don't drop below the register they set, because dropping register reads as presumption — as if you've claimed a familiarity that wasn't offered. You can raise it slightly, because raising register reads as respect.

### THE EMAIL, SENTENCE BY SENTENCE (10:35–12:05)

*[Example slide: the probation email; then the verdict]*

So. Monday morning. My PM pulled my email up on her screen and went through it sentence by sentence.

"Thanks for your note" was fine. Then: "I understand your concern, but I think the amber rating is accurate based on the facts." Three errors in one sentence. "I understand your concern" reads, in that register, as *I'm about to dismiss your concern*. "But I think" is an adversarial construction where a senior person expected a collegial one. And "accurate based on the facts" implicitly accused him of not having his facts straight. The sentence I was proudest of — "the rating reflects the current status, not an assignment of fault" — I'd meant as a peace offering. It read as a lecture: *let me explain to you, a director, what our rating system means*. And "I'd be happy to discuss further if you think the rating should be changed" — that was the real killer. To me it sounded conciliatory. To him it read as *I'm formally offering you the chance to ask me to change a rating I consider correct, which you may do if you insist*.

Then she said the thing I've carried ever since. "You wrote a defensible email. You also wrote an email that tells a senior director you're willing to argue with him in writing. That is not your job. Your job here was to acknowledge him, route it to me, and let me handle it." And then: "If this were your eighteenth month instead of your third, it would be recoverable. At three months, I have to actively manage the damage — and you have to watch me do it."

She did. She emailed him, copied me, took the blame squarely on herself for not coaching me sooner, and proposed a coffee. The relationship was repaired in three weeks. My probation was confirmed in July. But I'd learned, at the cost of a weekend of anxiety, that written voice is a real skill, and getting it wrong has real consequences.

---

### THE FORMS THAT TEST YOU (12:05–13:15)

*[Example slide: bad minutes vs good minutes; bad-news structure]*

A few specific forms, because the voice transcends the form. Minutes are not transcripts. Bad minutes read like "John said he thought we should go with Option A, Priya said she wasn't sure, after some discussion the team seemed to agree." Good minutes read like a record. "Decision: proceed with Option A. Action: Priya to complete additional analysis by May 24 and flag concerns by end of that week; if none raised, Option A is confirmed." Half the space, no ambiguity, and still usable six months from now.

Risk descriptions carry weight only when they're specific. Not "risk that vendor may be late" — that's nothing, that's a meeting waiting to happen. Instead: name the constraint, what it affects, the impact and likelihood, and the mitigation, in ninety words.

And bad news. The instinct is to soften — bury it, lead with positives, hedge the severity. That's wrong. Bad news is worse when buried; the reader works harder, then feels manipulated when they realise they were being cushioned. Four sentences. The bad news, plainly. The minimal context. What's being done. What, if anything, you need from them. "David — integration delivery has slipped from end of May to the third week of June. Vendor capacity issue, not a scope change; we've tracked it for two weeks. Helen is working the downstream impact and will bring options to Thursday's SC. Let us know if you need an interim briefing." Slightly hard to read, and honest. That's the right ratio.

---

### THE EDITING PASS (13:15–13:55)

*[Statement slide]*

One discipline does more than any other. Before you send anything of consequence, read it once with a single question in mind: *what can I cut?*

Almost everything you write can be twenty percent shorter without losing anything. Hedges not carrying information. Throat-clearing openings. Padding — "at this point in time" becomes "now," "in order to" becomes "to." Context the reader already has. The pass takes ninety seconds for a short email. I've been editing my own prose for thirty years and I still cut twenty percent every time. It's not optional. It's the difference between writing that's merely acceptable and writing that's actively working for you. And in the age of AI drafts, it matters more, not less — the model gives you a competent, generic, slightly padded draft, and the editing pass is what makes it sound like a person wrote it. The editorial judgment is yours. That's the part that doesn't get delegated.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:55–14:30)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. In the workbook, open your sent folder and read your last five substantive emails. For each, ask: is the lede in the first sentence? How many hedges? Is the register right for the audience? How much could I cut? Don't rewrite them — just notice. Then write one risk description in the richer form, ninety to a hundred words, with specifics and a named mitigation, and notice how it reads next to the one-line descriptions around it.

*[Back on camera, slower]*

The reason I started with that probation email is that it's the whole lesson in miniature. Every sentence was polite. Every sentence was defensible. And it still told a senior director I was willing to argue with him in writing, three months into a job where I had no capital to spend. The words were doing something I never intended — and I couldn't see it, because I'd never been taught to read my own prose the way the reader would. That's the skill. Learn to read what your words are actually doing, before you hit send. Do that, and your writing starts coming back with more trust than it left with.

See you in the workbook.
