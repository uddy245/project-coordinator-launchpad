# Lesson 25 — The Path From Coordinator to PM (and Whether to Take It)
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30
**Source chapter:** Handbook Ch 25; reading `docs/lessons/coordinator-to-pm.md`
**Lesson slug:** coordinator-to-pm
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1. This is the final lesson of the program — the close should land as a capstone.

---

### COLD OPEN — The wrong question (0:00–2:05)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start the last lesson of this program the way I started the most important conversation of my career — by asking the wrong question, out loud, to someone who had the patience to fix it.

Around year six, I had a coffee with a senior PM I admired. Not my own PM at the time — someone I'd worked with on a previous project, whose judgment I trusted. I'd been wanting to ask her one thing for months, and I finally asked it.

How do I become a PM?

She laughed. She said it was the wrong question. The right question, she told me, was: do I actually want to be a PM — and if so, what about the role am I drawn to?

She said that because she'd watched, over a whole career, too many coordinators step into PM roles simply because it was the next rung. And then discover they didn't enjoy the parts of the job that had been invisible to them. The accountability to the sponsor. The political navigation. The carrying of the project's emotional weight. The comfort with ambiguity they'd never needed as coordinators. Some adapted. Some were miserable and quit within two years. A few went back to senior coordination roles — and were happier.

She asked me to think for a week about what I actually wanted. Then, she said, we'd talk about how to get there.

I spent that week more thoughtfully than I'd spent any previous week on my career. And I realized, with some clarity, that I did want it — not because of the title, but because the parts of the role I was curious about, the judgment, the strategy, the sponsor relationship, were exactly the parts I was already trying to observe. And the parts I was anxious about, the accountability, the political weight, were exactly the parts I needed to grow into.

A year and a half later, I was a PM. This lesson is about that transition — what it actually is, how to prepare for it, and how to decide whether to take it at all.

---

### IT'S A ROLE CHANGE, NOT A PROMOTION (2:05–4:00)

*[Statement slide, then list.]*

So let me start where my mentor eventually started with me. The move from coordinator to PM is not a promotion. It's a role change. And mistaking the one for the other is where most of the pain comes from.

A coordinator supports. A PM owns. That's the whole thing in five words, and it's worth sitting with.

A coordinator produces artifacts under the PM's direction and approval. A PM is accountable for whether the project succeeds, measured against objectives the sponsor set. The PM's name is on the charter. The PM answers to the steering committee. The PM defends the budget at the sponsor's forum. The PM makes the calls you, as a coordinator, were observing from the side. This is a different job — not a bigger version of the one you have.

Some of your skills transfer directly. Written voice. Organizational intelligence. Operational discipline. Those come with you, intact, and they're worth more than you think. But others have to be built new — sponsor management, strategic framing, accountability under pressure, the ability to hold a project together emotionally when things are hard.

And here's the trap. The mistake some coordinators make is assuming that if they just do the coordinator job well enough, they'll become a good PM automatically. They won't. Being a good coordinator is necessary but not sufficient. The step requires deliberately building the skills the coordinator role never asked you to exercise. Excellence in the role you have does not, on its own, manufacture the role you want.

---

### SHOULD YOU TAKE IT? FOUR HONEST QUESTIONS (4:00–6:25)

*[List slide: the four questions.]*

Now the harder part, and the part most people skip. Not everyone should take this step. So here are the four honest questions my mentor was really asking me to sit with that week.

One. Do I want the accountability? PMs are accountable for outcomes. When the project goes wrong, the PM carries the conversation with the sponsor. When the team is burning out, the PM is the one who has to act. When a hard call needs making, the PM makes it. That weight is real. Some people thrive on it. Others find it corrosive over time. Be honest about which you are — not which you wish you were.

Two. Do I want the political exposure? PMs are more visible than coordinators, and more often in rooms where the politics are live. That can be fascinating, and it can be exhausting. Here's a clean test: think about the rooms you've already observed as a coordinator. If they felt energizing, you're probably suited to the PM role. If they felt draining, the PM role will feel more so, not less.

Three. Do I want to spend less time on artifacts and more on people? PMs spend a far larger share of their time on stakeholders, sponsors, team dynamics, and decisions than on producing documents. Some coordinators love the craft of producing artifacts and genuinely miss it when they move up. Others find it tedious and welcome the shift. Neither answer is wrong — but you need to know yours.

Four. Am I willing to be wrong publicly? PMs make calls that turn out wrong, and some of those calls are visible. You have to own them, learn from them, and keep going. If the idea of being publicly wrong is unbearable to you, the PM role will be painful in a way no amount of skill will fix.

If most of these land as yes, the step is probably for you. If several land as no — hear this clearly — senior coordinator or specialist roles can be satisfying, respected, and lucrative without the costs of the PM role. Either choice is defensible. Coordinators are professionals in their own right, not apprentice PMs.

---

### BUILD THE SKILLS BEFORE YOU NEED THEM (6:25–8:45)

*[List slide: five deliberate moves.]*

Say you've sat with the four questions and landed on yes. Good. Now you build toward it deliberately, while you're still a coordinator — because the worst time to start learning the PM job is the day you're handed it.

Observe your PM intentionally. Every single interaction they have is a small lesson. How they prep for a sponsor meeting. How they frame bad news. How they handle a difficult conversation. Watch for the specific moves, and then ask yourself how you would have handled the same situation. Over years, that quiet observational practice teaches you the craft from the inside, for free.

Ask for stretch responsibilities. In your one-on-ones, ask your PM to delegate pieces of their work that sit slightly beyond your role. Drafting a sponsor briefing. Running a small workshop. Leading a vendor conversation. Most PMs say yes when asked thoughtfully — and each stretch is a rehearsal for the full role.

Begin handling small things end-to-end. Before the title ever changes, ask to own something small with real accountability. A sub-workstream. A low-risk internal initiative. The experience of carrying something across the finish line yourself is genuinely different from supporting someone else's project. Do it on something small before you ever have to do it on something large.

Find mentors. The senior PM whose coffee reframed my whole career was not my own PM. Often the most useful mentor is someone a rung or two above you, outside your direct reporting line. Seek them out. Ask for twenty minutes a quarter. Most senior people are generous with their time to juniors who ask thoughtfully.

And read outside the discipline. The PM canon is useful. But leadership, organizational behavior, decision-making — that broader literature is often more useful, because the PM role is, in large part, the management of humans through uncertainty.

---

### THE FIRST PM PROJECT (8:45–10:45)

*[List slide: four things to expect.]*

When the opportunity finally comes — and it comes through a promotion, a lateral move, or a slow expansion of scope until the title catches up — your first project as the PM will feel different from anything before it. Four things to expect, so they don't surprise you.

You will miss being a coordinator. The production work — the artifacts, the records, the chasing-down of actions — was satisfying in a way the PM role often isn't. Early on, you'll feel the pull to reach back down into the operational work. Resist it, mostly. Your job is now upstream.

You will feel imposter syndrome. Even when you're ready, the weight of accountability feels strange the first few times it lands. The steering committee is waiting for your view. The sponsor is watching your face. The team is looking to you for direction. That's normal — and it fades as you accumulate reps.

You will make visible mistakes. Early PM mistakes are the most common and the most formative. You'll misjudge a sponsor, miscalibrate a status, under-escalate an issue. The goal was never to avoid every mistake — that's impossible. The goal is to handle them well when they happen.

And your old coordinator habits will carry you. Reliability. Signal clarity. Written voice. Those aren't left behind — they become the foundation that frees up your attention for the new skills. Coordinators who skipped the craft of the coordinator role tend to struggle as PMs, precisely because they never built that foundation. The years you spent doing this job well are not a detour. They're the floor you'll stand on.

---

### THE CONVERSATION BEING TESTED (10:45–12:00)

*[Example slide: the open door.]*

Let me make this concrete, because there's one specific moment this whole lesson is preparing you for.

A PM you trust opens the door. They ask you, plainly: where do you want to go? It's a development conversation, and how you handle it tells them more about your readiness than any artifact you've ever produced.

The unskilled move is one of two things. You chase the title — "I want to be a PM, when can I be a PM" — which reads as ambition without judgment. Or you abdicate — "whatever you think is best" — which hands them a decision that was yours to make.

The skilled move is neither. You come to that conversation having already worked the four honest questions against your own evidence — and reached a reasoned answer, whether that's yes, no, or not yet. You show you understand a PM is a role change with new skills to build, not just a promotion. You propose concrete stretch steps that build exactly those skills — own something small end-to-end, draft a sponsor briefing, find a mentor. And you own the choice with clear eyes about its costs. A thoughtful reflection, plus a specific, reasonable ask. That's the kind of conversation a good PM will meet generously — because you've done the thinking, and made it easy to help you.

---

### THE LONGER ARC (12:00–13:10)

*[Story slide. Slower. This is the capstone.]*

Here's where I want to end the whole program — honestly.

When you're in year one or two of this work, the future feels long and the stakes of every decision feel enormous. By year ten, you'll see something that's hard to believe now: the specific projects recede quickly. The ones you worried about, the ones that dominated your attention, the ones you took home with you — they fade. What persists is the professional you became while doing them.

You are not building a portfolio of projects. You are building a version of yourself. The version who knows how to hold a room, write a sentence that lands, read a situation, tell the truth under pressure, keep a commitment, own a mistake, treat people with respect, and do hard work consistently over long horizons.

Everything in this program — the artifacts, the methods, the judgment, the identity — all of it has been scaffolding for that one construction. Some of you will become PMs. Some of you won't. The craft is the same either way, and it matters on its own, regardless of where the career eventually goes. The scaffolding matters. But the person is the point.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:10–14:20)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close.]*

So here's your final work for this program. In the workbook, you'll answer the four honest questions against your own evidence — accountability, political exposure, people over artifacts, being wrong publicly — and write down a reasoned yes, no, or not-yet, with the reason. Then you'll script the development conversation: the reflection you'd offer, and the one specific, reasonable stretch step you'd ask for.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

The reason I opened with the wrong question I asked over coffee in year six is that the whole lesson is hiding inside it. "How do I become a PM" is a question about a title. "Do I actually want this, and what am I drawn to" is a question about a self. My mentor didn't answer the first one. She made me answer the second. And answering it honestly is what got me there — and what would have kept me happily where I was, if the honest answer had been no.

You have the program now. The rest is reps. Take the job seriously — not grimly, with care. Coordination is real professional work, and the people who treat it that way are the ones who go on to shape projects, teams, and careers.

Good luck out there.
