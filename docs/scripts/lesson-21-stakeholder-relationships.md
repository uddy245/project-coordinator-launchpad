# Lesson 21 — Stakeholder Relationships That Last
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30
**Source chapter:** Handbook Ch 21; reading `docs/lessons/stakeholder-relationships.md`
**Lesson slug:** stakeholder-relationships
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1 and Lesson 20.

---

### COLD OPEN — The client I had not seen in eight years (0:00–2:00)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a LinkedIn message, because almost everything in this lesson is hiding inside it.

In 2019 I got a message from a woman I had worked with in 2011. She had been a business analyst on her side, I had been a coordinator on mine. We worked together for about ten months on a claims-system project, we were on good terms, and then the project ended and we did not keep in touch. Eight years went by.

Her message was short. She was now a director at a different firm. They had a delivery program starting the following quarter, and she wanted to know two things: whether my firm would be interested in pitching, and whether I would be the PM if we did.

We pitched. We won. The program was four million dollars of consulting work across eighteen months.

Now here's the part I want you to sit with. That engagement came to me because, eight years earlier, I had returned her emails promptly. I had written minutes that didn't misrepresent her positions. I had handled a small dispute between our teams without making it worse. And when she left her role at the client during the original project, I'd sent her a brief, sincere note thanking her for working with us.

That's it. I had done my job, with a little care. The care got noticed, remembered, and almost a decade later it turned into a material business opportunity. This lesson is about that — the care.

---

### THE ONLY DEFENSIBLE STRATEGY (2:00–3:20)

*[Statement slide: "treat every relationship as if it might be the important one"]*

Careers in project delivery compound through relationships. You will work with thousands of people over a working life — and a small fraction of them will become long-term professional allies. The trouble is that you cannot tell, when you meet someone, which fraction they're in. The business analyst in 2011 did not arrive with a label on her saying "this one matters in eight years."

So the only defensible strategy is the simple one: treat every working relationship as if it might be the important one. Because some of them will be, and you cannot predict which.

I want to be precise about what this lesson is not. It is not about networking. It is not about the strategic cultivation of useful people. The word "networking" carries a whiff of the transactional, of working a room with an eye on what you can extract, and I reject that framing entirely. This is about something quieter and more durable: becoming the kind of professional whom people, looking back years later, remember as a pleasure to work with.

---

### THE FOUR QUALITIES PEOPLE REMEMBER (3:20–5:35)

*[List slide: Reliability · Respect · Integrity · Generosity]*

So what do people actually remember, years later, about a colleague? Four things.

Reliability. Did you do what you said you'd do, when you said you'd do it? If the answer is usually yes, you get remembered as someone who could be counted on. This is the foundational quality, and most of the others flow from it.

Respect. Did you treat people as adults, regardless of their level? Were you as polite to the junior analyst as you were to the sponsor? Did you credit other people's contributions fairly? Here's the thing about respect — people remember how they were made to feel in the small moments. They remember whether you made them feel respected on a day when you were busy and they were not important.

Integrity. When things got difficult, did you behave consistently with your stated values? Did you tell the truth, even the uncomfortable truth? Did you refuse to throw someone else under the bus when throwing them under the bus would have been convenient? Integrity under pressure is exactly what separates the remembered colleagues from the forgotten ones.

And generosity. Did you share credit, share information, share help — without being asked? Generosity is expensive in the short term and it compounds enormously over the long term.

Now, none of those four is unusual in the abstract. Everyone agrees with all four. What's unusual is consistency — being reliable on Tuesday of week forty-seven of a brutal project. Being respectful to the junior analyst on day three of bad news. Being honest when the honest answer is the awkward one. Consistency is what builds the reputation that lasts.

---

### THE THREE CIRCLES (5:35–7:30)

*[List slide: Inner · Middle · Outer]*

Let me give you a map for all of this, because it helps to see where your relationships actually live. Picture three concentric circles.

The inner circle is the people you work with directly, day by day. Your PM. Your workstream leads. Your key stakeholders, your close peers. Ten to thirty people at any given time. You maintain these relationships through the work itself — you don't need to do anything special beyond doing your job well.

The middle circle is the people you've worked with in the past, still know reasonably well, and could credibly reach out to without it being strange. Former PMs. Past colleagues. Clients from prior projects. A hundred or more people over time. These need occasional, light maintenance — an email every year or two, a note on a shared connection, a congratulation on a promotion — to stay warm.

The outer circle is the thousands of people you've worked with once or briefly, whose names you remember but whom you don't really know. You don't actively maintain these. They exist as latent possibilities.

And here's the compounding effect of a career: over time, all three circles grow, and people migrate inward. Someone from your outer circle moves into a role where they can hire you. Someone in your middle circle becomes your peer in a senior position. Someone from your inner circle becomes the senior who opens a door. The business analyst from 2011 started in my inner circle, drifted out to the middle, and then walked all the way back in as the person who handed me a four-million-dollar program.

---

### MAINTENANCE WITHOUT PERFORMANCE (7:30–9:50)

*[List slide: Respond · Remember · Introduce · Keep promises · Congratulate]*

So how do you keep the middle circle warm without it feeling like a performance? Because the relationships that matter are almost never built at networking events. They're built through decent, consistent behaviour over time. Here are five practices that cost almost nothing and signal a great deal.

Respond to old colleagues promptly. When someone you worked with three years ago sends you a note, reply within a few days — even if the reply is brief. The cost is low. The signal, that you valued the relationship enough to respond, compounds.

Remember specifics. When you do reconnect, reference something real. Not "great working with you" — say "the access-matrix you built made the whole workstream tractable." Specifics prove you actually remember them, which is something most people don't do for most relationships.

Introduce generously. If you know two people who'd benefit from knowing each other, introduce them. Ask both first, briefly. A good introduction costs you fifteen minutes and can change someone's career.

Keep promises to old contacts. If you said three months ago you'd send a book recommendation, send it. Old promises are the easiest to forget and the most damaging when forgotten. And a late follow-up — "finally getting to this, here's that book I mentioned" — turns a potential breach into a reinforcement.

Congratulate real achievements. When someone you worked with gets promoted or accomplishes something visible, send two sincere sentences. Not everyone does this. The ones who do are remembered.

None of this is strategic. It's just attention. And over years, attention accumulates into a network that actually exists — as opposed to a list of names on a screen.

---

### WHEN TO STEP FORWARD (9:50–11:15)

*[Statement slide: "the people who step up when it matters build the strongest reputations"]*

Every so often, a moment arrives where you can make a disproportionate difference for someone — at modest cost to yourself. A former colleague needs a lead and you know of one. A junior's good work went uncredited and you can name them. A peer is in a difficult spot and needs an ally.

The temptation, every time, is to be busy. To decide it's not your problem. To let someone else step up. But the people who step up when it matters are the people whose reputations build the most. Not always. Not predictably. But over a career — disproportionately.

The rule I've followed, imperfectly but consistently, is this: when someone can be helped without compromising anything else, help. And help *helpfully* — not as an investment. The help is not a deposit you expect to withdraw later. If it benefits you down the line, fine. If it never does, also fine. The help was the right thing to do, and that's the whole of it.

---

### RELATIONSHIPS THAT NEED TO END (11:15–12:45)

*[Example slide: difficult-sometimes versus toxic-pattern]*

Now the other side, because it would be naive to pretend every relationship is worth keeping.

Some people are toxic in ways that will contaminate your career if you stay close to them. Consistently dishonest. Takes credit they didn't earn. Throws others under the bus as a habit. You do not have to burn these bridges — that's a rookie move that costs you more than it costs them. You simply stop investing. You respond professionally when contacted. You don't initiate. You don't spend energy preserving the connection. Over time it fades, which is exactly the right outcome.

But here is the distinction that matters, and it's the one people get wrong. There's a difference between someone who is *difficult in specific situations* — which, honestly, most of us are sometimes — and someone who is *toxic as a professional*. The difference is the pattern. One-off incidents do not define a person. Patterns do. A single bad day, a single sharp email, a single dropped ball is not a verdict. So before you let a relationship thin, ask whether you're looking at an incident or a pattern. If it's a pattern, and it's concerning, let the relationship fade. Don't confront, don't capitulate — just stop investing.

---

### THE MOVES, IN ONE PROJECT CLOSE (12:45–13:45)

*[List slide: Reconnect · Correct credit · Congratulate · Decline]*

Let me show you what all of this looks like in a single, ordinary moment — the close of a project.

A colleague who genuinely helped you is leaving. You send a sincere, specific note — naming the thing they actually did. The quiet junior's work got credited vaguely, so you correct the record to the people who matter, by name. A former boss has moved up, so you send a brief no-ask congratulation. And a peer whose toxic pattern you've watched for months asks you to team up and vouch for them — so you decline, courteously, without burning the bridge.

Four small moves. The throughline is the same in every one: you do them because they're right, with a little care — not because they're useful. The care is the part that gets remembered.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:45–14:30)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Think of three people you worked with in the past, whom you valued and haven't spoken to in over a year. Send a short note to one of them this week — no ask, no pretext, just "thinking about the such-and-such project, hope you're doing well," and see what comes back. Over the next month, notice one small chance to help someone in your network without being asked, and take it. And finally, look at your current project and ask whether there's a relationship you're letting drift through neglect. If there is, invest five minutes in it this week.

*[Back on camera, slower]*

I keep coming back to that LinkedIn message because it's the whole lesson in miniature. I didn't network my way to a four-million-dollar program. I returned some emails, wrote honest minutes, and sent one sincere note eight years before it mattered. The task, back in 2011, was just to do my job. The work — the thing that actually paid off a decade later — was doing it with a little care. Spend your career on the right side of that distinction, and the relationships will compound on their own.

See you in the workbook.
