# Lesson 13 — Status Reports: Three Audiences, Three Reports

### COLD OPEN — The status report that got me promoted (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I do not think I am being grandiose when I tell you that a single status report, written in my ninth year, was the single most visible moment in my whole progression toward becoming a PM. One page. One Thursday afternoon. And it changed how I was seen for the next three years.

It was not a dramatic report. A large systems integration program was in trouble — not catastrophically, but accumulating, in the quiet way projects accumulate, in ways the weekly reports had been soft-pedaling. The PM was under pressure. The sponsor was losing confidence. The PMO was asking hard questions that the previous reports had not really answered.

I was asked, on a Thursday afternoon, to draft the next weekly report. The PM was traveling and would approve it remotely.

So I wrote something different from what the team had been sending. I opened with three sentences that stated plainly where the program was: amber overall, two workstreams at risk of slipping the September milestone, cost tracking within baseline but with pressure building. Then a short, specific summary of what had slipped and why — no euphemisms, no "challenges," no "opportunities." Then the decisions needed from the sponsor that week, each with options and a recommended path. And I finished with three risks that had emerged in the last fortnight, and how each one was being managed.

The report was about a page. The previous reports had been four.

I sent it to the PM with a note: "Here's a draft. More direct than usual. Happy to tone it down if it's too much." He wrote back within two hours: "Don't tone it down. This is what the program needs. Send it."

---

### WHAT THE PAGE DID (2:10–3:30)

*[Story slide.]*

So I sent it. And the sponsor responded the next morning — with specific questions, specific decisions, and a direct call with the PM. Within a week, that program had a recovery plan, a renegotiated milestone, and better sponsor engagement than it had enjoyed in two months. The sponsor, in a conversation my PM later relayed to me, said: "I feel like I finally understand what's happening on this program."

Two things happened from that one page. First, the program recovered. Second — and this is the part that shaped my career — my PM told the next sponsor I would work with, and the one after that, that I wrote the best status reports he had ever read. That sentence, passed from senior person to senior person, decided how I was seen for three years. Eventually, it helped me make the step to PM.

Here's why I lead with it. Status reports are the most consistently visible written product of your role. They reach people you will never meet. They form impressions — of the project, and of you — that accumulate week over week, whether you intend them to or not. Done well, they're one of the highest-leverage things you can produce. Done poorly, they are expensive noise. This lesson is about doing them well.

---

### THREE AUDIENCES, THREE REPORTS (3:30–5:30)

*[List slide: Delivery team · PM & stakeholders · Sponsor & steering]*

The first thing to understand is that "the status report" is usually not one document for one audience. It is the same underlying information, cut for three different audiences with three genuinely different needs.

The delivery team needs operational detail. Who's doing what, what's blocked, what's coming up, what slipped. Their version is short, data-dense, operational — a standup update, a sprint summary, a team-facing note. They get it at least weekly, often more.

The project manager and immediate stakeholders need tactical awareness. The current state of the plan, what changed, what needs attention in the next two weeks, which risks are live. This is the canonical weekly report — one to two pages, weekly.

The sponsor and steering committee need strategic situational awareness. Are we on track for our objectives? What are the major risks? What decisions do they need to make? They don't need detail; they need signal. One page, or a single slide. Biweekly or monthly, plus the steering pack.

Now here is the mistake almost everyone makes. Most coordinators produce one report for all three audiences — and that is almost always wrong. The operational detail overwhelms the sponsor. The sponsor-level summary is too thin for the team. And the tactical report is the right length but the wrong register for both ends.

The fix is not three documents written from scratch — you don't have time for that, and you shouldn't. It's one body of source material — the current facts of the project — packaged three ways. The sponsor report is often a short summary derived from the tactical one. The team update is often a slice of the same source. You maintain the underlying picture once, and you publish it in the right shape for each audience.

### THE STRUCTURE THAT WORKS (5:30–7:15)

*[List slide: Headline · RAG · Workstreams · Asks · Risks · Upcoming]*

Let's build the tactical report — the weekly PM-and-stakeholders version — because the other two derive from it. It has six sections, and the order matters, because readers scan from the top.

One, the headline. One or two sentences summarizing where the project is. "Project on track for September milestone. Two workstreams showing amber due to vendor capacity; mitigations in progress." If the reader reads nothing else, they know the state.

Two, the overall RAG — that's R-A-G, red, amber, green. A single color, with one sentence of rationale. Don't get clever with yellow or purple. Three colors. Everyone knows them.

Three, workstream status. Each workstream gets a line or two: its color, what happened this period, what's upcoming. Brevity is a discipline here. If a workstream needs three paragraphs, something is wrong — with the workstream, or with the writing.

Four, key decisions and asks. What do you need from the readers? Decisions pending. Escalations that need sponsor attention. Inputs that are overdue. Be explicit, because vague asks get ignored.

Five, risks and issues. The three to five that matter most — not the full RAID log. The ones where attention would actually help.

Six, upcoming. What's coming in the next week or two: milestones, major meetings, deliverables.

That fits on one to two pages. Longer than that, and you're either reporting at too much detail or padding. Shorter, and you're probably underreporting.

### THE NARRATIVE VOICE (7:15–9:15)

*[Example slide: weak vs strong status writing.]*

Now the part that separates a report that gets read from one that gets skimmed: the voice. Status reports have their own register. It is neutral, specific, and unhedged. It tells the truth crisply.

Let me show you, because this is where most reports go soft. Watch the difference.

Weak: "We are navigating some challenges with vendor delivery." Strong: "The vendor has missed two deliverables in three weeks." Same situation. One of them tells the reader something. The other one is a fog machine. And experienced readers don't read euphemism as polish — they read it as something being hidden. Euphemism in a status report is distrust-inducing.

There are five named ways these reports fail, and I want you to be able to spot every one.

Euphemism — the fog machine we just saw. Say the plain thing.

False precision. "We are 67% complete on integration," when the underlying numbers are rolled up from soft estimates — that's a number pretending to be a fact. Say "integration is approximately two-thirds complete, based on team estimates." Honest softness beats false hardness.

Buried lede. The key signal is on page two, after four paragraphs of context. The lede goes first. A senior reader who reads only your first paragraph should leave with the correct picture — not a confidently wrong one.

Color washing. Everything is green, because going amber would mean admitting a problem. This is the most damaging failure mode, because it trains readers to stop believing the report. A status report that's been green for twelve weeks straight is not a well-run project. It's a badly reported one.

And the generic risk. "Resource availability remains a risk." That's true on every project that has ever existed. If that's the worst risk you can articulate, you haven't thought hard enough about your project.

So here's the test for every sentence you write: does this tell a reader something specific they did not already know? If not, it's probably filler — and it should come out.

---

### THE RAG RATING, AND THE COURAGE TO GO AMBER (9:15–11:30)

*[List slide: Green · Amber · Red definitions]*

The R-A-G rating is one of the most consequential decisions in the whole report, so let me give you the definitions I use.

Green means on track to meet your objectives with the current plan. Normal variability is absorbed by buffer or mitigation. No senior attention needed.

Amber means something is at risk. You may still meet your objectives, but there's identified exposure that warrants attention. Senior awareness is appropriate; senior action may or may not be.

Red means not on track to meet one or more objectives with the current plan. A material change is needed — scope, schedule, cost, or method. Senior action is required.

Those are mine. Your organization may use others. What matters is that you and your PM share a clear understanding of what each color means on your project.

And here's the move that separates the good coordinators from the rest: knowing when to go from green to amber. Because most projects drift on green far too long. The team feels going amber is admitting failure. The PM feels amber invites uncomfortable questions. The sponsor prefers green. Everyone — everyone — is incentivized to stay green slightly longer than they should.

Going amber early is almost always better than going amber late. Early amber means there's still time to act. Late amber — or worse, the direct jump from green straight to red — means the project has been flying blind for weeks, and the recovery options are fewer.

Now, you're usually not the person who sets the rating. The PM is. But you often draft the report, which means you produce the first rating. And you often have the clearest operational view, which means your judgment genuinely matters. So when you think it should be amber and the PM is reluctant, have the conversation directly. Something like: "I've put it at amber this week because of the integration slip and the open change request — what's your read?" Sometimes the PM has context you don't, and the green holds. Sometimes you've just given them the cover to go amber that they needed. Either way, the conversation is yours to start.

### PREPARING ONE EFFICIENTLY (11:30–12:45)

*[List slide: Monday · Wednesday · Thursday rhythm]*

One practical thing, because I don't want you losing a day to this. A weekly status report should take two to three hours, not a full day. If it's taking longer, the bottleneck is almost always information gathering, not writing.

Here's the rhythm that's served me for years. Monday, you send each workstream lead a tiny template: color this week — green, amber, or red — one sentence on what happened, one sentence on what's coming. Due end of Tuesday. Three sentences from each lead. That's your source material.

Wednesday morning, you consolidate. And you cross-check against your own knowledge — the schedule, the RAID log, the action tracker. Where an input disagrees with your operational awareness, you clarify with the lead before you write. This is the honesty step, and it's the one people skip.

Wednesday afternoon, draft. Use the six sections. Write the headline last, once you actually understand what the rest of the document is saying. Wednesday evening, review your own draft and cut twenty percent. Thursday morning, to the PM. Thursday afternoon, out the door.

One warning. If your reports are going out Friday afternoon, that's a red flag. It means production got compressed into a window where review is hard and the content hasn't been cross-checked — and your readers receive it at the lowest-attention moment of their week.

---

### AUDIT, DON'T REWRITE (12:45–13:30)

*[Statement slide.]*

One line I have to draw clearly. When you're asked to review a status report — your PM's draft, a junior coordinator's first attempt — your job is to surface what's wrong and propose the specific changes. Not to produce a version two and send it yourself.

The status report is the PM's document. The rating, the framing, the language — those are calls the PM owns. A coordinator who rewrites the report and sends it on the PM's behalf has stepped past their role, even when every single change was right. Audit. Recommend. Let the PM revise and send. It's the same line we drew for the schedule and the minutes — and the consequence of crossing it is higher here, on a document that goes to the sponsor under the PM's name.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:30–14:20)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Pull up your last four status reports and read them back-to-back. Do they read as four different reports, or as small variations on the same template? Check your R-A-G ratings over the last twelve weeks — count the greens, the ambers, the reds. Persistent green on any project lasting more than a few weeks is suspicious. Find the one workstream where your report's color and your operational gut disagree, and have that conversation this week. And for your next report, write the single headline sentence before you gather any inputs — then see whether the inputs confirm it or contradict it. Either answer teaches you something.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

The reason I started with that one-page report from my ninth year is that the whole lesson lives inside it. The previous reports were four pages and said nothing. Mine was one page and said the true thing, plainly. That's the entire craft — not length, not polish, but the courage to write down what's actually happening, in words a busy senior reader can act on. Learn to do that every week, and somewhere out of your sight, a sentence about you will start travelling senior-to-senior. Mine did.

See you in the workbook.
