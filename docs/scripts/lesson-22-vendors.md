# Lesson 22 — Working With Vendors, Contractors, and External Teams
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30
**Source chapter:** Handbook Ch 22; reading `docs/lessons/vendors.md`
**Lesson slug:** vendors
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1.

---

### COLD OPEN — The vendor PM who outlasted me (0:00–2:10)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I want to start with a man named Josh, because I almost lost one of the most valuable professional relationships of my life by mistaking his role for his character — and the whole lesson is hiding inside that mistake.

On a large systems integration in my mid-career, I spent nine months as the primary operational contact for a vendor PM named Josh. He was my counterpart on the vendor's side. I was nominally a senior coordinator moving toward a PM title; he was a mid-level PM at a global systems integrator.

Our relationship was adversarial for the first three months and professional for the next six. And here's the thing — it didn't start adversarial for any dramatic reason. It started that way because neither of us had been told, by our respective organizations, what the working model should be. So we both did the default thing. We operated as advocates for our own sides. Every commitment got negotiated. Every slip got contested. Every scope question became a point of tension.

Three months in, Josh asked if we could get coffee. Not a meeting — just coffee. We did. And he said something that reframed the entire relationship. He said, "I think you think I'm trying to get away with things. I'm not. I'm trying to deliver what I committed to, with the constraints my firm has given me. Most of what looks like me pushing back on you is me trying to protect my team from my own account management. If you and I could align on what we actually need from each other, I think we could both do our jobs better."

I was embarrassed. I had been treating him as an adversary. He had been trying to do his job — the same as me.

---

### WHAT CHANGED AFTER THE COFFEE (2:10–3:30)

*[Story slide.]*

After that coffee, we worked differently. We talked more openly about each other's constraints. We resolved issues between ourselves before they ever escalated to our respective leadership. When his firm tried to submit a change request that I thought was inflated, I told him directly, counterpart to counterpart, rather than routing it through some formal process. He revised it.

The project delivered. And a year later, Josh's firm won another piece of work at my client — and Josh asked for me personally to be on his side of the engagement. We have stayed in touch. He became, eventually, a senior partner at his firm.

I have thought many times about how close I came to missing all of that, by mistaking his role for his character. Vendor relationships are distinct from internal relationships, and they require their own craft. This lesson is about that craft.

---

### WHAT MAKES VENDORS DIFFERENT (3:30–5:10)

*[Statement slide: "Their job and your job are not the same job."]*

So let's start with the thing underneath everything. What actually makes a vendor different from a colleague?

A vendor is an external organization, contracted to deliver specific work. They have their own objectives, their own incentives, their own internal dynamics, and their own commercial pressures — and those differ, sometimes dramatically, from your organization's.

A vendor is accountable to their contract. The contract defines what they must deliver and what they'll be paid. And within that contract, they have reasonable latitude — to protect their margins, to manage their risk, to deliver efficiently. Your job, as the client-side coordinator, is to make sure they deliver what the contract requires. Their job is to deliver that while running a profitable engagement.

Sit with that for a second. These are not the same job. They overlap most of the time. But occasionally — and you have to be ready for it — they pull in different directions. That's not a sign something has gone wrong. That's the normal physics of the relationship.

---

### THE TWO POLES NEW COORDINATORS FALL INTO (5:10–6:30)

*[Statement slide: "Not an employee. Not an adversary. A partner."]*

New coordinators get this wrong at one of two poles, and you'll feel the pull of both.

The first pole is treating the vendor as an employee. You expect them to absorb scope. You expect them to extend effort beyond their contract because, well, we're all on the same team, aren't we? We're not. They have a contract, and a margin, and a firm behind them.

The second pole is treating the vendor as an adversary. You assume every pushback is an attempt to extract money. Every change request is a hustle. That was me, with Josh, for three months.

Both poles are wrong. The right posture is professional partnership. The vendor is a separate organization with legitimate interests that sometimes align with yours and sometimes don't — and the relationship is most productive when both parties are honest about exactly that. That's the whole reframe Josh gave me over coffee. Hold it for the rest of the lesson.

---

### THE THREE PHASES (6:30–8:30)

*[List slide: Selection & contracting · Execution · Closeout]*

A vendor engagement has three phases, and each one asks something different of you.

The first is selection and contracting. This is usually led by procurement, with input from the PM and sometimes the sponsor. Your role here is often limited. But if you are involved, know this: the shape of the contract sets the working relationship for the entire duration. A tight, detailed contract produces a transactional relationship — every request gets measured against scope. A loose, principle-based contract produces a flexible one — both sides extend themselves on spirit rather than letter. Neither is better. They fit different kinds of work.

The second phase is execution. This is where you live. Day-to-day coordination, status reporting, issue resolution. And this is where the relationship is actually built or damaged — not in any big moment, but in the small interactions. The coordinators who keep their commitments, communicate clearly, and handle disputes without drama are the ones whose vendors deliver better work.

The third phase is closeout. Formal acceptance of deliverables, reconciliation of invoices, resolution of any outstanding disputes, transition to operations or warranty. This phase is almost always underinvested. People are tired; they want to move on. But a clean closeout — explicit acceptance, clear documentation of what was delivered, a respectful conclusion — protects both parties and preserves the relationship for future work. Remember Josh's firm came back a year later. Closeout is where that becomes possible.

---

### SIX PRACTICES, PART ONE (8:30–10:10)

*[List slide: Be precise in writing · Separate contract from relationship · Respect their structure]*

Let me give you six practices that make this work. Three now, three in a moment.

One. Be precise in writing. Ambiguity in client-vendor communication is expensive — vague emails produce scope arguments later. When you send a vendor a request, be specific: what you need, when, in what form. And when they commit, get the commitment in writing, with enough specificity that there's no later reinterpretation. The S-O-W — the statement of work — is your friend here, but only if your day-to-day writing is as precise as it is.

Two. Separate the contract from the relationship. When a dispute arises, frame it as a contract question — "let's look at what the S-O-W says" — not a character question. That one move lets both sides disagree about substance without it becoming personal. It takes the heat out.

Three. Respect their internal structure. Vendors have their own PMs, account managers, delivery leads, executives. Do not casually bypass your primary contact to talk to someone else on their side. It undermines their internal operating model, and they will not forget it. If you genuinely need to engage above your counterpart, do it through them, or at the very least with their knowledge.

---

### SIX PRACTICES, PART TWO (10:10–11:50)

*[List slide: Pay on time · Hold them accountable, not punitively · Give credit]*

Four. Pay on time. Now, you're probably not in the payment chain directly — but you are very often the conduit for invoice processing. And this matters more than it looks. A vendor whose invoices get paid on time is a vendor whose goodwill is available to you. A vendor whose invoices are delayed without explanation is a vendor whose willingness to extend effort beyond the strict contract quietly erodes. You may be the only person who notices an invoice is stuck. Notice it.

Five. Hold them to the contract without being punitive. When a vendor misses a commitment, name it specifically. Ask for their view on what happened and how to prevent it. Then work on the corrective action together. Don't soften the fact of the miss — but don't escalate it theatrically either. Just handle it professionally. Vendors respect clients who hold them accountable without being hostile. That's the needle.

Six. Give credit. When the vendor's team does good work, say so. A short email to their PM, praising a specific person by name, costs you nothing and creates enormous goodwill. The vendor PM forwards it. The person remembers. And the whole engagement runs a little smoother for it. It's the cheapest investment you'll ever make.

---

### WHEN VENDORS FAIL (11:50–13:10)

*[List slide: Private · Formal · Last resort — and document throughout]*

Now the hard part. Occasionally a vendor underperforms materially. A deliverable isn't what was specified. A commitment is missed, repeatedly. The quality is below standard. Your role — always in collaboration with your PM — is to manage that without destroying the relationship, unless destroying it becomes genuinely necessary.

The escalation path is staged, and the order matters. First, private. A direct conversation between the client PM and the vendor PM — name the issue, understand the cause, agree on corrective action. A surprising number of problems resolve right here, if you surface them early.

If private doesn't produce change, then formal. A written escalation to the vendor's leadership, referencing contract terms, specifying the gap, requesting a remediation plan. This signals the client is prepared to take the matter seriously. It's used when private resolution has been tried and hasn't worked — not before.

And only as a last resort: legal, or formal breach proceedings. That's handled by the PM, legal, and procurement — not by you. Your job throughout all of this is to document carefully. The specific missed commitments. The communications. The attempts at resolution. So that if it does escalate, the record is clean and it's already there. Most vendor issues never reach breach. They resolve in that first private conversation — and the coordinator who keeps the operational relationship professional is the one who gives the PM room to handle the hard moments well.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:10–14:20)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Identify your primary vendor counterpart on your current project. Send them a short, professional email this week — just confirming a next step, or summarizing a recent decision. Then notice something: is your pattern with them transactional, or collaborative? If it's transactional, try to warm it, just slightly, on the next interaction. And if you have a disputed item sitting open with a vendor, ask yourself honestly whether it's been escalated appropriately — or whether it's just been drifting in contested-but-unresolved status, which damages both sides.

*[Back on camera, slower.]*

The reason I started with Josh is that he's the whole lesson in miniature. For three months I treated his role as his character — I assumed that because his interests sometimes differed from mine, he was working against me. He wasn't. He was doing his job inside his constraints, the same as I was doing mine. The skill in this whole chapter is firm, precise, proportionate, and on the record — never the adversary's nuclear escalation, never the pushover's friendly capitulation. So here's the last question, and it's the one that tells you everything: would you, given the chance, want to work with this vendor again? Your answer tells you something true about the relationship — and about how you're handling it.

See you in the workbook.
