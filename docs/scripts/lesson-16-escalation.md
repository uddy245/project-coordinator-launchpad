# Lesson 16 — When to Escalate (and How Not to Burn Bridges)
## Video script (14 minutes)

**Target runtime:** 13:30–14:30

**Lesson slug:** escalation
**Narrator style:** first-person senior PM, warm but direct, no PM jargon unless it is being explicitly taught. Same voice as Lesson 1 and Lesson 20.

---

### COLD OPEN — The escalation I got wrong twice (0:00–2:20)

*[Story slide. Direct address.]*

I have escalated badly more often than I care to admit. The two worst examples still teach me things, and I want to start with both of them, because the whole lesson lives in the gap between them.

The first was early, maybe year two. A stakeholder from a business unit was consistently late with his input, and each delay pushed a delivery date. I raised it to my PM three separate times, increasingly forcefully. Each time, patiently, he said he would handle it. I didn't believe him — I saw no evidence anything had changed. So on the fourth miss, frustrated, I wrote a short email to the PMO director, copying my PM, saying that "repeated delays are affecting the project and we need to explore options for escalating." It went out Friday at four p.m.

Monday morning, my PM pulled me into a room. He wasn't furious. He was disappointed, which was worse. He told me two things. First, he had been working the issue with the stakeholder's own manager for three weeks, and was making progress I simply couldn't see from where I sat. Second, by going over his head, I had implicitly told him I didn't trust his judgment. We patched it up, mostly. But that relationship never fully recovered, because he no longer knew whether I'd loop him in or bypass him.

The second was years later, year six. A vendor was making promises in their progress reports that didn't match what their team was actually delivering. I'd seen the pattern for five weeks. I raised it once to the PM, who said it was on her radar. And this time I chose not to push further. I thought I was being disciplined. I was actually being passive. The situation compounded, and when it finally surfaced, it was much worse than it needed to be. The PM later told me she'd been waiting for someone — anyone — to insist on action. She'd been overloaded, and the item had been quietly drifting on her list. If I'd pushed harder in week three, she'd have been glad of it.

One escalation that hurt my credibility. One failure to escalate that let a fixable problem grow. That's the whole problem in two stories.

---

### WHY THIS IS HARD (2:20–3:30)

*[Slide: "the failure modes run in both directions"]*

Here's why escalation judgment is genuinely hard, and why it trips up coordinators for years. The failure modes run in both directions. You can escalate when you shouldn't, and burn a bridge. You can fail to escalate when you should, and let something fixable grow into something that isn't. And the right answer is specific to the situation — there is no rule you can memorize that gets you out of having to think.

So this lesson isn't a rule. It's a set of tests, a ladder, and a packet — three tools that turn a gut feeling into a decision you can defend. Let me give you all three.

---

### WHAT ESCALATION ACTUALLY IS (3:30–4:45)

*[Slide: "the definition is neutral; the act is loaded"]*

First, what escalation actually is. Escalation is raising an issue to a higher level of authority than the one currently handling it. That's it. The definition is neutral. The act is loaded.

At its best, escalation is a disciplined use of the organisation's decision hierarchy. The current level can't resolve something in time. The right higher level gets engaged, with the information it needs, and empowered to act. The situation improves. That's the clean version.

At its worst, escalation is a political move. Someone goes around a peer or a manager to get a different answer. Trust gets damaged. The underlying issue may or may not get resolved — but the relational cost lingers for months.

And here's the part that surprises people. The difference between those two is usually in how the escalation is handled, not whether it should have happened. A clean escalation — one that keeps the right people informed, follows the right channels, and is proportionate — rarely damages relationships. The exact same escalation, done badly, can.

---

### THE THREE TESTS (4:45–6:50)

*[List slide: Appropriateness · Channels · Proportion]*

So before you escalate anything, run three tests.

The appropriateness test. Does this actually require a higher authority — or can the current level still resolve it with more time, more information, or more pressure? If the current level hasn't really been given the chance to act, escalation is premature. If it's been trying and genuinely can't get traction, escalation may be warranted. That's the test I failed in reverse in year six. The current level wasn't going to resolve it, and I sat on my hands.

The channels test. Does the person you're thinking of escalating to even know about the situation — and are you going through the right path? Escalation that bypasses the natural chain — going to a sponsor without the PM's knowledge, going to the PMO without involving the PM — is almost always a mistake. Escalation that follows the chain — raising it to the PM, who then engages the sponsor with your support — usually isn't. That's the test my Friday-afternoon email failed.

The proportion test. Is the seriousness of your concern matched to the level you're escalating to? Take a minor issue to a senior leader and you squander the relationship and get dismissed. Take a major issue only to a peer and you let it fester. Match the weight of the issue to the height of the rung.

Most bad escalations fail at least one of these three. Run all three, honestly, before you do anything.

---

### THE ESCALATION LADDER (6:50–8:40)

*[List slide: the five rungs]*

Now, the ladder. In most projects there's an implicit ladder of escalation, and it has five rungs.

Rung one. A direct conversation with the person involved. If someone's missing deadlines, the first move is to talk to them and understand what's actually happening. A surprising amount resolves right here, and never needs to go any higher.

Rung two. Their manager or workstream lead. If the person can't change the situation on their own — they're capacity-constrained, they need help from above — you engage their leadership. And usually that happens through their manager, not through your chain.

Rung three. Your PM. If the issue is affecting the project materially, your PM is the next rung. The PM may take it further, or may resolve it on the spot.

Rung four. The sponsor or steering committee. For issues that touch objectives, budget, timelines, or cross-functional trade-offs.

Rung five. Formal paths — PMO, legal, compliance. For specific categories of issue that have formal routes.

And here's the discipline that holds the whole thing together. Use the lowest rung that will work. Escalation is not a race to the top. It's a search for the level at which the issue can actually be resolved. My year-two mistake was jumping from rung three straight to a formal path, while rung two was quietly working.

---

### THE INFORMATION PACKET (8:40–10:40)

*[List slide: Situation · Impact · Tried · Asked · Options]*

When you do escalate — or when you help your PM escalate — the quality of it depends almost entirely on the packet you bring. A vague escalation produces a vague response. A well-structured escalation produces focused action. Keep it to five short paragraphs. Half a page.

The situation. What's happening, factually, with dates and specifics. Not "the vendor is being difficult" — rather, "the vendor has missed three of the last four weekly deliverables; the items and dates are X, Y, and Z."

The impact. What this means for the project. "The integration workstream is one week behind pace; if this continues two more weeks, the September milestone is at risk."

What has been tried. The actions already taken at lower levels. This is the part that proves escalation is appropriate — that the lower rungs have been worked and aren't enough.

What is being asked. The specific thing the recipient is being asked to do or decide. Not "we need help" — rather, "we need your intervention with the vendor's executive sponsor to secure a staffing commitment by Friday."

And what the options are. The paths forward, with their trade-offs. "Accept the slip and renegotiate the milestone; engage the backup vendor at additional cost; descope a feature to reduce the integration burden."

If you can't describe the situation in half a page, you probably need to think about it more before you escalate at all.

---

### THE TONE (10:40–12:10)

*[Example slide: bad escalation message, then the rewrite]*

Now the tone, because the same facts land completely differently depending on how you write them. Escalation communications need to be crisp, but not dramatic. The reader is senior and busy. They want the facts, not the emotional context.

So avoid these words: "catastrophic," "disaster," "unacceptable," "crisis," "failing." They raise the temperature without adding any information. Use these instead: "at risk," "behind plan," "material exposure," "needs intervention." They convey seriousness without theatrics.

And avoid blame aimed at individuals, unless it's genuinely necessary and accurate. "The vendor is not performing" is fine. "The vendor PM is incompetent" is unprofessional, and often just wrong — that person may be performing perfectly well within constraints you can't see.

Here's the single test that governs all of it. Would you be comfortable if this exact text were forwarded to the person being discussed? Because escalations get forwarded all the time. Write every one as if that forward is certain.

---

### WHAT HAPPENS AFTER (12:10–13:15)

*[Statement slide]*

A word on what happens after, because the work doesn't end when you hit send. An escalation that works produces action — a decision, resources unblocked, a conversation that should have happened earlier. An escalation that doesn't work leaves you with harder choices: escalate further, accept it and manage within it, or honestly revisit whether the issue is as serious as you thought.

What you do not do is keep raising the same issue, to the same level, hoping for a different answer. If one escalation didn't work, the next one needs to go higher, bring new information, or take a different form. Raising the same item over and over, with no new content, just makes you look obsessive.

And always close it out. Confirm what was decided. Thank the person who helped. Update the RAID log and the status report to reflect the new state. Escalations that never get closed out in the record become confusing artefacts that haunt the project later.

One last nuance before your work. Escalation across — engaging a peer's manager in a different reporting line — is more sensitive than escalation up your own line, because to them it can feel like a complaint even when it isn't. The move there is almost always through your PM, not directly. And never surprise your PM with an email that went out that they didn't know was coming.

---

### WHAT YOU'RE GOING TO DO (13:15–14:20)

*[Slide: workbook brief + close]*

Here's your work for this lesson. Pick one issue on your current project that's been drifting at the wrong level — something you raised that hasn't resolved, or something you haven't raised but should have. Run the three tests on it honestly: appropriateness, channels, proportion. Be honest about whether you're over-escalating or under-escalating. Then, if escalation is warranted, draft the information packet — five paragraphs, half a page — and bring it to your PM. Do not send it past them.

*[Back on camera, slower]*

I told you about both of my worst escalations on purpose. The Friday email that cost me my PM's trust, and the five weeks of silence that let a fixable problem grow. One was too loud. One was too quiet. Neither was a failure of skill — I knew how to write an email both times. They were failures of judgment about when, and how, and to whom. That judgment is the entire craft of this lesson, and you build it one issue at a time. Next time something starts to drift, you'll have three tests, a ladder, and a packet. Use them before your gut talks you into a Friday-afternoon email.

See you in the workbook.
