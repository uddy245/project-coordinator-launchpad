# Status Reports — Three Audiences, Three Reports

## The status report that got me promoted

A single status report, written in year nine, was the most visible moment in one coordinator's progression toward a PM role.

It was not a dramatic report. A large systems integration program was in trouble — not catastrophically, but accumulating in ways that the weekly reports had been soft-pedaling. The PM was under pressure. The sponsor was losing confidence. The PMO was asking hard questions that the previous reports had not really answered.

The coordinator was asked on a Thursday afternoon to draft the next weekly report. The PM was traveling and would approve remotely.

The draft was different from what the team had been sending. It opened with three sentences that stated plainly where the program was: amber overall, two workstreams at risk of slipping the September milestone, cost tracking within baseline but with pressure building. A short summary followed: what had slipped and why — no euphemisms, no *"challenges,"* no *"opportunities."* Then the decisions needed from the sponsor that week, with options and a recommended path. The report finished with three risks that had emerged in the last fortnight and how each was being managed.

The report was about a page. The previous reports had been four.

Sent to the PM with a note: *Here's a draft. More direct than usual. Happy to tone down if it's too much.* The PM wrote back within two hours: *Don't tone it down. This is what the program needs. Send it.*

It was sent. The sponsor responded the next morning with specific questions, specific decisions, and a direct call with the PM. Within a week the program had a recovery plan, a renegotiated milestone, and better sponsor engagement than it had had in two months. The sponsor told the PM: *"I feel like I finally understand what's happening on this program."*

Two things followed. The program recovered. And the PM told the next sponsor, and the one after that, that the coordinator wrote the best status reports he had ever read. That sentence — passed senior-to-senior — shaped how the coordinator was seen for the next three years. It eventually helped them step up to PM.

**Status reports are the most consistently visible written product of your role.** They reach people you will never meet. They form impressions of the project and of you that accumulate week over week. Done well, they are one of the highest-leverage things you can produce. Done poorly, they are expensive noise.

## Three audiences, three reports

The first thing to understand: *"the status report"* is usually not one document for one audience. It is the same underlying information, cut for three different audiences with three different needs.

| Audience | What they need | Length | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Delivery team** | Operational detail — who is doing what, what is blocked, what is coming, what slipped | Short, data-dense, operational | At least weekly, often more |
| **PM and immediate stakeholders** | Tactical awareness — current state, what changed, what needs attention in two weeks, active risks | 1–2 pages | Weekly |
| **Sponsor and steering** | Strategic situational awareness — are we on track, major risks, decisions needed from them | One page or one slide | Biweekly or monthly + steering pack |

**Most coordinators produce one report for all three audiences, which is almost always wrong.** Operational detail overwhelms the sponsor. The sponsor-level summary is too thin for the team. The tactical report is the right length but the wrong register for both ends.

The fix is not three documents from scratch. **It is one body of source material — the current facts of the project — packaged three ways.** The sponsor report is often a short summary derived from the tactical report. The team update is often a slice of the same source. Maintain the underlying picture once; publish it in the right shape for each audience.

## The structure that works

A tactical status report — the weekly PM-and-immediate-stakeholders version — has six sections, in this order, because **readers scan from the top**.

1. **Headline.** One or two sentences summarising where the project is. *"Project on track for September milestone. Two workstreams showing amber due to vendor capacity; mitigations in progress."* If the reader reads nothing else, they know the state.
2. **Overall RAG.** A single colour — green, amber, red — with a sentence of rationale. Three colours. Everyone knows them.
3. **Workstream status.** Each workstream gets a line or two: RAG, what happened, what's upcoming. Brevity is a discipline. If a workstream needs three paragraphs, something is wrong with the workstream or with the writing.
4. **Key decisions and asks.** What does the PM (or the report's author) need from the readers? Decisions pending. Escalations that need sponsor attention. Inputs overdue. **Vague asks get ignored.**
5. **Risks and issues.** The three to five items that matter most. Not the full RAID log. The ones where attention would be useful.
6. **Upcoming.** What is coming in the next week or two — milestones, major meetings, deliverables.

This fits on one to two pages. Longer and you are reporting at too much detail or padding. Shorter and you are probably underreporting.

## The narrative voice

Status reports have their own register: neutral, specific, unhedged. They tell the truth crisply.

The failures are five named patterns:

**Euphemism.** *"We are navigating some challenges with vendor delivery"* means *"the vendor has missed two deliverables in three weeks."* Say the latter. **Euphemism in status reports is read as distrust-inducing by experienced readers.**

**False precision.** *"We are 67% complete on integration"* — when the underlying numbers are rolled up from soft estimates — is false precision. Say *"integration is approximately two-thirds complete based on team estimates"* if precision is soft. The hours-vs-completion cross-check from Chapter 10 applies here too.

**Buried lede.** The key signal is on page two after four paragraphs of context. The headline goes first. A senior reader who reads only the first paragraph should leave with the correct picture, not a confidently wrong one.

**Colour washing.** Everything is green because going to amber would be admitting a problem. **This is the most damaging failure mode** because it trains readers to not believe the report. A status report that is green for twelve weeks in a row is not a well-run project; it is a badly reported one.

**The generic risk.** *"Resource availability remains a risk"* is a risk that is always true on every project. If that is the worst risk you can articulate, you have not thought hard enough about your project. Generic risk is the **least useful sentence** in any status report.

The test for every sentence in a status report is: **does this tell a reader something specific they did not already know?** If not, it is probably filler.

## The RAG rating, and the courage to go amber

The RAG rating is one of the most consequential decisions in a status report.

| Rating | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Green** | On track to meet objectives with the current plan. Normal variability absorbed by buffer or mitigation. No senior attention needed. |
| **Amber** | Something is at risk. May still meet objectives, but identified exposure warrants attention. Senior awareness appropriate; senior action may or may not be. |
| **Red** | Not on track to meet one or more objectives with the current plan. A material change is needed — scope, schedule, cost, or method. Senior action required. |

Your organisation may use different definitions. What matters is that you and your PM share a clear understanding of what each colour means on your project.

**The courage move is knowing when to go from green to amber.** Most projects drift too long on green. The team feels going amber is admitting failure. The PM feels amber invites uncomfortable questions. The sponsor prefers green. Everyone is incentivised to stay green slightly longer than they should.

**Going amber early is almost always better than going amber late.** Early amber means there is time to act. Late amber — or the direct jump from green to red — means the project has been flying blind and recovery options are fewer.

As coordinator, you are usually not the person who sets the rating. The PM is. But you often draft the report, which means you produce the first rating, and you often have the clearest operational view, which means your judgment matters. When you think the project should be amber and the PM is reluctant, **have the conversation directly:**

> *"I've put it at amber this week because of the integration slip and the open CR — what's your read?"*

Sometimes the PM has context you don't and the green holds. Sometimes you give them the cover to go amber that they needed. Either way, the conversation is yours to start.

## Preparing a status report efficiently

A weekly status report should take two to three hours, not a full day. If it is taking longer, the bottleneck is usually information gathering, not writing.

A workflow that works:

- **Monday morning.** Send a short template to each workstream lead: *RAG this week (G/A/R), one sentence on what happened, one sentence on what's coming.* Due end of Tuesday. Three sentences from each lead.
- **Wednesday morning.** Consolidate inputs. Cross-check against your own knowledge — the schedule, the RAID log, the action tracker. Where inputs disagree with operational awareness, clarify with the workstream lead before writing. **This is the honesty step.**
- **Wednesday afternoon.** Draft the report. Use the six-section structure. Write the headline last, once you understand what the rest of the document is saying. Keep it tight.
- **Wednesday evening.** Review your own draft. Cut twenty percent. Sharpen the risk and ask sections.
- **Thursday morning.** Send to PM for review. Turnaround is usually a few hours. Incorporate changes. Send out Thursday afternoon.

This rhythm — inputs Monday, consolidation Tuesday-Wednesday, draft Wednesday, review Thursday, send Thursday — is manageable within a normal week and produces reports that are current enough to be useful.

**Friday-afternoon sends are a warning sign.** They mean the report's production has been compressed into a window where review is hard and the content has not had time to be cross-checked. They also mean readers receive the report at the lowest-attention moment of their week.

## Audit, don't rewrite

When you are asked to review a status report — your PM's draft, a junior coordinator's first attempt — your job is to **surface what is wrong and propose the specific changes**, not to produce a v2 and send it yourself. Status reports are the PM's document; the RAG, the framing, the language are all calls the PM owns. A coordinator who rewrites the status report and sends it on the PM's behalf has stepped past their role even when every change is right. **Audit. Recommend. Let the PM revise and send.** The audit-vs-rewrite line is the same as it was for the WBS, the schedule, the meeting setup, and the minutes — and the consequence of crossing it is higher on a document that goes to the sponsor under the PM's name.

## Tomorrow, specifically

1. **Pull up your last four status reports.** Read them back-to-back. Do they read as different reports, or as small variations on the same template? If the latter, what has actually changed in the project over that month?
2. **Check your RAG ratings over the last twelve weeks.** How many greens, how many ambers, how many reds? **Persistent green on any project lasting more than a few weeks is suspicious.**
3. **Identify the single workstream where your report's RAG and your operational gut disagree.** Have a conversation with the workstream lead this week.
4. **For your next report, try the three-audience framing.** Produce the tactical report, then derive a five-line sponsor summary from it. See how different they look.
5. **Write one headline sentence for your next report *before* you gather inputs.** See whether the inputs confirm or contradict it. Either answer is useful — if they confirm, you have crystallised the picture early; if they contradict, you have just learned something the inputs needed to tell you.
