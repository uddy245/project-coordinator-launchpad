# When to Escalate (and How Not to Burn Bridges)

## The escalation I got wrong twice

The two worst escalations of my career still teach me things.

The first was early. A business-unit stakeholder was consistently late with input, and each delay pushed a delivery date. I raised it to my PM three times, increasingly forcefully. Each time he said he would handle it. I saw no evidence anything changed. On the fourth miss, frustrated, I emailed the PMO director — cc'ing my PM — that "repeated delays are affecting the project and we need to explore options for escalating." It went out Friday at 4 p.m.

Monday, my PM pulled me into a room. He was not furious; he was disappointed, which was worse. He told me two things. He had been working the issue with the stakeholder's own manager for three weeks and was making progress I could not see from where I sat. And by going over his head, I had implicitly said I did not trust his judgment. **My credibility with him never fully recovered, because he no longer knew whether I would loop him in or bypass him.**

The second was years later. A vendor was making promises in progress reports that did not match what their team was delivering. I had seen the pattern for five weeks. I raised it once to the PM, who said it was on her radar. I chose — this time — not to push further. I thought I was being disciplined. **I was actually being passive.** The situation compounded, and when it surfaced it was much worse than it needed to be. The PM later told me she had been waiting for someone to insist on action; she had been overloaded and the item had been drifting on her list. If I had pushed harder in week three, she would have been glad of it.

One escalation that hurt my credibility. One failure to escalate that let a fixable problem grow. **Escalation judgment is hard because the failure modes run in both directions, and the right answer is specific to the situation.**

## What escalation actually is

Escalation is raising an issue to a higher level of authority than the one currently handling it. The definition is neutral; the act is loaded.

At its best, escalation is a disciplined use of the organisation's decision hierarchy: the current level cannot resolve something in time, the right higher level is engaged with the information it needs, and the situation improves. At its worst, escalation is a political move — someone goes around a peer or a manager to get a different answer, trust is damaged, and the relational cost lingers.

**The difference between the two is usually in how the escalation is handled, not whether it should have happened.** A clean escalation — one that keeps the right people informed, follows the right channels, and is proportionate — rarely damages relationships. The same escalation done badly can.

## The three tests before you escalate

Before you escalate anything, apply three tests.

**The appropriateness test.** Does this actually require a higher authority, or can the current level resolve it with more time, information, or pressure? If the current level has not been given the chance to act, escalation is premature. If it has been trying and cannot get traction, escalation may be warranted.

**The channels test.** Does the person you are thinking of escalating to know about the situation, and are you going through the right path? Escalation that bypasses the natural chain — going to a sponsor without the PM's knowledge — is usually a mistake. Escalation that follows the chain — raising to the PM, who then engages the sponsor with your support — usually is not.

**The proportion test.** Is the seriousness of your concern matched to the level you are escalating to? Taking a minor issue to a senior leader squanders the relationship; taking a major issue only to a peer lets it fester.

**Most bad escalations fail at least one of these tests.** My year-two email failed the channels test. My year-six silence failed the appropriateness test in reverse — I was under-escalating.

## The escalation ladder

In most projects there is an implicit ladder.

1. **Direct conversation with the person involved.** The first rung. If someone is missing deadlines, talk to them and understand what is happening. Often the issue resolves here.
2. **Their manager or workstream lead.** If the person cannot change the situation alone, engage their leadership — usually through their manager, not your chain.
3. **Your PM.** If the issue is affecting the project materially, your PM is the next rung. The PM may take it further or resolve it.
4. **The sponsor or steering committee.** For issues affecting objectives, budget, timelines, or cross-functional trade-offs.
5. **Formal paths — PMO, legal, compliance.** For specific categories of issue with formal routes.

**The discipline is to use the lowest rung that will work. Escalation is not a race to the top; it is a search for the level at which the issue can actually be resolved.**

## The information packet

When you escalate — or when you help your PM escalate — the quality of the escalation depends on the packet you bring. A vague escalation produces a vague response. **A well-structured escalation produces focused action.** Keep it to five short paragraphs, half a page:

- **The situation.** What is happening, factually, with dates and specifics. Not "the vendor is being difficult"; rather "the vendor has missed three of the last four weekly deliverables; the items and dates are X, Y, Z."
- **The impact.** What this means for the project. "The integration workstream is one week behind pace; if this continues two more weeks, the September milestone is at risk."
- **What has been tried.** The actions already taken at lower levels — this is what makes escalation appropriate.
- **What is being asked.** The specific thing the recipient is asked to do or decide. Not "we need help" but "we need your intervention with the vendor's executive sponsor to secure a staffing commitment by Friday."
- **What the options are.** The paths forward with their trade-offs. "Accept the slip and renegotiate the milestone; engage the backup vendor at additional cost; descope a feature to reduce integration burden."

**If you cannot describe the situation in half a page, you probably need to think about it more before escalating.**

## The tone

Escalation communications need to be crisp but not dramatic. The reader is usually senior and busy; they want the facts, not the emotional context.

**Avoid:** "catastrophic," "disaster," "unacceptable," "crisis," "failing." These raise temperature without adding information. **Use:** "at risk," "behind plan," "material exposure," "needs intervention."

**Avoid blame aimed at individuals** unless it is necessary and accurate. "The vendor is not performing" is fine. "The vendor PM is incompetent" is unprofessional and often wrong.

**The test is: would you be comfortable if this exact text were forwarded to the person being discussed?** Escalations frequently get forwarded. Write as if that is certain.

## What happens after

An escalation that works produces action — a decision, resources unblocked, a conversation that should have happened earlier. An escalation that does not work leaves harder choices: escalate further, accept and manage within it, or revisit whether the issue is as serious as you thought.

**What you do not do is keep escalating the same issue repeatedly to the same level in the hope of a different response.** If one escalation did not work, the next needs to go higher, bring new information, or take a different form. Repeatedly raising the same item without new content makes you look obsessive.

And always, after an escalation, follow up. Confirm what was decided. Thank the person who helped. **Update the RAID log and the status report to reflect the new state.** Escalations that are not closed out in the record become confusing artefacts later.

## Escalation up versus across

One nuance. Escalation *across* — engaging a peer's manager in a different reporting line — is more sensitive than escalation up your own line. Going across someone's hierarchy can feel like a complaint, even when it is not intended that way.

**The move here is almost always through your PM, not directly.** Your PM engages the peer's manager, and you stay out of that conversation. When you do need to communicate across hierarchies directly, keep your PM informed — **never surprise them with an email that went out that they did not know was coming.**

The exceptions are rare: situations where your PM is unavailable, has chosen not to act, or is part of the problem. These are hard, and there is no clean rule. Consult a mentor if you find yourself in one.

## Tomorrow, specifically

1. **Pick one issue on your current project that has been drifting at the wrong level** — something you raised that has not resolved, or something you have not raised but should have.
2. **Apply the three tests.** Appropriateness, channels, proportion. Be honest about whether you are over- or under-escalating.
3. **If escalation is warranted, draft the information packet** — five paragraphs, half a page — and bring it to your PM. Do not send it past them.
4. **Review the last escalation on your project** (yours or the PM's). Was it clean or messy? What would you change?
5. **Watch how your PM handles issues that arrive** over the next week — who they involve, in what order, with what timing. That is where the judgment is learned.
