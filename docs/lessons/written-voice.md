# The Written Voice of a Professional

## Why this matters more than you think

A coordinator at a mid-sized firm writes between fifty and a hundred separate pieces of prose a week — emails, Slack messages, minutes, status narratives, risk descriptions, change requests, briefings. Almost all of them are read by someone else. A meaningful fraction are read by people senior to you, or people you have never met, or people whose impression of you will be formed entirely by what you have written.

Your written voice is the most visible surface of your professional self. Decisions about you are made on its basis — small cumulative ones, made in the three seconds after an email lands. Whether someone reads through or skims. Whether they think of you as someone to lean on or someone to route around.

You cannot easily out-work bad writing. You can, with effort, learn to write well enough that your prose works *for* you.

## The four commitments

Professional prose is writing that respects the reader's time, states what it needs to state clearly, takes responsibility for its own claims, and doesn't ask the reader to do interpretive work the writer should have done first.

- **Respect the reader's time** — write as short as you can while still being clear. No padding. No throat-clearing.
- **Clear statement** — what you're saying, asking, and want done is unambiguous.
- **Responsibility for your claims** — say things you believe, without hedging. If you're uncertain, name the uncertainty with precision.
- **Don't ask the reader to interpret** — you've already done the structuring. The reader who stops after sentence one still gets the most important thing.

## Lead with the lede

Most unstudied writers bury the lede. They start with context, then more context, and three paragraphs in, finally arrive at the point. This is how we are taught to write in school. It is the wrong model for professional writing.

Professional writing is inverted. The lede is first.

**Buried lede (25 seconds to read):**

> Hi Helen, hope your morning's going well. Just wanted to let you know about a couple of things. So I spoke to the vendor and they're saying they can't get the integration plan to us until Wednesday, which I'm a bit worried about because Raj was hoping to start the testing prep this week. I've let Raj know.

**Lede first (10 seconds to read):**

> Helen — vendor has slipped the integration plan from today to next Wednesday. This affects testing, which was planning to start dependent work Tuesday. I haven't spoken to Raj yet — want me to, or will you?

The terser version is not less polite. It is the result of more work — the writer decided what mattered before they started writing.

A subject line is the *first* lede. *Update*, *Quick question*, *Following up* are not subject lines. They are labels for the fact that an email exists. Try instead: *Vendor delivery slipped — needs decision on testing start.*

## Strip the hedges

A hedge is a word or phrase that distances you from what you are saying. *I think. I believe. Perhaps. Maybe. Just. It seems. I might be wrong but. Just wondering. If that makes sense.*

Each has a use. The problem is junior writers use them in clusters. Each additional hedge compounds the weakening effect.

> I was just wondering if maybe we should perhaps think about moving the design review to next week? I might be wrong but it seems like a few of the key people might not be available. Just a thought though.

Nine hedges. Replace with:

> Suggest we move the design review to next week — three of the key reviewers are out this week, and the extra time lets us circulate the pre-read properly. OK with you?

Same information. The writer has taken a position.

## Stop apologising reflexively

*Sorry to bother you. Sorry for the delay. Sorry to keep chasing.* Each is sometimes appropriate. None should be automatic.

Used automatically, they do three bad things:

1. **They inflate.** If every interaction starts with sorry, you have nothing left for the moments that warrant one.
2. **They mis-signal.** Apologising for chasing frames the normal as abnormal.
3. **They undermine authority.** You cannot apologise your way into being taken seriously.

Apologise when you have done something that warrants it. *Sorry, I got the numbers wrong in yesterday's status — here's the corrected version.* That apology lands because it is doing work.

Don't apologise for: doing your job, asking questions, following up, taking space, existing.

## Match the register to the audience

| Register | Audience | Sound |
|---|---|---|
| **Conversational** | Peers, your PM in routine interactions | Contractions, short sentences, occasional humour |
| **Standard** | Most cross-team writing | Polite, neutral, efficient. Full sentences. No jokes. |
| **Formal** | Sponsors, external readers, SC minutes | Spare, precise, free of anything casual |

The error new coordinators make is using the wrong register for the audience. They write to a senior director in the conversational tone they use with their PM. Or they write to their PM in the formal tone appropriate for a sponsor.

A specific rule: when replying, *match or slightly elevate* the register of the incoming message. Dropping register reads as presumption.

## Specific forms

**Minutes** are not transcripts.

> *Bad:* John said he thought we should go with Option A. Priya said she wasn't sure. Helen asked if there were other considerations. After some discussion, the team seemed to agree that Option A was the way forward.

> *Good:* **Decision:** proceed with Option A. **Action:** Priya to complete additional analysis on Option A by May 24 and flag any concerns by end of that week. If no concerns raised, Option A is confirmed.

**Risk descriptions** carry weight when they are specific.

> *Bad:* Risk that vendor may be late.

> *Good:* Vendor capacity constraints may delay the integration plan beyond the May 28 deadline, affecting the testing workstream's ability to start dependent work on June 1. Currently rated Medium impact / Medium likelihood. Mitigation: weekly check-ins with vendor lead; fallback plan to deprioritise non-critical testing scope if delay exceeds one week.

## Delivering bad news

The instinct is to soften — bury the bad news, lead with positives, use the passive voice, hedge severity. This is wrong. Bad news is worse when buried; the reader has to work harder, and then feels manipulated when they realise they were being cushioned.

Four sentences:

1. The bad news, plainly stated.
2. The minimal context needed to understand it.
3. What is being done.
4. What, if anything, you need from the reader.

> David — integration delivery has slipped from end of May to third week of June. Vendor capacity issue, not a scope change; we've been tracking it for two weeks. Helen is working through the downstream impact and will bring options to Thursday's SC. Let us know if you need an interim briefing before then.

Four sentences. Direct. Honest. The matter is not being hidden.

## Saying no

Three bad versions:

- **The apologetic overrun** — four sentences of apology, four of context, then eventually no.
- **The passive dodge** — *let me see what I can do* — and hope they forget. Worse than a real no.
- **The brittle no** — curt, no reason, no alternative. Reader feels dismissed.

The good version has three elements:

- A clear no, stated in the first sentence.
- A brief reason — one sentence.
- An alternative or next step.

> Can't take this on this week — finalising the SC pack and the CR. Could pick it up Monday if that works, or happy to point you to someone with capacity sooner.

Saying no upward — to your PM or someone senior — uses a different pattern: *say yes to the intent, renegotiate the specifics.*

> Let me pull something together — tight timeline given the status report is also due today. I can get you a first draft by 5, and a revised version by tomorrow noon. Workable?

## The editing pass

Before you send anything of consequence, read it once asking: *what can I cut?*

Almost everything you write can be twenty percent shorter without losing anything. Look for: hedges not carrying information; throat-clearing openings; padding phrases (*at this point in time* → *now*; *in order to* → *to*); repetitions; context the reader already has.

The pass takes ninety seconds for a short email. It is the highest-leverage habit in professional writing.

## A note on AI

AI tools write in a particular register — polite, slightly padded, reliably competent, somewhat generic. The problem isn't that it's bad. The problem is that it's *obvious*, and identical across ten million users.

The fix is the editing pass. Use AI to get to a draft faster. Then edit the draft until it sounds like a person wrote it. Strip the padding. Cut the hedges the model inserted. Add a phrase or two that is specifically, recognisably you.

The professional editorial judgment is yours. That is what does not get delegated.
