# Working With Vendors, Contractors, and External Teams

Vendor relationships are distinct from internal ones and have their own craft. A vendor is an external organisation contracted to deliver specific work — with their own objectives, incentives, internal dynamics, and commercial pressures, which differ (sometimes sharply) from your own organisation's. They are accountable to their contract: it defines what they must deliver and what they'll be paid, and within it they have reasonable latitude to protect their margins and manage their risk. Your job, as the client-side coordinator, is to ensure they deliver what the contract requires. Their job is to deliver that while running a profitable engagement. These are not the same job, and occasionally they pull in different directions.

## The posture: professional partnership

New coordinators err at one of two poles. Some treat vendors as **employees** — expecting them to absorb scope and extend effort beyond the contract. Others treat them as **adversaries** — assuming every push-back is an attempt to extract money. Both are wrong. The right posture is professional partnership: the vendor is a separate organisation with legitimate interests that sometimes align with yours and sometimes don't, and the relationship is most productive when both parties are honest about that. A reframing one vendor PM once offered: *"Most of what looks like me pushing back on you is me trying to deliver what I committed to within the constraints my firm has given me. If we align on what we actually need from each other, we can both do our jobs better."*

## The three phases

A vendor engagement has three phases. **Selection and contracting** is usually led by procurement; if you're involved, know that the contract's shape sets the working relationship — a tight, detailed contract makes every request transactional; a loose, principle-based one makes both sides extend on spirit. **Execution** is where you spend most of your time and where the relationship is built or damaged, in the small interactions. **Closeout** — formal acceptance, invoice reconciliation, resolution of disputes — is often underinvested; a clean closeout protects both parties and preserves the relationship for future work.

## Practices that make it work

- **Be precise in writing.** Ambiguity in client–vendor communication is expensive and breeds later scope arguments. When you ask for something, be specific about what, when, in what form. When they commit, get it in writing with enough specificity that there's no later reinterpretation.
- **Separate the contract from the relationship.** When a dispute arises, frame it as a contract question — *"let's look at what the SOW says"* — not a character question. Both sides can then disagree on substance without making it personal.
- **Respect their internal structure.** Don't casually bypass your primary contact to talk to someone else on their side; it undermines their operating model. If you need to engage above your counterpart, do it through them or with their knowledge.
- **Pay on time.** You're often the conduit for invoices. A vendor paid on time has goodwill available; a vendor paid late without explanation withdraws effort beyond the strict contract.
- **Hold them to the contract without being punitive.** When they miss, name it specifically, ask for their view on what happened and how to prevent it, and agree corrective action together. Don't soften the miss; don't escalate it theatrically. Vendors respect clients who hold them accountable without hostility.
- **Give credit.** When their team does good work, say so. A short email to their PM praising a specific person costs nothing and creates enormous goodwill.

## When vendors fail

If a vendor underperforms materially — a deliverable below spec, a commitment missed repeatedly — manage it without destroying the relationship unless that becomes necessary. The escalation path is staged: first **private** (a direct conversation between the client PM and vendor PM to name the issue, understand the cause, agree corrective action — many issues resolve here if surfaced early); then **formal** (a written escalation to the vendor's leadership, referencing contract terms, only after private resolution has been tried and failed); and only as a **last resort**, legal/breach proceedings, handled by the PM, legal, and procurement, not you. Your role throughout is to document carefully — the specific missed commitments, the communications, the attempts at resolution — so the record is clear if it does escalate.

## The move being tested

Given a vendor that has missed a critical-path commitment twice *and* submitted a change request for work that looks already in scope: the skilled move is to take both to your counterpart directly, firmly but as a partner — name the miss specifically and agree a heads-up protocol (not a breach threat), frame the change request as a SOW-clause reconciliation (not an accusation of bad faith), keep it counterpart-to-counterpart rather than bypassing to their executives, log the specifics for the record, and credit the good work where it's due. Neither the adversary's nuclear escalation nor the pushover's friendly capitulation. Firm, precise, proportionate, on the record.
