# Using AI as a Coordinator Without Losing Your Edge

AI tools are double-edged in this role. They are genuinely useful — a real productivity multiplier. They can also, used without care, substitute for the very practice that builds the professional you're becoming. A junior coordinator who has every email, report, and set of minutes drafted by AI and lightly edited may feel twice as productive — and may also, six months on, have a craft no stronger than when he started, because he was curating output rather than producing it. This lesson is about using AI well.

## What AI is genuinely good at

For the coordinator's work, current AI tools are substantively useful for the **mechanics** — the production of text at volume:

- **Drafting** first cuts of emails, status narratives, minutes from rough notes, change-request descriptions — saving you from the blank page, where procrastination lives.
- **Summarising** long documents, transcripts, or threads.
- **Rephrasing** a draft you've written into different tones, lengths, or registers.
- **Structured extraction** — pulling actions from a transcript, risks from a requirements doc.
- **Explanation** of a concept or method you're unfamiliar with.
- **Ideation** — generating options to prime your thinking.

Used for these, AI lets you get more done and reinvest the time saved into higher-judgment work.

## What AI is not good at

The same tools are consistently weak at the things that make you a professional:

- **Context specific to your project** — the politics of your steering committee, the history of your vendor relationship. Output without this is generic and sometimes wrong in ways obvious to anyone who knows the situation.
- **Judgment under ambiguity** — should you escalate? push back? what's the right RAG rating this week? These depend on relationships, history, and reading the situation.
- **Reading the room** — AI cannot see the unspoken no, the pre-alignment, the deferred decision.
- **Telling the truth when the truth is uncomfortable.** AI cheerfully produces green status reports and deferential emails because it optimises for coherence, not for the honest signal your situation requires. It will also state fabricated facts — numbers, dates — with total confidence.

## The rule: mechanics, not meaning

**Mechanics** is the production of text at volume — drafting, formatting, rephrasing. The substance is yours; the production of the words can be assisted. **Meaning** is what you're actually saying — the judgment call, the framing, the specific signal. That's yours. If you let AI make meaning for you, you're outsourcing the part of the job that makes you a professional.

The test: could you defend what you sent? If a senior person asked "why did you frame it that way?", could you give a reasoned answer rooted in your own thinking? If the answer is "the AI suggested it," the AI was doing work that should have been yours. A RAG rating is meaning — "the AI said green" is never a defence.

## Specific practices

- **Write your own first drafts while you're learning.** For the first year or two, write your own emails, reports, minutes, and risk descriptions; use AI as a tutor (ask it to critique your draft), not a replacement. The skill compounds.
- **Accelerate, don't substitute.** Once you can produce a reasonable first draft, use AI to get to a second draft faster — provide your draft, ask for tightening or register adjustment. You remain the author.
- **Never paste AI output directly into a sensitive communication.** A sponsor status, steering-committee minutes, an escalation — these go through your own edit even when the draft was AI-assisted. The edit makes it yours.
- **Audit AI output for accuracy** — especially specifics: dates, names, numbers, references. AI confidently produces wrong facts; check everything consequential before it goes out.
- **Build your own voice, not the AI's.** AI has a recognisable register — slightly generic, slightly padded, reliably competent. If all your output lands there, people notice your writing reads like everyone else's. Keep the edits that make it yours.
- **Disclose when appropriate**, per your organisation's policies; some contexts (research summaries from AI-assisted synthesis) warrant a note on method.

## The strategic shift

If AI can draft status reports and extract actions, the coordinator's distinctive value moves *up the stack*: the production of artifacts becomes less differentiating; the judgment about what to produce, what it should say, and how it should land becomes more. You'll spend less time typing and more time thinking — you'll supervise the typing, but the judgment is where the career lives. This is exactly why the judgment chapters — escalation, push-back, chasing, reading the room — matter more than ever: they're the skills that remain yours even when much of the production is assisted.

## The move being tested

Given an AI-drafted Steering Committee status that rates a failed-gate workstream GREEN, cites a fabricated metric, pads its risks, and reads in generic AI register: the skilled move is to hold it from the SC and fix the substance — name the RAG rating as a meaning/judgment call the coordinator owns (GREEN is wrong), catch the fabricated number and the dishonest framing, insist the sponsor-facing report carry the coordinator's own edit and accountability — *and* coach the junior developmentally: AI is genuinely useful for mechanics, so keep using it, but draft-your-own-first-then-assist to build the craft, and keep your own voice. Neither the rubber-stamp that ships a fabricated GREEN to the board, nor the contemptuous blanket ban that throws out a genuinely useful tool.
