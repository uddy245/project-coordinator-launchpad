# When to Push Back: Saying No Up

## The instruction I should have questioned

A PM I admired once told me, with urgency, to send a deliverable to a client by end of day on a Thursday. I knew — because I had been tracking it — that it was not ready. A supporting analysis from another team had not arrived; the numbers in the draft were provisional. I said, tentatively, "Are you sure? The analysis isn't in yet." She said yes; the client wanted it, and we would send an update if needed. I sent it at 4:45 p.m.

The client used the numbers in a board presentation the next morning. They were meaningfully wrong. The corrected analysis, when it arrived on Friday, produced a different conclusion. The client was publicly embarrassed. Our firm had to issue a formal correction. The PM was in genuine trouble for a week.

I was, in the strictest sense, blameless. I had flagged the concern. I had been overruled. I had followed instruction. **I was also part of the failure.** I had flagged at the lowest possible intensity — *are you sure?* — and accepted the overrule without pressing. A stronger push — "I'd rather not send this; the numbers could be materially wrong when the analysis lands" — would have been awkward in the moment and would have saved everyone a great deal of trouble.

Pushing back on a senior person is not comfortable. It is, in certain situations, the most valuable thing a coordinator can do.

## Why coordinators fail to push back

The default for most junior coordinators is to accept what senior people say. The reasons are understandable: **hierarchy** (they outrank you, so it feels presumptuous); **information asymmetry** (maybe they know something you don't); **relational cost** (a bad push damages relationships); **speed** (accepting is faster); and **self-doubt** (what if you're wrong and just being difficult?).

Each has some merit. None justifies silence when you have a real concern about something consequential. **The failure mode is to let these reasons compound until the default becomes "do not push back, ever"** — which is a worse failure than the occasional wrong push.

## The distinction that matters

Pushing back is not the same as disagreeing. You will disagree often; you do not need to push back every time. **Pushing back is warranted when four conditions are present:**

1. **The stakes are material.** A small formatting preference isn't worth a push. A decision that could embarrass the client is.
2. **You have relevant information the senior person may not.** You've seen the data, tracked the timeline, know the detail. Your view is informed, not just a preference.
3. **The decision is reversible only at higher cost if wrong.** The harder it is to reverse, the more a push is warranted.
4. **The senior person is deciding under time pressure or incomplete information.** Often the reason a PM is about to make a wrong call is that they're rushed or haven't seen what you've seen.

**When all four are present, push. When only one or two are, you probably shouldn't.**

## How to push back well

The move that works:

- **State your concern specifically and briefly.** Not "I have some concerns" — rather, "The supporting analysis isn't in yet; the numbers could shift materially when it lands."
- **Make the consequence concrete.** "If the client uses these figures and the analysis changes them, we'll have to issue a correction."
- **Offer an alternative.** "Could we send a short note saying the full analysis arrives Monday, and defer the deliverable until then?"
- **Accept the decision if overruled.** If the PM considers your concern and still says send, you send. You do not keep relitigating. You have done your job.

The posture is respectful but clear. You are not accusing anyone of wrongdoing; you are offering information and a recommendation. **Reasonable senior people appreciate this.**

One script that has served me: *"Before I do this, I want to flag something — [concern]. My recommendation would be [alternative]. But if you've thought about this and still want me to proceed, I'll proceed."* It states the concern, offers an alternative, and explicitly accepts the senior person's authority to overrule. It cannot reasonably be read as insubordination.

## Written versus verbal

Pushback is often better verbal — softer, more flexible, less paper trail for senior embarrassment. A written push becomes a record, which changes its social weight.

**The rule: for everyday pushes, talk. For consequential pushes where you want a record — because the stakes are high and you may need to refer back to your flag — write,** respectfully. The Thursday deliverable was a case where I'd have wanted the concern in writing: a short, dated Slack — "Want to flag — the supporting analysis isn't back; these numbers could shift. Still ok to send?" — would have put the PM's reply on record and protected both of us.

## Pushing back on scope

A specific variant. Stakeholders sometimes ask coordinators to do things outside the coordinator's role or the project's scope. The urge is to say yes, because no feels unhelpful. **The habit to build is to say yes carefully:**

- "Happy to help — can I check with the PM first to make sure this fits?"
- "I can put together a first draft; the PM will want to review before it goes out."
- "That sounds useful — is it in the current scope, or would we need a CR?"

These are not refusals. They are professional reframings that route the request through the right process, protect you from unauthorized work, and slow things down slightly — useful when the request shouldn't have been made.

## Pushing back on yourself

The hardest pushback is sometimes internal. You are about to do something because it is easier, or because you don't want the conflict, or because you are tired. A small voice says this is not right. **The discipline is to listen.** The Thursday deliverable was a case of not pushing back on myself — I knew the move was wrong and let it go anyway. Over years I have learned to trust that voice, because it is almost always right. **Professionals who cannot push back internally cannot push back externally.**

## Tomorrow, specifically

1. **Recall the last instruction you accepted that, honestly, you'd have pushed back on.** Ask what stopped you.
2. **If the stakes were material, treat the next one like it as a chance to do it differently.**
3. **At your next PM one-on-one, bring one small push** — not high-stakes, just practising the move: "I'd do X differently, here's why — what do you think?" The low-stakes reps make the high-stakes version feel natural.
