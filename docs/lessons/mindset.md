# The Mindset That Makes or Breaks You

## The story behind this chapter

Picture the third project of your coordinator career. You are nine months in. Your project manager — Helen — goes on annual leave for ten working days. She has briefed the sponsor. She has cover for emergencies. She leaves you with a list of things to do, a list of things to watch, and an explicit instruction: *"If anything comes up that worries you, call me. Otherwise run it."*

You do the list. You do not run it.

In her absence the vendor slips a deliverable by four days. You note it. You do not call. A senior stakeholder asks a real regulatory question. You don't know the answer. You don't reply. He follows up three days later, annoyed. The downstream workstream comes in Monday with no input and quietly loses a week — you notice on Tuesday, add it to your "for Helen" list.

She comes back. By Friday she has called the vendor, answered the stakeholder, rebuilt the schedule, and briefed the sponsor. None of these took more than a few hours. She is calm about it. The following Friday she invites you for coffee:

> "I want to talk to you about what you think your job is, because I think we have a gap in understanding, and I'd like to close it before it becomes a problem."

That coffee is the most important conversation of the early career. It introduces the seven mindsets below — the ones that distinguish a coordinator who grows from one who hits a ceiling and stays.

## Shift 01 — Task mindset vs ownership mindset

The **task mindset** says: *I am here to execute tasks. When someone tells me what to do, I do it well. When nobody tells me, I do the tasks I know are mine.*

The **ownership mindset** says: *I am here to keep this project moving. My tasks are the mechanism through which I do that — but the tasks are not the job. The job is the project.*

These are not distinguished by effort. They are distinguished by framing.

A task coordinator opens their to-do list and asks *what do I need to do today?* An ownership coordinator opens the project and asks *what does this project need from me today?* The task list is a subset of what the project needs. The ownership coordinator sees the part that isn't on the list — and either does it, or finds who should.

**The 6-week practice:** every morning, ask yourself in writing — *what does this project need from me today that isn't on my task list?* Answer it honestly. Act on one of the answers. Within a month it stops being a question you have to ask deliberately. It becomes how you see.

## Shift 02 — Composure: be the eye of the storm

Projects are stressful. The PM will be short with you because three things are on fire. The sponsor will send a three-line email that reads as furious when it is actually just brief. A stakeholder will bring you a problem at four o'clock on a Friday and expect you to care about it as much as they do.

The mindset to cultivate: *the emotional weather around me is not my emotional weather. I can observe it, respond to it, and let it pass through me without becoming it.*

Three composure habits worth practising:

- **The 10-minute rule.** When an email arrives that triggers a strong reaction, do not reply for ten minutes. Read it. Let the reaction pass. Reply only to what was actually asked. Use neutral, specific language.
- **Let them finish.** When someone vents at you, don't interrupt. Don't rush to solve. Acknowledge briefly: *"That sounds frustrating."* Move toward action: *"Let me come back to you by EOD."* Nine times out of ten, the person feels heard, and is then ready to be helped.
- **When it has gone wrong, don't panic.** Be the person who says, calmly: *"Here's what we know. Here's what we don't. Here's what I'm doing next. I'll come back to you by [time]."* That sentence — delivered well — is worth months of training.

Composure is not stoicism. It does not mean not feeling things. It means not broadcasting what you feel in real time, in front of the team.

## Shift 03 — The refusal to pretend

Junior coordinators feel pressure to appear competent. There is a strong pull to produce an answer — any answer — rather than admit ignorance. This pull is a career trap. Each fabricated answer is a small withdrawal from your credibility account, and credibility is the currency of the role.

> *I would rather say "I don't know, let me find out" ten times a day for a year than be caught out once on something I claimed to know and didn't.*

When you don't know, say so — crisply, without apology, with an action attached:

- *"I don't know — let me check and come back to you by end of day."*
- *"I'm not sure, but I can find out. Is tomorrow morning OK, or do you need it sooner?"*
- *"I don't have the detail on that. Let me route it to the technical lead and come back with an answer."*

These sentences feel like weakness the first few times. They are the opposite. The people who hesitate to admit ignorance are the ones whose egos are fragile. The people who say "I don't know" easily are the ones whose credibility is robust enough to absorb the admission.

## Shift 04 — The dignity of the small thing

You will be asked to do things that feel trivial. Book a room. Update a folder. Send a meeting invite to twelve people with the right attachments.

Junior coordinators — especially ambitious ones — sometimes treat these as a tax on their real potential. They do them, but sloppily. **This is a mistake.**

The small tasks are where your reliability is built. The PM forms a *single* impression of you, not separate impressions for "substantive work" and "admin." That impression is built more from the high-frequency small tasks than from the rare big ones.

- At **90% reliability**: a typo here, a wrong date there, a meeting invite without the attachment. Impression: *needs supervision.*
- At **99% reliability**: consistent, clean, accurate, across hundreds of small tasks. Impression: *I can trust their work without checking.*

The gap between those two impressions is the difference between a coordinator who gets assigned the interesting work and one who doesn't.

> Every task is worth doing at 100% — not because it matters intrinsically, but because doing it at 90% is a habit you cannot afford to form.

A related thought: do not be embarrassed to be a coordinator. Do not apologise for the title. Do not talk about the role as a rung you are trying to climb off as fast as possible. The coordinators who hold the role with pride coordinate with pride. The ones who are ashamed coordinate shamefully — and the work reflects that too.

## Shift 05 — The long view

You are not building a week. You are building a career. Every document, every meeting, every email is a data point in a reputation that is forming with or without your attention.

Your project manager today is someone who will, in five or ten years, be a senior figure in the industry — and will either recommend you or not. The stakeholder you are mildly annoyed with today is someone who will, a decade from now, be in a position to hire you or not. The colleague junior to you now will one day decide whether to bring you in on a project.

The long view changes specific things:

- **Battles to fight.** Fewer. Picked carefully. Being the person who fights everything is its own reputation — and not the one you want.
- **Corners not to cut.** Someone reads even the documents you think nobody reads. Cutting corners on those is a habit that leaks into the documents that matter.
- **Slights to let go.** The curt PM, the rude stakeholder, the colleague who took credit. The long view processes these as *information about the situation* — not personal injuries. Carrying grievances is expensive and the interest rate is high.

The long view is also kind. *This week is not the measure of me.* You will have bad weeks. The long view says: one bad month out of sixty is four percent of your career so far. Fix what you can, learn what you can, and keep going.

## Shift 06 — The failure mindset

You will make mistakes. How you handle them shapes your career more than whether you make them.

**Four wrong responses:**

1. **Hide it.** Hope nobody notices. When the mistake surfaces — and they almost always do — you have now lied about a mistake. A different and worse thing.
2. **Excuse it.** Acknowledge it but spend most of the acknowledgment explaining why it was understandable. Signals you care more about your reputation than the fix.
3. **Catastrophize.** Apologise profusely. Bring it up three times this week. Treat a small error as a major event. Forces everyone to manage your feelings about it.
4. **Spiral.** Carry it internally. Replay it. Lose sleep. Start next week from a deficit. The most destructive — because it compounds.

**The right way is clean and boring:**

> **Own it.** *"I made an error in this morning's status report — the finance figures were outdated. I'm fixing it now and will resend by 11."* No drama. No defensiveness. Just the fact.
>
> **Fix it.** Resend the document. Follow up with whoever was affected. Close the loop.
>
> **Extract the lesson.** One sentence, written down only for you. *"In future, verify financial figures against the budget tracker before the status report goes out."*
>
> **Move on.** This is the hardest step. Don't keep apologising. Don't let the mistake become the reference point for every subsequent interaction. You have acknowledged it, fixed it, learned from it. Go back to doing the job well.

## Shift 07 — Service without servility

Coordination is a service role. It is not a servant role. The distinction matters and most coordinators do not understand it clearly for the first couple of years.

**Service** makes other people's work easier. You anticipate needs. You remove friction. You are useful in ways the people around you can count on.

**Servility** is different. You absorb rudeness without comment. You stay late because someone expressed mild disappointment, not because the work required it. You take on work that belongs to someone else's role because you don't know how to say no.

The mindset: *I am here to help — and helpful is the core of my job. But I am a professional. Professionals have roles and boundaries.*

When someone asks you to take on something that isn't yours, do not say no rudely. Also do not say yes silently. Say something like:

> *"Happy to help. Can you tell me what to deprioritise this week to make room?"*

Two signals at once: you are willing, *and* your time is a finite resource the asker is part of allocating. Nine times out of ten they will reconsider, route the task to someone else, or make a real priority call. The one time out of ten they say *everything — this matters more*, you have a real answer and can plan accordingly.

## A mindset is a practice

I have written this chapter as if it were a matter of making seven shifts. In a sense it is. In a more important sense it is not — because each of these shifts is not something you do once. They are practices. The things you work on every week for your entire career.

You will not become an ownership coordinator, a composed coordinator, a coordinator who refuses to pretend, all at once. You will become those things slowly, through practice, with lapses, over years.

But the direction is clear. And the earlier you start, the more the compounding works in your favour.

## Tomorrow

1. The next time you sit down at your desk and open your to-do list, ask yourself — *before* you start — *what does this project need from me today that isn't on this list?* Write it down. Do one of them.
2. The next time someone asks you a question you don't know the answer to, say *"I don't know, let me find out"* without hedging. Note the feeling. Then actually find out by EOD.
3. Open your sent folder and read your last five emails. Count the hedges. Edit the next three you send to be more direct without being rude.
4. Think of one interaction this week where you were annoyed by someone's behaviour. Ask: *is this really about me, or is something else going on with them?* If the honest answer is "something else," let it go.
5. The next time you make a mistake: own it in one sentence, fix it, learn from it, move on. Don't apologise more than once. See if you can take it from noticing to closed in under twenty minutes.
