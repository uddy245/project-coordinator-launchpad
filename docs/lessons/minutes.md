# Minutes, Actions, and Decisions — The Written Record

## The minutes that settled a dispute eighteen months later

A project coordinated in 2014 came back through a phone call in late 2015 — a legal colleague at the firm calling about a vendor dispute. The vendor was claiming they had delivered their scope in full and were entitled to their final payment. The former client was claiming that two specific items had never been delivered and were part of the original agreement. The dispute was headed for a formal claim.

The legal colleague asked whether anyone remembered what had been agreed in the kickoff, and specifically whether the two items had been discussed. The coordinator did not remember — it had been a year and a half, two projects intervening. But the minutes had been written, and they had been written carefully.

The kickoff minutes, pulled from the archive, contained a clear statement:

> *"Vendor to deliver A, B, and C. Items D and E confirmed as out of scope per procurement, to be considered under separate engagement if required."*

The minutes had been sent to all attendees, including the vendor's lead, at the time. No one had objected. **The minutes, accepted by silence, were the record.**

The dispute was resolved within a week. The vendor accepted that the items had been out of scope. The claim was withdrawn. The legal colleague's note afterward called the case "closed by good paperwork."

**Minutes are not bureaucratic afterthoughts.** They are the durable record of what a project decided, agreed, and committed to. They are referenced in disputes, audits, closure reviews, and scope arguments months or years later. Done well, they protect the project and the people in it. Done badly, they create confusion and dispute.

## The three things minutes must capture

Minutes are not transcripts. Transcripts are almost never useful. Minutes are structured records with three components.

**Decisions.** Things the meeting settled. A decision entry says what was decided, by whom (if attribution is relevant), and when. If a decision was unanimous, say so. If there was dissent, note the dissent with the name of the dissenting party. If a decision was deferred, say so explicitly with the date of the next conversation.

**Actions.** Things someone committed to do. An action entry says what will be done, who owns it, and by when. **Every action has exactly one owner.** *"The team will investigate"* is not an action. *"Priya will investigate X and report back by May 15"* is.

**Key context.** Only when necessary. Sometimes a decision or action requires a sentence of context to be intelligible later. *"Decision: proceed with Option B (noting Option A was rejected due to licensing cost)."* The context is minimal and serves only to make the record interpretable six months from now.

Everything else — discussion, side remarks, compliments, complaints, rehashing of known context — does not belong in minutes. **Leaving them out is not an omission; it is a professional judgment about what the record is for.**

## The format that scales

A consistent format makes minutes easier to write and easier to read.

**Top of the document:** meeting name, date, attendees (present and absent), agenda items.

**Three sections, each with a clear heading:**

- **Decisions.** A list, each item one to three sentences. Clear, attributable, dated.
- **Actions.** A table (or formatted list) with columns: action, owner, due date, status. Status starts as "open" on all new actions.
- **Notes.** Only when necessary. Brief context on specific items for future readers.

No narrative prose. No *"The team discussed…"* entries. If something is worth recording, it is a decision, an action, or structured context.

## The writing discipline

A few specific disciplines that separate competent minutes from mediocre ones.

**Write in real time, not after.** Notes you take during the meeting are always better than notes you reconstruct after. Reconstruction introduces errors — you misremember who said what, you conflate two discussions, you miss details. If you take structured notes live (decisions and actions in their own columns from the start), the minutes almost write themselves.

**Send within 24 hours, ideally same-day.** Minutes that arrive three days later are minutes that nobody reads because the meeting has faded from memory. Same-day is the gold standard; next-morning is acceptable; **more than 48 hours is a warning sign about your process**.

**Use consistent language for status.** Actions are *open, in progress, completed,* or *dropped*. Decisions are *decided, deferred,* or *reopened*. Consistency across meetings lets minutes be searched and tracked across months.

**Attribute carefully.** When you write *"the team agreed,"* you are saying the room was unanimous. When you write *"the sponsor approved,"* you are saying the sponsor exercised decision authority. These words have weight. Do not use them loosely. If three people in the room raised concerns, the room was not unanimous and the minutes should not say so — that creates the kind of buried-disagreement that surfaces unpleasantly later.

**Resolve ambiguity by asking before sending.** If your notes have an unresolved question — you were not sure what was decided, or who owned an action — resolve it with a quick Slack to the PM or the relevant party before the minutes go out. Minutes that go out with *"TBD"* entries signal incomplete meetings. They land worse than minutes that take another half-day to ship.

**Read once before sending.** Catch typos, wrong dates, ambiguous sentences. You will catch at least one of each per document. The ninety seconds of review is worth it.

## Tone and register

Minutes are formal documents. They use standard register — **no contractions, no humour, no editorialising, no emotion.** Even when the meeting was contentious, the minutes are spare and neutral. They record what happened without evaluating it.

This matters because minutes are read by people who were not in the room and who may have different sensitivities than the participants. They are also referenced by people months later, who have forgotten the context. **Neutral phrasing holds up. Evaluative phrasing does not.**

A specific example. A meeting in which a senior stakeholder argued forcefully for a position that was eventually rejected. In your notes, you might think of it as *"X was pushed back on heavily."* In the minutes, you write:

> *"X was discussed. After review of the alternatives, the committee decided to proceed with Y. Concerns raised by [name] regarding [issue] have been added to the risk register."*

Neutral. Factual. No one is embarrassed by the record. The decision is captured. The dissent is acknowledged but not editorialised. The follow-up exists.

## Following up on actions

Minutes are not the end of the job. The actions in them need to be tracked to closure.

Your operational discipline is to maintain an **action tracker** — a running list of all open actions across all meetings, with owners and due dates. This is separate from (and more useful than) minutes themselves, because it is a living operational view rather than a static record.

Before each meeting, review the tracker. Which actions from the previous meeting are complete? Which are overdue? Which are approaching due date? **The first agenda item of most standing meetings should be a thirty-second review of last meeting's actions.** *"Four actions open from last week, three on track, one overdue — Priya, can you update on the vendor analysis?"*

This discipline does several things:

- It makes actions real rather than ceremonial.
- It gives slippage a place to be visible.
- It reinforces the expectation that commitments matter.
- It saves you from the passive-aggressive dynamic where actions are assigned, forgotten, and rediscovered three meetings later.

**Chase overdue actions privately rather than in the next meeting, when possible.** A quick *"Priya — just checking on the vendor analysis, due yesterday; any obstacles?"* is usually more productive than a public reminder. Public reminders are for chronic cases or when the action genuinely needs to be escalated.

## Minutes for different meeting types

The format scales, but the weight differs.

| Meeting type | Formality | Reviewed by PM? |
|---|---|---|
| **Steering committee** | High — referenced formally, written with extra care | Yes, before sending |
| **Sponsor-level** | High — precise language, distribute with formality | Yes |
| **Change Control Board** | High — formal, referenced in closure and audits | Yes, especially early |
| **Weekly team** | Lighter — short summary, decisions, actions | Spot-check once trust is built |
| **Working session** | Adapt format to purpose — workshop output, design outcome, retro summary | Depends |
| **One-on-ones / informal** | No formal minutes; if a commitment emerges, confirm in writing afterward | N/A |

For informal commitments: *"Following up on our chat — confirming that you'll review the revised plan by Thursday, and I'll incorporate your feedback before Friday's steering."* One sentence, written, sent. That is your record.

## When the minutes are wrong

Occasionally someone will challenge your minutes. *"I didn't say that." "We didn't agree to that." "That's not what was decided."*

The right posture is neutral and evidence-based. Not defensive; not apologetic; just factual.

> *"My notes from the meeting record this — can you tell me what you think should be corrected, and I'll compare against my notes and reach out to others if needed?"*

If your notes are wrong, correct them and reissue with a brief note. *"Please see updated minutes; the decision on X has been corrected to reflect the actual discussion — apologies for the confusion."* If your notes are right but someone remembers differently, check with another attendee. If the record is supported, hold it. If there is genuine disagreement, the PM or the meeting chair needs to rule.

**What you do not do is quietly revise minutes to match someone's later preference without a transparent correction note.** Minutes are legal-ish documents; silent revision is worse than initial error. If a correction is needed, it is made openly.

## The minutes that did not need a defence

A side-benefit of disciplined minutes is that the discipline itself ends arguments before they start. When the minutes are clean — decisions captured precisely, actions owned and dated, attribution accurate, attribution carefully scoped — there is less to dispute. People do not push back on what was decided because the decision is recorded in language they themselves would have used. They do not push back on actions because they own them and saw them go out. They do not push back on the record because the record is plainly factual.

Untidy minutes invite challenge. A line that reads *"the team agreed"* invites someone to say *"I did not agree."* A line that reads *"V. Bauer pushed back forcefully on the deferral"* invites her to say *"that's not how I would have characterised my contribution."* A line that reads *"Decision: deferred to phase 2 per the CCB's authority; concerns from V. Bauer regarding [issue] will be brought to steering for ratification"* invites neither response. **Precision is its own defence.**

This is why writing minutes well is not just about the document being right. It is about the document being uncontestable on the things that should not be contested, so that the energy of the project can go into the things that actually need argument — the trade-offs, the deferrals, the genuine disagreements about what to build. Minutes that are messy on the procedural facts steal attention from the substantive ones.

## Audit, don't rewrite

When you are asked to review someone else's minutes draft — a junior coordinator's first CCB, or a PM's note from a meeting you weren't in — your job is to **identify what needs to change and let the author make the changes**, not to produce a v2 yourself and send it. Producing a v2 minutes document on someone else's behalf, especially one where you fill in TBDs with your own best guesses, lands a different document in the audit trail than the one the meeting actually produced. **Inventing facts in minutes is a particularly bad failure mode**, because minutes are durable — wrong content in the record is hard to roll back. Audit. Recommend the specific changes. Let the author resolve the open questions with the room.
