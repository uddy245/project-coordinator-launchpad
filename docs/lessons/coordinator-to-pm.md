# The Path From Coordinator to PM (and Whether to Take It)

The wrong question is "how do I become a PM?" The right question is "do I actually want to be a PM, and if so, what about the role am I drawn to?" Too many coordinators step into PM roles because it's the next rung, only to discover they don't enjoy the parts of the job that were invisible to them — the accountability to the sponsor, the political navigation, the carrying of the project's emotional weight, the comfort with ambiguity they didn't need as coordinators. Some adapt. Some are miserable and leave within two years. A few go back to senior coordination and are happier. This lesson is about that transition — what it actually is, how to prepare, and how to decide whether to take it.

## What the PM role actually is

The transition is not a promotion; it's a **role change**. A coordinator *supports*; a PM *owns*. A coordinator produces artifacts under the PM's direction and approval. A PM is accountable for whether the project succeeds, measured against the sponsor's objectives — their name is on the charter, they answer to the steering committee, they defend the budget, they make the calls the coordinator was observing from the side.

Some skills transfer directly — written voice, organisational intelligence, operational discipline. Others have to be built new: sponsor management, strategic framing, accountability under pressure, the ability to hold a project together emotionally when things are hard. The mistake is assuming that doing the coordinator job well enough will automatically make you a good PM. It won't. Being a good coordinator is **necessary but not sufficient**; the step requires deliberately building the skills the coordinator role doesn't exercise.

## Should you take the step?

Not everyone should. A few honest questions:

- **Do I want the accountability?** PMs carry the sponsor conversation when it goes wrong, act when the team is burning out, make the hard call. Some thrive on this weight; others find it corrosive. Be honest about which you are.
- **Do I want the political exposure?** PMs are more visible and more often in rooms where the politics are live. If the rooms you've observed felt energising, you're probably suited; if draining, the PM role will feel more so.
- **Do I want less time on artifacts and more on people?** PMs spend more time on stakeholders, sponsors, team dynamics, and decisions than on producing documents. Some coordinators love artifact craft and miss it; others welcome the shift.
- **Am I willing to be wrong publicly?** PMs make visible calls that turn out wrong, and have to own them and continue. If that's unbearable, the role will be painful.

If most land as yes, the step is probably for you. If several land as no, senior-coordinator or specialist roles can be satisfying, respected, and lucrative without the costs. **Either choice is defensible** — coordinators are professionals in their own right, not apprentice PMs.

## Building the skills before you need them

If you do want it, build toward it deliberately while still a coordinator:

- **Observe your PM intentionally.** Every interaction is a small lesson — how they prep for a sponsor meeting, frame bad news, handle a difficult conversation. Ask how you'd have handled it.
- **Ask for stretch responsibilities.** In your one-on-ones, ask to take on pieces slightly beyond your role — drafting a sponsor briefing, running a small workshop, leading a vendor conversation. Each is practice for the full role.
- **Begin handling small things end-to-end.** Before the title changes, own something small with real accountability — a sub-workstream, a low-risk internal initiative. Carrying something across the line yourself is different from supporting someone else's project.
- **Find mentors.** Often the most useful mentor is someone a rung or two above you, outside your direct line. Ask for twenty minutes a quarter.
- **Read outside the discipline** — leadership, organisational behaviour, decision-making. The PM role is in large part managing humans through uncertainty.

## The first PM project

Expect to **miss being a coordinator** — the production work was satisfying in a way the PM role often isn't; resist the urge to reach down into the operational work, your job is now upstream. Expect **imposter syndrome** — the weight of accountability feels strange the first few times; it fades with reps. Expect **visible mistakes** — early PM mistakes are the most common and most formative; the goal isn't to avoid them but to handle them well. And your **old coordinator habits will carry you** — reliability, signal clarity, written voice are the foundation that frees attention for the new skills.

## The longer arc

You are not building a portfolio of projects. You are building a version of yourself — one who knows how to hold a room, write a sentence that lands, read a situation, tell the truth under pressure, keep a commitment, own a mistake, treat people with respect, and do hard work consistently over long horizons. The specific projects recede quickly. What persists is the professional you became while doing them. Some coordinators become PMs; some don't; the craft is the same, and it matters on its own regardless of where the career goes.

## The conversation being tested

Given a PM who's opened the door — "where do you want to go?" — the skilled move is not to chase the title, and not to abdicate the decision. It's to come to the development conversation having worked the honest questions against your own evidence (reaching a reasoned yes, no, or not-yet), to show you understand PM is a role change with new skills to build, to propose concrete stretch steps that build those skills (own something small end-to-end; draft a sponsor briefing; find a mentor), and to own the choice with clear eyes about its costs — framed as a thoughtful reflection plus a specific, reasonable ask, the kind a good PM will meet generously.
