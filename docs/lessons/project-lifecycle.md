# The Project Lifecycle and Where the Coordinator Fits

## A lifecycle is a decision structure, not a calendar

A common beginner mistake is to think of a project as one long block of work. In reality, projects move through five phases — initiation, planning, execution, monitoring and controlling, and closing — and each phase exists to produce the evidence required for the next decision. ISO 21502 makes the bigger point: projects can use predictive, incremental, iterative, adaptive, or hybrid approaches, but they all need structure, practices, and decision points that fit the work.

For a coordinator, this is liberating as much as it is demanding. You do not have to understand every technical detail on day one. You do have to understand what phase the project is in, what it is trying to produce now, who needs to make the next decision, and which artifacts keep the work visible. That is the difference between looking busy and being useful.

## What changes in each phase

**Initiation — confirming why the project exists.** This is where the project is justified, authorised, and framed. The Treasury Board of Canada defines the project charter as the document issued by the sponsor that formally authorises the project's existence. Your role here is not to approve anything — that belongs to the sponsor and the PM. Your role is to *gather the essentials*: confirm approval state, identify sponsors and stakeholders, record gates and milestones, find where the source documents live, note early assumptions, dependencies, and constraints.

**Planning — turning intent into a controlled approach.** PMI defines a milestone as a significant event, a schedule as linked activities with planned dates and resources, and a project management information system as the tools used to gather and disseminate outputs. As coordinator, you prepare meeting cadences, set up trackers, organise stakeholder lists and folders, draft naming conventions, prepare first-draft status templates. This is not clerical. It is information design for delivery.

**Execution — moving work, not just holding meetings.** This is where deliverables are built, configured, tested, procured, or implemented. An action item, by definition, is follow-on work — usually arising from a meeting — that must have a responsible person and a due date. You become the team's continuity mechanism: agendas, captured actions, owners and due dates, visible issues, vendor coordination.

**Monitoring & controlling — keeping reality visible.** ISO 21502 defines control as comparing actual to planned performance, analysing variance, and taking corrective action. This is the phase where your judgment becomes visible. A strong coordinator does not just copy data into a status report. They help clarify whether the team is dealing with a risk, an issue, a decision, or an action — and what should escalate, what is normal follow-up, and what belongs in change.

**Closing — finishing well to protect value.** Closing is not ceremonial. PMI's guidance warns that incomplete close-out can expose organisations to loss, leave benefits unrealised, and weaken handover to operations. Your work: confirm the final action list is burned down or transferred, support sign-off logistics, organise closure documents, archive controlled versions, capture lessons, hand over to operations cleanly.

## Phase gates: continue, modify, or stop

PMI defines a phase gate as a review at the end of a phase where a decision is made to continue, continue with modification, or stop. The Treasury Board's gating guide stresses that gating plans should document the considerations and evidence needed for each decision. Phases exist *partly to produce that evidence*.

Your job at the gate is not to make the decision. It is to know which gate is coming, what evidence the team needs to clear it, and where that evidence lives.

## Coordination is not authority

One of the easiest ways to lose credibility is to confuse coordination with authority.

| Question | Owner |
|---|---|
| Approve the project, fund it, change direction | Sponsor / governance body |
| Day-to-day trade-offs and integration | Project manager |
| Product value and backlog ordering (in Scrum) | Product Owner |
| Scrum effectiveness, removing impediments | Scrum Master |
| Logs, follow-up, status packaging, repository discipline, meeting support | Project Coordinator |

The point isn't rigid hierarchy. It's clarity. Scrum explicitly states that the Scrum Team has one Product Owner, one Scrum Master, and Developers — with no sub-teams or hierarchies. A coordinator can absolutely support scheduling, documentation, stakeholder readiness, and cross-team follow-up — but should not silently absorb accountabilities that belong to others.

## The phase checklist

Run these six questions at every phase boundary or major handoff:

1. What phase are we in, and what is the next gate, milestone, or review?
2. What evidence does the team need for that decision?
3. Which documents are authoritative — and where is the source of truth?
4. Which actions, issues, risks, and changes need updating now?
5. Who needs an update, approval, or escalation before the next milestone?
6. What must be handed forward so nothing gets lost?

## Hybrid delivery and digital discipline

PMI's 2024 future-of-work research reports that project teams perform equally well across predictive, hybrid, and agile approaches, and across onsite, hybrid, and remote arrangements. Your job is not to defend a method label. Your job is to make information, timing, accountability, and follow-through visible — in whatever method your organisation actually uses.

For projects, that translates into:

- One source of truth — not multiple trackers
- Clear version control on every controlled document
- Disciplined folder structures and naming conventions
- Written follow-up that people can find weeks later

## AI guardrails

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework says trustworthy AI must be valid, safe, secure, accountable, transparent, explainable, privacy-enhancing, and fair. The Government of Canada's generative-AI guidance adds operational rules: do not treat generated content as authoritative. Verify against trusted sources. Do not paste sensitive information into public tools. Do not rely on AI when factual accuracy is critical.

Practically: AI may help draft an agenda, summarise non-sensitive notes, or suggest wording for a status update. *You remain accountable for accuracy, privacy, and the final communication.*

## Five mistakes to avoid

1. **Treating phases as terminology.** If you don't know what phase the project is in, your updates will be irrelevant.
2. **Overstepping role boundaries.** Approving, prioritising, or promising things that belong to the PM, sponsor, Product Owner, or Scrum Master.
3. **Poor information hygiene.** Multiple trackers. Outdated files. Missing owners. No source of truth.
4. **Weak follow-up.** Notes with no due dates. Status updates with no implications. Issues without escalation logic.
5. **Uncritical AI use.** Pasting sensitive material into the wrong tool. Forwarding generated text without review.
