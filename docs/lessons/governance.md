# Governance, Roles, and the People You Answer To

## The steering committee that wasn't a steering committee

A procurement transformation for a provincial government agency. Twelve-month budget, four million dollars. Sponsor is a deputy minister. Steering committee of seven — three assistant deputies, a director from finance, the chief procurement officer, and a representative from the minister's political office.

The committee meets monthly. The deputy speaks last and briefly. The political representative is silent — the only word she says in six months is *thanks* at the end of her second meeting. The minutes describe decisions as *the Committee agreed.* The minutes are accurate in the sense that no one disagreed in the room. They are misleading in every other sense.

Real decisions are made before the meetings. The deputy has a thirty-minute briefing from his chief of staff. The political representative has a standing weekly call with the chief of staff. The CPO has his own monthly meeting with the deputy. The committee is a ratifying body. The actual decisions are made in pre-meetings and side channels, and the committee's role is to provide visible endorsement.

For the first three months, you write minutes as if the meetings are where decisions are made. You send follow-ups to *the committee*. You coordinate through the formal structure and get slow, cautious, generic responses.

Month four. An assistant deputy takes you aside after a meeting. *"You know how decisions really happen here, right?"* He spends twenty minutes walking you through the real structure — names and patterns. After that conversation, you change how you route work. Pre-briefings before SC. Heads-ups for political concerns. Working through finance director's office in advance instead of hoping committee resolves it.

Pace of decisions doubles the following month. **Nothing in the formal structure changes.**

> The structure on paper is a starting point, not a description. Your job is to learn the real structure — not enforce the stated one.

## What governance actually is

Most project governance is taught as a set of roles and documents — a sponsor, a steering committee, a PM, a PMO, a RACI, a charter, a set of gates. These are real things. But they are not governance.

**Governance is the system by which decisions about the project are made.**

- Who decides what
- Who gets consulted before decisions
- Who is informed afterward
- Who can escalate, and to whom
- Who can veto, and over what
- Who has budget authority, and up to what threshold
- Who is accountable if things go wrong, and accountable to whom

A governance structure attempts to answer these questions in a way the organisation understands and respects. When it works well, it speeds delivery by removing confusion about decision rights. When it works badly, it creates a theatre of approvals that doesn't map to actual authority.

The reason new coordinators misread governance is that they take the documents at face value. The documents are a hypothesis about how decisions work. Your observations are the data. When they conflict, trust the data.

## The sponsor

The sponsor is the most important person on your project.

A **good sponsor** is a senior leader who owns the business outcome, has authority to make decisions and unblock escalations, is engaged enough to provide direction but not so engaged that they micromanage, and has genuine political capital to spend on the project's behalf. When you have a good sponsor, your project has a reasonable chance of succeeding. When you don't, it almost certainly won't.

Your own relationship with the sponsor is almost always second-hand — primary relationship is with the PM. You appear, if at all, as the person who sends materials, captures their words in minutes, occasionally answers a question on the PM's behalf. **By the time you meet the sponsor, they will already have a view of you based on your written work.**

### Five sponsor failure modes

| Mode | What it looks like | Counter-move |
|---|---|---|
| **Absent** | Named on charter, attends no meetings | Work through the chief of staff or senior advisor. Don't complain. |
| **Weak** | Attends but defers; lacks political capital | Do more pre-decision work. Make their job: just say yes to a well-prepared recommendation. |
| **Over-engaged** | Micromanages decisions that should be the PM's | Structured engagement. Regular 1:1s. Briefing doc. Make them feel in control. |
| **Wrong-level** | Too junior for the political weight required | Often no good move. Flag as a project risk requiring senior consideration. |
| **Misaligned** | Personally invested in a different outcome | Candid early conversation: *"What does success look like to you personally?"* |

## The steering committee — four shapes

| Shape | Members | What it actually does |
|---|---|---|
| **Decision-making** | 3–5, real authority | Actually debates and decides. Minutes reflect real decisions. |
| **Ratifying** | 5–10 | Real decisions made elsewhere. Committee approves what's been agreed. Most common. |
| **Ceremonial** | 8–15, quarterly | Exists for visibility. Receives reports — does not decide. |
| **Political** | Mixed membership | Manages relationships between stakeholder groups as much as the project itself. |

Your coordination role differs by shape:

- **Decision-making** — substantive pre-reads, crisp recommendations, precise minutes (high-stakes documents).
- **Ratifying** — real work happens *before* the meeting. Identify who needs briefing. Anticipate questions. Prep the sponsor.
- **Ceremonial** — manage attention. Pre-read tells a coherent story in 10 pages or less.
- **Political** — heavy relationship work. Individual briefings. Address constituency concerns. Map who has side-channels with whom.

A note on **steering committee minutes**: these are not routine minutes. They are legal-ish documents that will be referenced in contract disputes, audit reviews, post-project reviews — sometimes years later. Be precise about decisions. Specific about actions. Careful about attribution. If a decision was unanimous, say so. If it was made with dissent, say so. If a discussion was inconclusive and deferred, say so — don't paper over with language suggesting agreement.

## The PMO

The PMO sits in an ambiguous relationship to individual projects.

**At its best**: standards, tools, templates, training, and oversight that make projects more likely to succeed. Maintains a portfolio view. Helps projects avoid pitfalls others have hit.

**At its worst**: a bureaucratic layer that adds reporting overhead without delivery value. Heavy templates on projects that don't need them. Reports for its own consumption. Gate-keeper.

Five practical moves:

1. **Know what the PMO actually needs.** Most have a standard cadence and artifact set. Produce them on time, in the right format, with the right level of detail. Submit clean work.
2. **Understand the individual people.** A PMO contact who feels respected is an ally. A PMO contact who feels dismissed is a bureaucratic obstacle in waiting.
3. **Use the PMO for help when it helps.** Templates, processes, advice — most PMO staff enjoy actually helping projects.
4. **Don't go around it.** PMOs notice. The second offence is much worse than the first.
5. **Don't become a PMO employee in disguise.** Your first allegiance is to your project.

The PM/PMO relationship in many organisations is mildly adversarial. Be reliable in both directions. Don't take sides.

## The project manager

Your most important relationship on a daily basis. You work for the PM in the functional sense, even when you report elsewhere on the org chart. Their assessment is the single biggest factor in your career progression for the duration of the project.

| Type | What you do |
|---|---|
| **Good** — a teacher | Be genuinely engaged. Ask questions. Volunteer for stretch work. Stay humble. |
| **Mediocre** — transactional | Be self-directed. Build your own learning loops through observation, reading, informal mentors. |
| **Bad** — damaging | Depends on severity. Cover, switch, or document. Don't try to manage them by manipulation. |

> Whatever the experience: leave every relationship as cleanly as you can. The industry is small. The PM who was mediocre when you were 24 will be senior at a different firm when you're 34, and they may be in a position to hire you — or not.

## The product owner (in agile and hybrid)

Owns the **WHAT** — the backlog, its priority, the definition of success. The PM owns the **HOW** — coordination, governance, risk, stakeholder management.

A good product owner has three qualities:

1. **Domain expertise** — deep enough to make good decisions about what to build.
2. **Real authority** — yes and no without escalating every priority question.
3. **Availability** — answers requirements questions in hours, not days.

The friction point: when backlog choices have implications for the project-level schedule the PM is accountable for. PO wants to add a feature → pushes delivery 3 weeks → PM has a deadline commitment to the sponsor. Whose call? In theory: PO + PM + sponsor. In practice: friction. **You may be the person who has to surface the trade-off cleanly so it can be decided rather than drift.**

## Workstream leads and SMEs

Workstream leads are your daily operational partners. Their quality varies enormously, and your relationships with them shape every project artifact.

- **Invest early.** Spend a few minutes with each lead in your first weeks. Learn what they care about, their pet peeves.
- **Ask for what you need clearly and early.** Don't send ambiguous requests two days before the status report.
- **Be genuinely helpful.** Reciprocity matters.
- **Flag bad inputs carefully.** When a workstream is showing green but the schedule shows a slip — go to the lead first, not the PM. *"Status is showing green but the schedule shows the integration deliverable slipping. Want to talk through how to represent it?"*

**SMEs** are senior individuals whose expertise the project needs intermittently. Their time is scarce. Manage by being respectful — precise requests, advance notice, clear deliverables — rather than treating them as always-on resources.

## The RACI

| Letter | Means | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **R** Responsible | Does the work | |
| **A** Accountable | Owns the outcome and signs off | **Exactly one per decision** |
| **C** Consulted | Input sought before | |
| **I** Informed | Told after | |

A good RACI is a useful clarification tool. The conversations in which it is built are often more valuable than the document, because they surface unspoken assumptions.

A bad RACI exists because a template demanded one. Too many people listed as Accountable. Vague entries. Never referenced again.

**Common failure**: multiple people as Accountable for important decisions. Wrong by definition — but common, because organisations are uncomfortable concentrating accountability. Quietly flag it: *"The RACI has three Accountable for budget sign-off. In practice, who does the PM actually go to?"* The answer is usually one person. Update the RACI to reflect reality.

## Reading the room

Four habits worth practising:

1. **Pay attention to pre-meetings.** Who briefs whom before the SC? You will hear about them through calendars, offhand references, and who arrives already aligned with whom.
2. **Notice whose questions get answered.** Some get substantive responses. Others get deflections. The first group has the sponsor's real attention.
3. **Watch who gets consulted outside formal processes.** When a decision comes up, whom does the PM actually call before deciding? That's the truer list of Consulted.
4. **Track whose assessments shape the sponsor's view.** They form opinions through a small group of trusted advisors. Some are on the SC. Some aren't.

None of this is conspiracy thinking. It is how organisations actually work. Formal structures are useful, but they are overlays on human networks.

## Five governance failure modes

1. **Accountability is diffuse.** When everything goes wrong, no one is accountable. Too many names listed as Accountable for too many things.
2. **Steering committee is a black hole.** Decisions go in, updates come out — nothing actually decided. Minutes full of *the Committee noted* — never *decided*.
3. **Sponsor is performative.** Attends meetings, never engages substantively. Interventions are generic.
4. **PMO has no teeth.** Asks for reports but cannot enforce. Requirements routinely missed, nothing happens.
5. **Governance bypasses are the norm.** Decisions routinely made outside the formal structure. RACI says one thing, everyone knows it's wrong.

When you see these patterns, **the first move is to notice them and form a private assessment. The second move is to share the assessment carefully with your PM** — so the PM can decide what to do about it. You don't try to fix broken governance. You understand it, work within it, and make sure your PM sees it clearly enough to advocate for changes where appropriate.

## Tomorrow

1. Take your project's governance document and compare it to what you've actually observed in the last month. Where does the stated structure match reality? Where doesn't it? Private exercise — train yourself to see the gap.
2. Identify the 3–5 people whose assessments actually shape the sponsor's view. Some on the committee. Some not. Pay attention to each over the next month.
3. Ask your PM in your next 1:1: *"How do decisions really get made on this project?"* Not in a critical tone. Just as a learning question.
4. Pick one workstream lead and invest 10 minutes this week in a non-transactional conversation. Not about a deliverable. Just a conversation.
5. The next time you produce minutes from a senior-stakeholder meeting, read them through the lens of *would a new reader, six months from now, understand what was decided and why?* Edit for clarity, precision, durability.
