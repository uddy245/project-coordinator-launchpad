# Running Meetings That People Don't Hate

## The standup that took forty-five minutes

Early career. A fifteen-minute daily standup that took forty-five minutes every day for three weeks. The coordinator counted. Twelve people attended. Ten reported in serial — yesterday, today, blockers — and most of their updates did not need to be heard by the other nine. A side-discussion would erupt about a specific issue; the well-meaning scrum master let it run. Someone would catch up late and ask for a recap. One day a senior stakeholder joined and the team spent ten minutes explaining context.

The arithmetic:

> Twelve people × forty-five minutes × fifteen working days = **one hundred and thirty-five person-hours.**
>
> More than three weeks of one person's work, for updates that could have been a Slack thread of ten minutes a day.

The PM, to her credit, eventually shut it down. She made standup attendance optional except for the core delivery team (six people), moved status questions to an async channel, imposed a hard fifteen-minute cap. The remaining meetings averaged twelve minutes. **Nobody missed the extra thirty.**

Meetings are the most expensive thing a project does. They spend people's time in large blocks and produce decisions, alignment, or coordination — or, if badly run, **confusion, resentment, and slightly worse versions of what would have happened without them.** As coordinator, you are often running or supporting the meetings of a project, and you can make them significantly better than most people's defaults.

## Know what the meeting is for

Every useful meeting has one of three purposes. You should know which one you are in.

| Type | Purpose | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **Decision** | A choice between options needs to be made | The decision itself. If no decision is made, the meeting failed. |
| **Alignment** | Information needs to reach multiple people in a way that allows questions | A group that shares a working model of something |
| **Working** | Several people need to do work together (requirements workshop, design session, retro) | The work itself — a draft, a design, an agreed list |

**Status meetings, the most common type on projects, are alignment meetings.** Their purpose is shared situational awareness, not decisions. When people try to use a status meeting to decide, either the meeting balloons or the decision is made poorly by a room that is not set up for decision-making.

Before you schedule any meeting, ask which of the three it is. **If you cannot answer, do not schedule it.**

## Agendas that actually work

An agenda is not a list of topics. A list of topics is a meeting that will wander. **An agenda is a sequence of outcomes, each with a time budget and an owner.**

Good agenda item:

> *"Decide on integration vendor (15 minutes) — Sylvia leads; options briefed in the pre-read; decision needed today so procurement can start by Friday."*

Bad agenda item:

> *"Integration vendor discussion."*

The first tells participants what they are being asked to do, how long it should take, and what the stakes are. The second tells them nothing.

**Pre-reads are part of the agenda.** Any meeting requiring context should have a pre-read sent at least 24 hours in advance (48 is better). If you find yourself spending the first ten minutes of every meeting explaining context, the pre-read is the fix. Pre-reads respect people's time; meetings that brief in real time do not.

The coordinator's specific job is often to draft the agenda, circulate it, confirm pre-reads exist, and adjust timing after the meeting based on what actually happened. **Agendas are living documents.** If your standing meeting's agenda has been unchanged for six months, the agenda is ceremonial.

## Facilitation, which matters more than the agenda

A good agenda run badly is still a bad meeting. A mediocre agenda run well can still produce value. **Facilitation — steering a conversation toward outcomes — is a real skill**, and you will be asked to do it whether or not the job description says so.

The core moves:

- **Start on time.** Waiting for latecomers teaches the room that the meeting starts late. Start on time with whoever is there. Latecomers learn.
- **State the goal explicitly in the first minute.** *"We need to leave this meeting with a decision on X. We have thirty minutes."* Orient everyone before you begin.
- **Keep the conversation on the topic.** When people drift, redirect. *"Helpful point — let's park that for the follow-up and come back to the decision."* Polite but firm.
- **Surface dissent.** When one person is clearly unconvinced and the room is pretending consensus, name it. *"Priya, it looks like you have a concern — can you say what it is?"* **Suppressed dissent comes out later; surfaced dissent gets resolved.**
- **Close with a summary.** In the last two minutes, recap what was decided, what actions were agreed, and who owns each action by when. Write them down visibly. If the meeting has ended without clear actions, it has not produced value, and you should say so rather than hide it.
- **Respect the time budget.** If a meeting was scheduled for thirty minutes, end at thirty minutes. If the conversation is not done, acknowledge that and schedule a follow-up. **Running over trains the room to treat meeting times as suggestions.**

You will not always be the facilitator. When the PM runs the meeting, you support — capturing actions, watching the clock, occasionally nudging the PM privately if the meeting is drifting. When you run the meeting yourself, the moves above are yours.

## The meetings on a typical project

A quick tour:

- **Project kickoff** — one-off alignment, 60–90 min. Your role: draft agenda, prepare materials, capture outcomes.
- **Weekly team standup** — short alignment for delivery team. 15 min strictly. Your role: capture blockers, follow up async.
- **Weekly status** — alignment for wider team. 30–60 min. Your role: agenda, timekeeper, minutes.
- **Workstream working sessions** — irregular working meetings within a workstream. Your role: sometimes attend, often help with scheduling and capture.
- **Steering committee** — monthly or less; alignment + decision. Your role: agenda, pre-read, minutes, decision capture.
- **Sponsor 1:1** — PM's meeting. Your role: minimal, but often prepare briefing materials.
- **Change control board** — decision meeting for CRs. Your role: prepare CR docs, capture decisions.
- **Retrospectives** — working meetings at end of phases or sprints. Your role: facilitate (smaller projects), capture outputs, track improvement actions.
- **Ad-hoc problem-solving** — working meetings when something is wrong. Your role: often the one convening, ensuring the right people attend, capturing outcomes.

Not every project needs every meeting. A good project has the meetings it needs and no more. **When you see meetings that no one can articulate a clear purpose for, that is the time to propose consolidation or elimination.**

## The meetings you should propose canceling

A specific skill. When you notice a standing meeting is no longer producing value — attendance drifting, discussions listless, actions rarely materialising — propose to the PM that it be restructured or canceled.

The way to propose this is *not* "this meeting is useless." The way to propose it is:

> *"I've noticed the last four weekly syncs have produced mostly status updates rather than decisions. Could we move status to async and replace the standing slot with a fortnightly working session?"*

**You are proposing a design change, not attacking the meeting.**

Canceling unnecessary meetings is one of the highest-impact things a coordinator can do. **Every canceled hour returns to the team as work time.** Your reputation benefits too — coordinators who add meetings look busy; coordinators who remove meetings look thoughtful.

## When you are the youngest person in the room

A real coordinator pain point. You are facilitating — or supporting facilitation — and the room contains people senior to you, sometimes by a decade or more. The chapter's facilitation moves (redirect drift, surface dissent, close with summary) are all moves that involve gently constraining what senior people do with their time. The fear that doing them will land badly is real, and it is the single most common reason coordinators do not do them.

A few notes on this.

**The moves are easier when they are framed as service to the room, not control of it.** *"Helpful point — let's park that for the follow-up"* lands very differently from *"can we get back on track."* The first treats the room as collaborators; the second treats the meeting as the coordinator's territory. Same intent, different framing.

**You do not have to do them all at once.** Start with the easiest: the opening goal-statement and the closing summary. Both are scripted-feeling moves that look like preparation, not assertion. Redirecting drift and surfacing dissent are harder; come to them as you build trust with the room.

**You can do the moves on behalf of the PM.** If the PM is running the meeting and the conversation drifts, a coordinator note ("we have ten minutes left and three items remaining") gives the PM the opening to redirect without you having to do it directly. **You are protecting the meeting's outcomes; the PM is wielding the authority.**

**Note what is hard and bring it to your 1:1.** *"I felt like the conversation drifted off the vendor decision and I did not feel I had the standing to call it back — how do you want me to handle that next time?"* is a conversation that builds real skill and gives the PM a chance to coach. Most PMs will appreciate it.

The room learns. The first time you do a closing summary with decisions, actions, owners, and due dates written visibly, someone will notice. The second time, people will start looking for it. By the fourth or fifth meeting, the closing summary becomes the meeting's natural shape, and you have not had to assert anything — you have just kept doing the move until the room adopted it.

## Audit, don't rewrite

When you are asked to review a meeting setup — your PM forwards an agenda and asks what you think — your job is to **surface what is broken and propose specific changes**, not to send out v2 invites yourself. The PM owns the meeting and owns the relationship with the room; your contribution is the careful read and the named recommendation. Producing your own v2 invite with a finished agenda and a pre-read template and offering to send it out yourself replaces the audit with the artefact — same anti-pattern as the WBS coordinator who rewrites the WBS solo or the schedule coordinator who produces a v2 schedule and sends it to the steering committee. **The doers (and the PM) need to own the change for it to land.** Audit. Recommend. Let the PM run the recommendation through the team.

## Tomorrow, specifically

Five moves.

1. **Look at your next week's calendar.** For each meeting, write down which of the three types it is — decision, alignment, or working. If any cannot be categorised, that is a meeting worth questioning.

2. **Review the agenda of the next meeting you will support.** Check that each item has an outcome, a time, and an owner. If it doesn't, that is a 10-minute fix worth making.

3. **For the next meeting you facilitate, practise the closing summary.** The last two minutes where you recap decisions and actions. Write them visibly. Most meetings end without one of these; yours can be the exception.

4. **Identify one standing meeting that is consuming more time than it is producing value.** Think about how you would propose changing it — splitting the decision out, moving status to async, trimming the invitee list. Bring it to your PM in your next 1:1 as a design proposal, not a critique.

5. **The next time someone tries to bolt a decision onto a status meeting**, notice. That is the chapter's central anti-pattern — alignment-meeting-being-used-to-decide. Whether you surface it or quietly let it pass tells you something about how you are reading the room.
