# When to Chase, and When to Let Go

Chasing — reminding someone of a commitment they haven't met, in the hope they'll now meet it — is a real part of the coordinator's job. Most coordinators do it reflexively, without thinking. The ones who do the job well decide *when* to chase, when to escalate, and when to let go. They do better work and spend less energy doing it. This lesson is about that judgment.

## The seventeen-nudge trap

It is easy to chase an action far past the point of usefulness. You nudge once, twice, four times; a habit forms; the nudging becomes ceremonial — an accumulated reflex rather than purposeful work. The tell is that you are chasing to *close the action on the tracker*, not because the deliverable still matters. New coordinators believe their job is to close actions, and that closing one means getting the thing delivered. It isn't. Your job is to keep the project's record honest and its live work moving. Sometimes that means getting the deliverable. Sometimes it means recognising the deliverable is dead and saying so.

## What chasing is for

A chase is useful when the commitment is still live and reasonable prompting can produce action. It is wasted when the commitment is dead and prompting will not revive it. The whole skill is distinguishing the two — and the signals are knowable.

## Signals the chase is still useful

- **The person is responsive.** They reply to nudges, even if the reply is "sorry, still on it." Responsiveness means the commitment is still alive in their mind.
- **The slippage has a cause that is resolving.** They were on leave and are back; a cross-team blocker has lifted. A temporary constraint is clearing.
- **The deliverable is still needed.** The downstream workstream still cares; the milestone still depends on it.
- **Gentle prompting produces movement.** After a nudge, something happens — a partial response, a revised estimate, a request for help. The loop is producing progress, even if slowly.

When these hold, chase. You are closing a feedback loop the person hasn't closed themselves.

## Signals you should stop

- **The person has stopped responding** — not to one nudge, but across several over time. A strong signal the commitment is dead in their mind, whatever they originally said.
- **Circumstances have changed.** They've moved roles; priorities shifted; the reason they committed no longer applies.
- **The deliverable is no longer needed.** The downstream work has moved on; the output would now be historical rather than useful.
- **Chasing produces no movement.** After repeated nudges, nothing changes. You are spending energy on a dead loop.

When these hold, chasing is wasted. Something else is needed.

## What to do instead of chase

When a chase has run its course, three paths are available — and choosing the right one for the situation is the heart of the skill.

- **Escalate.** If the deliverable is *still needed* and the person isn't producing it, the next rung of the escalation ladder may fit — their manager, or your PM for intervention. Reserve this for items that still matter.
- **Reclassify.** Close the action as dropped, note why, and convert the underlying need into a risk or issue: *"Risk: X input not forthcoming from [name]; mitigation: Y team to proceed with current information, accept lower confidence in output; owner: Y lead."* Honest, and it preserves the record.
- **Let it go.** If the deliverable is no longer needed, close the action and move on. Don't leave dead actions on the tracker; they accumulate into clutter that hides what's actually live.

The hardest of these is usually letting go. Closing an undelivered action can feel like admitting failure. It isn't — it's accurate record-keeping. The real failure is a tracker full of nominally-open items nobody expects to close.

## Proportion

Match the move to the item's actual importance. A soft, off-critical-path dependency whose downstream work has already moved on does not warrant a nuclear escalation to someone's manager and the sponsor — that spends senior attention and goodwill on a non-issue, and it misreports a non-problem as a crisis. Equally, a genuinely critical, still-needed deliverable warrants more than a polite weekly nudge. Proportion cuts both ways.

## The chase, written well

When you *do* send a chase, it's a small professional artifact:

- **Be specific.** "Just checking on the vendor summary for next week's SC pack — do you have a view on timing?" Not "following up."
- **Be polite but not apologetic.** "Just checking" is fine. "So sorry to bother you, I know you're super busy, but…" is not. You are doing your job.
- **Make it easy to respond.** "Should I plan on Friday, or would Monday work better?" Yes/no and either/or questions get faster answers than open ones.
- **Offer help.** "Happy to grab fifteen minutes to walk through it if useful." Sometimes the block is something they'd be embarrassed to name; offering help makes it easy to ask.
- **Vary the channel occasionally.** Three silent emails? Try Slack, or a short in-person check-in. Varying channel can break inertia without feeling like pressure.

The tone throughout is patient professionalism. You are not annoyed — you are tracking. If you feel annoyed, wait before sending; the annoyance will leak through and hurt the relationship without helping the deliverable.

## The move that's being tested

Given an action you've nudged repeatedly with the owner now silent, on a soft dependency the downstream team has already worked around: the skilled move is to recognise the chase is dead, *let it go* — closing the action with an honest note and converting any genuine residue (an unconfirmed assumption the downstream work now rests on) into a named risk with a mitigation and an owner. Not a seventh ceremonial nudge. Not a nuclear escalation of a redundant item. Not leaving it open because closing feels like failure. Read the signals, then match the move to them.
